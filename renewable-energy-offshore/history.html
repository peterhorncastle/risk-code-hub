The Origins of Offshore Wind (1991–2001)

Offshore wind energy emerged from the onshore wind industry in Denmark — a country that had been developing wind power since the 1970s following the oil crisis. The motivation for going offshore was simple: stronger, more consistent winds and fewer planning constraints than on land.

1991

Vindeby — The World's First Offshore Wind Farm

Lolland, Denmark · 4.95MW · 11 × Bonus 450kW turbines · Water depth 2–4m

Built by ELSAM (later part of Ørsted) in the shallow waters off the island of Lolland, Vindeby was a proof-of-concept project. The turbines were mounted on gravity-based concrete foundations and connected to shore by submarine cable. The project generated data for over 25 years before decommissioning in 2017 — making it a landmark not only as the first offshore farm but also as the first to be decommissioned.

From an insurance perspective, Vindeby was too small to require specialist offshore energy coverage. Standard contractor's all risks and industrial property policies were used. The concept of a dedicated offshore wind insurance class did not yet exist.

1999–2001

First Commercial Scale Projects — Blyth & Middelgrunden

UK and Denmark · First projects warranting specialist marine insurance

Blyth Offshore Wind Farm (UK, 2000) was the first offshore wind installation in open sea, using monopile foundations in 6m water depth. Middelgrunden (Denmark, 2001) at 40MW was the first genuinely commercial-scale project. These projects began to attract attention from Lloyd's marine and energy underwriters as TIVs reached levels requiring specialist placement.

Early insurance for these projects was placed as marine all risks — treating the turbines and foundations much like offshore structures. There was no specific offshore wind product; underwriters adapted existing marine CAR and offshore energy wordings. The Lloyd's R1 and R3 risk codes had not yet been formally established.

The Pioneer Era — Round 1 & Horns Rev (2002–2007)

2002–2004

Horns Rev 1 — The Defining Early Loss Event

Denmark · 160MW · 80 × Vestas V80 turbines · 14–20km offshore

Horns Rev 1 was the first large-scale offshore wind farm in true open sea conditions, located 14–20km off the Jutland coast of Denmark. Commissioned in 2002, it immediately became the benchmark for the industry — and the source of its first major insurance loss.

Within two years of operation, serial gearbox and generator failures began affecting the Vestas V80 turbines. The problem was linked to the offshore marine environment — salt air ingress, humidity, and temperature cycling accelerating mechanical degradation faster than the onshore design anticipated. By 2004, all 80 transformer units were removed to shore for refurbishment — an engineering first, and an insurance claim that defined the serial defect problem for the entire industry.

⚠ Market Impact: The Serial Defect Problem Emerges

Horns Rev 1 established what would become the most contested coverage question in offshore wind insurance: when a design or manufacturing defect affects multiple turbines identically, does each failure constitute a separate loss event, or is the entire failure sequence a single occurrence? Underwriters began inserting serial defect clauses and series loss limitations from this point onwards.

2004–2007

UK Round 1 & Round 2 — London Market Develops Specialist Products

UK · Scroby Sands, Barrow, Burbo Bank, Kentish Flats, Beatrice Demo

The UK Crown Estate's Round 1 lease programme (awarded 2001, construction 2003–2006) produced the first cluster of UK offshore wind farms. The UK's strong regulatory framework, Crown Estate leasing system, and Renewables Obligation (RO) subsidy made it the world's most active offshore wind market through the 2000s — a position it maintained until the mid-2010s.

As these projects were financed by project finance structures (non-recourse debt from banks including HSBC, Barclays, and export credit agencies), lenders required robust insurance programmes. This created the market for dedicated Lloyd's offshore wind product lines — separate from the upstream energy market — with specialist underwriters developing R1 and R3 wordings.

Key Lloyd's syndicates developing offshore wind underwriting expertise during this period included Chaucer (now part of Channel Syndicate), Brit Insurance, and Ascot. Munich Re and Swiss Re were active as reinsurers and sometimes co-insurers on the larger projects.

Scale & Growth — The Offshore Wind Decade (2008–2017)

2008–2012

German Bight Enters & London Array Sets New Records

UK & Germany · Alpha Ventus (2010), London Array (2013, 630MW)

Germany launched its offshore wind programme with Alpha Ventus in 2010 — the country's first offshore wind farm, located 45km from shore in 30m water depth. The project was a government-backed demonstration, operated by a consortium of E.ON, Vattenfall, and EWE. German offshore wind development accelerated rapidly thereafter, with the North Sea and Baltic Sea becoming major markets.

The UK's London Array project (off the Thames Estuary) reached 630MW on completion in 2013, making it the world's largest offshore wind farm at the time. TIVs at this scale — approaching £1.5bn for a single project — required London market limit towers with multiple participating insurers. The era of the mega-project had arrived, and with it, a step-change in the insurance capacity requirements for the class.

The German market brought the additional complication of the Haftpflichtverband der Deutschen Industrie (HDI) and local insurer requirements, with Lloyd's typically participating as reinsurer behind German fronts (HDI, Allianz) for domestic regulatory compliance.

2013–2017

CfD Replaces RO — UK Market Matures; Costs Fall Dramatically

Offshore wind LCOE falls from ~£140/MWh (2014) to £57/MWh (2017 CfD auction)

The UK government's switch from the Renewables Obligation to Contracts for Difference (CfD) from 2014 transformed offshore wind economics. CfD auctions drove fierce competition between developers, compelling radical cost reduction — in turbines, foundations, installation, and O&M. The 2017 CfD round produced bids at £57.50/MWh, half of the £112/MWh level from 2015, vindicating the auction mechanism and cementing offshore wind as a competitive energy source.

From an insurance perspective, cost reduction had complex effects. Lower project costs meant lower TIVs for the same MW capacity, but rapidly scaling turbine sizes (from 3.6MW to 8MW+ through this period) meant larger single-unit values and new technology risk. The shift to jacket and tripod foundations for deeper water, and the introduction of floating foundation prototypes, created new underwriting questions.

The Megaproject Era & Market Headwinds (2018–Present)

2018–2022

Hornsea 1 & 2, Dogger Bank — and the Serial Defect Crisis

1GW+ projects dominate; Siemens Gamesa quality issues shake the market

Hornsea 1 (1,218MW, completed 2019) and Hornsea 2 (1,320MW, completed 2022) became the world's largest offshore wind farms in succession, both developed by Ørsted off the Yorkshire coast. These projects, with TIVs approaching £4bn and £5bn respectively, required limit towers assembled from a wide range of insurers — Lloyd's, Bermuda, European, and Asian — often with sovereign wealth fund or utility captive layers beneath.

The defining event of this period for the insurance market was the emergence of serial defect claims on the Siemens Gamesa SG 8.0-167 DD turbine platform. From 2021, Siemens Gamesa began disclosing quality issues with this turbine model, ultimately acknowledging problems that affected a significant proportion of the installed global fleet. The commercial cost to Siemens Gamesa exceeded €4bn; the insured loss to the offshore wind insurance market from these and related serial defects is estimated at over £2bn across the industry.

⚠ Serial Defect Coverage — The Market's Central Challenge

The Siemens Gamesa crisis demonstrated that serial defect exclusions and series loss limitations in policies — designed to cap exposure — were being tested as never before. Underwriters tightened serial loss language materially from 2021 onwards, with many syndicates imposing per-turbine sublimits and defining serial events by reference to the same root cause affecting more than a specified number of units.

2023–2026

Supply Chain Crisis & US Market Growing Pains

Cost inflation, CfD underbidding, Vineyard Wind blade failure, market reset

The post-COVID supply chain crisis hit offshore wind hard. Steel prices, shipping costs, and specialised installation vessel rates surged, pushing project costs above the CfD strike prices bid in 2019–2021. Multiple US East Coast projects were cancelled or renegotiated (Avangrid's Park City Wind, Equinor's Empire Wind renegotiation); Ørsted wrote down $4bn on US offshore assets in 2023.

In January 2024, Vineyard Wind (the first large-scale US offshore wind farm) suspended operations after a blade failure on a Siemens Gamesa turbine — further damaging market confidence. The offshore wind insurance market responded with continued rate pressure on construction risks (R3) given the complex loss environment, while operational risks (R1) for well-performing European projects saw modest softening for loss-free accounts.

Despite these headwinds, the long-term trajectory remains strongly positive. Global offshore wind capacity is projected to reach 500GW by 2030 and over 1,000GW by 2040, driven by national net-zero targets. Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, India, and Australia represent the next wave of major markets — each bringing distinct regulatory, geological, and meteorological risk profiles for Lloyd's underwriters.

The Insurance Market Parallel to Industry Growth

The offshore wind insurance market has grown from ad-hoc marine policies covering a handful of 400kW turbines in 1991 to a sophisticated specialist class at Lloyd's with dedicated wordings, standard serial defect language, technical risk surveys, and limit towers exceeding £5bn for single projects.

The class is fundamentally different from upstream oil and gas in several ways: losses are more frequent (primarily attritional — component failures, cable faults, blade damage) but rarely catastrophic in the way that blowouts or platform fires are; TIVs are large but loss severity per event is generally capped by the modular nature of wind farms; and the technology is evolving faster than historical loss data accumulates, making pricing science genuinely difficult.

The coming decade will see Lloyd's offshore wind market expand materially as new geographies (US East Coast, Taiwan, Korea, Australia) come online — each requiring new market expertise, new reinsurance structures, and new relationships with state utilities and sovereign wealth fund developers who have different risk appetite from the European private utility sector that built the existing market.
