Coverage Structure

Physical Damage — Operational (R1)

All-risks cover for turbines, foundations, inter-array cables, export cables, offshore substations, and associated equipment on an operational wind farm.

R1

Advanced Loss of Revenue (ALOR)

Business interruption linked to physical damage during operations. Covers lost revenue for the indemnity period (typically 12–24 months) following insured damage.

R1

Contractors' All Risks — Offshore (R3)

Marine CAR for the construction and installation phase of offshore wind projects. Covers all works, plant, and equipment during transport, installation, and pre-commissioning testing.

R3

Delay in Start-Up (DSU)

Business interruption arising from a delay to project completion following an insured physical damage event during construction. Covers lost revenue during the delay period.

R3

Marine Cargo & Transit

Coverage for turbine components, foundations, and cables in transit from manufacturer to installation site. Typically written on a marine cargo basis separate from the CAR policy.

R1 R3

Third Party Liability

Public and employers' liability arising from operations. Construction phase typically covered under project-specific liability policy; operational under operator's general liability programme.

R1 R3

Serial Loss / Serial Defect

The defining coverage challenge of offshore wind. Serial defect clauses (or exclusions) govern how the policy responds when the same failure mode affects multiple turbines — the key negotiating battleground.

R1 R3

Decommissioning Liability

Emerging line — covers costs associated with end-of-life turbine and foundation removal. Regulatory requirements are tightening across UKCS, Germany, and US OCS. Still developing at Lloyd's.

Why Offshore Renewable Energy Matters to Lloyd's

Offshore wind is the fastest-growing class in the Lloyd's specialty energy market. As upstream oil and gas volumes decline with the energy transition, offshore renewable energy is the natural growth opportunity — bringing large TIVs, complex risk profiles, and long-term relationships with developers, utilities, and governments.

The class is dominated by the North Sea (UK and Europe), but is rapidly expanding to the US East Coast, Taiwan, Japan, South Korea, and Australia. Each new market brings new regulatory requirements, new installation vessel fleets, and new manufacturer warranty regimes that interact with the primary insurance programme.

The defining challenge of the class is serial defect risk — when a manufacturing or design defect affects multiple turbines simultaneously across a fleet, insured losses can be enormous (Siemens Gamesa's 2021–2024 quality crisis is estimated to have cost the company over €4bn). How underwriters define, limit, and price serial loss coverage is the central underwriting question of the decade.
