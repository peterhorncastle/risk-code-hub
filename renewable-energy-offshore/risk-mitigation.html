Risk Mitigation & Loss Prevention

Technical Certification Standards

Offshore wind turbines and their supporting structures must be designed, manufactured, and certified to internationally recognised standards. Certification status is the first risk mitigation indicator an underwriter should request — uncertified or conditionally certified equipment attracts significant loading.

Blade Risk Management

Blades are the single most loss-prone component in an operational offshore wind farm, accounting for approximately 30–40% of all operational physical damage claims by value. Blade failures range from catastrophic (full blade drop) to attritional (leading edge erosion requiring coating repair). Both present distinct insurance challenges.

Leading Edge Erosion (LEE) — The Market's Attritional Loss Driver

Leading edge erosion occurs when rain droplets, hail, and airborne particles impact the blade leading edge at tip speeds of 70–100m/s, progressively removing the protective coating and underlying laminate. Unmanaged LEE can reduce turbine energy yield by 2–5% per year and ultimately requires blade replacement. It is the largest single source of attritional ALOR losses in the market.

LEE Inspection Regime

Drone-based visual inspection at least annually; close visual inspection (CVI) by rope access technicians every 2–3 years. Condition grading systems (DNVGL-RP-0171 erosion rating scale 0–5) allow trending. Underwriters request most recent drone inspection report and erosion grade distribution at renewal.

Leading Edge Protection (LEP) Systems

Factory-applied coatings (polyurethane, thermoplastic) or field-applied tape/paste systems. Modern LEP systems (Blade Repair Solutions, 3M, Mankiewicz) extend time-to-erosion onset from 3–5 years to 8–12 years. Operators with active LEP programmes demonstrate better loss profiles and attract underwriting credit.

Predictive Erosion Modelling

Rain drop impact models (DTU, DNVGL) combined with site metocean data allow prediction of erosion onset. Operators running predictive models and scheduling LEP application before onset — rather than reactively — show measurably lower blade repair claims. Underwriters increasingly request access to these models.

Tip Speed Reduction

Reducing blade tip speed (typically by curtailing rotor speed in rain events) reduces erosion rate significantly — a 10% tip speed reduction extends coating life by approximately 30%. Smart curtailment systems that identify rain via precipitation sensors are available and being deployed across newer projects. Active curtailment disclosure attracts underwriting credit.

⚠ Serial Defect Disclosure — Manufacturer Technical Bulletins

Underwriters must request disclosure of any outstanding manufacturer Technical Bulletins (TBs) or Campaign Field Modifications (CFMs) at inception and renewal. The Siemens Gamesa serial defect crisis began with TBs issued to operators months before public disclosure. An insured who receives a TB and does not disclose it to their underwriter may face a coverage challenge if a subsequent claim is related to the notified issue. This is a material fact under English law duty of fair presentation.

Structural Blade Failures — Underwriting Considerations

Information requested for blade risk assessment

  • Blade manufacturer and model — LM Wind Power, Siemens Gamesa, Vestas, TPI Composites, Aerojet Rocketdyne
  • Blade serial numbers and manufacturing batch — allows correlation with known defect batches
  • Most recent drone inspection report with erosion grade distribution
  • Any outstanding manufacturer Technical Bulletins or Campaign Field Modifications
  • LEP system applied: type, application date, supplier, and planned next application
  • Lightning strike count and any lightning-related blade damage in past 3 years
  • Tip speed profile and any curtailment systems in operation

Cable Risk Management

Cable faults are the most frequent loss category in offshore wind (by number of events), accounting for approximately 35–45% of all operational claims by frequency. Most cable losses are attritional — repair costs of £1m–£10m — but aggregate cable exposure across large portfolios is material.

Cable Burial & Protection

Inter-array cables should be buried to minimum 1m in sandy seabeds; 1.5m in areas with shipping or fishing activity. Export cables in shipping lanes typically buried 2–3m with rock armour protection at crossings. Post-lay burial verification by ROV survey is a standard Lloyd's underwriting requirement. Unburied cable segments must be disclosed.

Vessel Traffic Management

Anchor strike by fishing vessels and commercial shipping is a leading cause of cable damage. Array security zones, AIS monitoring, and coordination with MCA / national maritime authorities to register cable routes reduce anchor-strike frequency. Operators with active vessel monitoring attract lower cable loss rates.

J-Tube & Interface Management

The J-tube interface (where cable enters the foundation) is the highest-stress point on the cable system and the location of a disproportionate number of faults. Regular inspection of J-tube seals, cable centraliser condition, and dynamic bend restrictor condition is required. Any known J-tube anomalies are material facts.

Partial Discharge & Condition Monitoring

Distributed temperature sensing (DTS) and partial discharge monitoring systems detect early-stage insulation degradation before it becomes a fault. Operators running continuous cable condition monitoring demonstrate proactive risk management and attract underwriting credit. Requested as part of risk survey for large operational programmes.

Offshore Substation Risk Management

The offshore substation — containing the main power transformer(s), switchgear, and associated equipment — is the highest single-unit value component in most offshore wind farms after the turbine fleet. Transformer failures (Beatrice 2018, NnG 2023) have produced losses of £35–50m from a single component failure.

Transformer Specification & Testing

Factory Acceptance Testing (FAT) to IEC 60076 standards. Dissolved gas analysis (DGA) during FAT and at regular intervals post-commissioning detects internal faults before failure. DGA trending is a key early-warning indicator. Underwriters request DGA records for offshore transformers over 3 years old.

Redundancy Design

N-1 transformer redundancy (two transformers, each capable of carrying full farm output) dramatically reduces ALOR exposure from a transformer failure. Many offshore substations are N-0 — a single transformer — making ALOR exposure from a transformer failure effectively equal to full farm revenue for the repair duration. N-0 designs attract ALOR loading.

Cooling System Maintenance

Forced-oil, forced-air cooling systems (OFAF) must be maintained to specification. Cooling fan failures leading to transformer overtemperature are a known failure precursor. Cooling system service records are requested at survey. Any overtemperature events in previous 3 years are material facts.

Spare Parts Strategy

Lead times for replacement offshore power transformers can be 18–24 months from order. Operators without a spare transformer strategy or a framework agreement with the transformer manufacturer are exposed to extended ALOR periods following a failure. Underwriters assess spare parts strategy as part of ALOR indemnity period reasonableness review.

Marine Warranty Survey — Construction Phase

For R3 construction risks, a Marine Warranty Surveyor (MWS) acts as the underwriter's technical representative on site, approving critical marine operations (monopile installation, turbine upending and lifting, cable lay) before they are performed. Lloyd's construction policies typically require MWS sign-off as a policy condition — operations performed without MWS approval may result in coverage disputes.

MWS approval typically required for:

  • Monopile installation — stabbing guide, pile hammer selection, set criterion
  • Transition piece installation — grouted or bolted flange connection
  • Turbine component upending — blade, nacelle, tower section crane lifts
  • Jack-up vessel positioning and leg penetration assessment
  • Cable lay commencement — cable route clearance, vessel heading, weather window
  • Offshore substation installation — crane barge stability, lift plan
  • Any lifts above Hs = 1.5m significant wave height without specific MWS approval

Underwriter's Risk Mitigation Summary

The offshore wind insurance market's loss history is fundamentally different from upstream oil and gas: losses are predominantly attritional and serial rather than catastrophic. This changes the risk mitigation question — instead of asking "has the operator prevented a blowout?", underwriters must ask "has the operator detected and managed the early signs of serial component degradation before they become a fleet-wide claim?"

The three most important risk mitigation indicators for an offshore wind underwriter are: (1) the turbine manufacturer's current quality status and any outstanding TBs on the platform; (2) the blade LEE inspection programme and proactive LEP application record; and (3) the cable burial depth verification data and J-tube inspection record. These three data points explain the majority of the difference between good-risk and poor-risk offshore wind accounts at the same rate.

The parallel lesson from the Siemens Gamesa crisis is that manufacturers are not always transparent about emerging quality issues in real time. Underwriters who require annual disclosure of manufacturer correspondence and TB notifications — rather than waiting for public announcements — are better positioned to manage their accumulation exposure before losses crystallise.
