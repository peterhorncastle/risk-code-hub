The Offshore Wind Pricing Cycle (2010–2026)

The offshore renewable energy market is younger than upstream oil and gas, with its pricing cycle driven primarily by accumulated serial defect losses rather than catastrophic events. From 2010 to 2019 the market was characterised by fierce competition for new business as the sector grew rapidly; the 2020–2022 serial defect crisis triggered the first genuine market hardening seen in the class.

⚠ Serial Defect Loss Experience: The Market-Defining Event of 2021–2024

Unlike upstream energy (where catastrophic single events drive pricing), offshore wind pricing turns on accumulated serial defect losses. The Siemens Gamesa SG 8.0-167 quality crisis of 2021–2024 — affecting projects across the UK, Germany, Netherlands and Taiwan — is estimated to have cost the Lloyd's offshore wind market over £2bn in aggregate. This single loss experience drove physical damage rates up 40–80% on affected turbine platforms and forced fundamental changes to serial loss policy language.

Loss Ratio Analysis by Coverage Section

Offshore wind loss ratios are heavily skewed by serial defect events. On a non-serial year, physical damage attritional loss ratios are moderate; in a serial year, the loss ratio can spike to several hundred percent for affected turbine models. This asymmetry makes 5- and 10-year average loss ratios far more meaningful than single-year figures.

Key Conclusions — Rate Adequacy & Market Outlook

The offshore wind market is emerging from its first genuine hard market. Physical damage rates for non-SG platform turbines are now approaching technical adequacy for the first time since the class's inception. This represents a structural improvement — but the 2015–2019 trough was so deep that many syndicates' 10-year loss ratios remain above 100% even after the correction.

Serial defect risk remains the dominant pricing uncertainty. Until the Siemens Gamesa loss tail is fully developed (expected 2026–2028), underwriters cannot accurately price the serial defect component for SG-platform risks. The rational response is continued use of serial defect exclusions with facultative buy-back, rather than attempting to price the exposure within the base rate.

New turbine platforms (15–22MW class) introduce fresh technology risk. The Vestas V236-15.0, Siemens Gamesa SG 14-236, and GE Haliade-X turbines represent step-changes in size and complexity. Historical loss data from 8–10MW machines has limited applicability to 15–22MW platforms — underwriters pricing these risks are effectively writing first-of-kind technology with no credible loss history. Technology premiums of 15–25% above equivalent established-platform rates are being applied.

US East Coast is the growth market but carries elevated uncertainty. US offshore wind combines first-of-kind turbines, a new installation vessel fleet, new regulatory frameworks (BSEE offshore wind), and a challenging supply chain. US R3 risks carry a premium over comparable European construction risks reflecting this uncertainty.
