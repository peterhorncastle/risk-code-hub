2004Horns Rev 1 — Serial Gearbox & Transformer Failure (Denmark)Serial Defect

All 80 Vestas V80 transformers at Horns Rev 1 required removal to shore for refurbishment within two years of commissioning. Salt air ingress and humidity accelerated mechanical degradation beyond design assumptions. This was the offshore wind industry's first major serial loss and the event that introduced serial defect clauses into Lloyd's offshore wind wordings.

The insurance claim — believed to be in the low hundreds of millions of DKK — was the largest offshore wind loss to that date. More importantly it established the template for how serial defect claims are handled: manufacturer warranty response, operator business interruption, and the grey area between the two.

2007Burbo Bank — Vestas V80 Gearbox Serial Failures (UK)Component Failure

Burbo Bank (90MW, Liverpool Bay) experienced widespread gearbox failures in its Vestas V80 turbine fleet within the first two years of operation. The failures were linked to the same offshore marine environment issues seen at Horns Rev — and in some cases to the same gearbox design defects subsequently attributed to the V80 platform by independent investigators.

The Burbo Bank losses accelerated the industry's move away from the V80 platform and towards V90 and later V112 turbines with redesigned offshore-specific gearboxes. Insurance claims fell partly under construction warranty and partly under the operational all-risks policy.

2009Rhyl Flats — Inter-Array Cable Fault (UK)Cable Fault

Rhyl Flats (90MW, North Wales) suffered failure of its inter-array cable system within its first operational year. Cable faults became one of the most frequent loss categories in offshore wind — caused by installation damage, third-party anchor strikes, seabed movement, and manufacturing defects. This early incident highlighted the need for cable design margins appropriate to tidal scour conditions.

2010Alpha Ventus — Germany's First Offshore Wind Farm & Transformer FailureMarket Milestone

Alpha Ventus (60MW, 45km offshore, 30m water depth) opened as Germany's first offshore wind project — a government-backed demonstration jointly operated by E.ON, Vattenfall, and EWE. During commissioning, a transformer failure occurred in the offshore substation, causing delays and losses that tested both the construction (R3) and the nascent German regulatory insurance framework.

Alpha Ventus established the template for German offshore wind insurance: local fronting insurer (typically Allianz or HDI) with Lloyd's reinsurance behind — a structure that persists to this day due to BaFin requirements for admitted coverage of German risks.

2012Multiple UK Projects — Storm Blade Damage SeasonStorm / Weather

An active winter storm season in the North Sea caused blade tip damage and blade erosion claims across several UK Round 1 and early Round 2 projects. While individual claims were modest (£500k–£5m per turbine), the cumulative impact on Lloyd's offshore wind portfolios was significant — and highlighted that aggregate storm exposure across multiple projects in similar North Sea locations creates meaningful PML for the market.

The 2012 experience prompted underwriters to begin modelling aggregate storm exposure across portfolios and to more carefully review windspeed and wave height design criteria against actual operational data.

2013London Array — Inter-Array Cable Fault; Porpoise Strike ClosureCable Fault

London Array (630MW), then the world's largest offshore wind farm, suffered an inter-array cable fault in its first operational year — causing substantial revenue loss during the repair period. The farm also controversially suspended expansion plans due to concerns about sandwich tern and harbour porpoise disturbance, illustrating the environmental regulatory risk that affects project scope and insurance TIV assumptions.

2015UK CfD Round 1 — Offshore Wind Wins at £119/MWh; Market InflectionMarket Event

The UK's first CfD allocation round for offshore wind cleared at £119.89/MWh for Hornsea 1 — substantially higher than many expected but sharply below the Renewables Obligation equivalent. The CfD mechanism locked in long-term revenue certainty, enabling project finance and driving cost reduction. For insurers, the CfD price became the basis for ALOR/DSU sum insured calculations — a shift from ROC-based revenue assumptions.

2018Beatrice — Offshore Substation Transformer Failure (UK)Component Failure

Beatrice (588MW, North Sea, Caithness) suffered a transformer failure in its offshore substation shortly after commissioning — one of the largest single offshore wind insurance losses to that date. Transformer failures in offshore substations became a recurring theme as projects moved further offshore and installed higher-voltage transformers operating in challenging marine environments. Estimated insured loss: ~£50m.

The Beatrice loss accelerated underwriter scrutiny of offshore substation design, transformer cooling systems, and the adequacy of substation-specific engineering risk surveys. Separate substation sublimits became a common feature of R1 policy wordings from this point.

2019Hornsea 1 Complete — World's Largest Offshore Wind Farm (1,218MW)Industry Milestone

Ørsted's Hornsea 1 (1,218MW, off Yorkshire) became the world's largest operating offshore wind farm on completion in 2019. The project's TIV exceeded £4bn and its insurance programme was one of the most complex ever assembled for an offshore wind asset — requiring a limit tower of over £2bn across Lloyd's, Bermuda, and European insurers, with an Ørsted captive layer beneath. The insurance structure for Hornsea 1 became the template for the mega-project era.

2020East Anglia ONE — Serial Inter-Array Cable Faults (UK)Cable Fault

East Anglia ONE (714MW) experienced repeated inter-array cable faults within its first operational year, affecting multiple cable strings and causing extended production losses. The faults were linked to installation technique — cable bending radius violations during laying — and resulted in a significant ALOR claim alongside repair costs. The case reinforced the importance of cable burial depth monitoring and post-lay inspection programmes as underwriting conditions.

2021–2024Siemens Gamesa SG 8.0-167 — Global Serial Defect CrisisSerial Defect — Major

Siemens Gamesa disclosed in its 2021 annual results that its SG 8.0-167 DD turbine platform — the workhorse of the global offshore wind industry with installations across the UK, Germany, Netherlands, Taiwan, and beyond — was experiencing quality issues that affected a significant proportion of the fleet. Root causes included bearing failures, blade structural issues, and gearbox problems across multiple sub-component suppliers.

By 2024, Siemens Gamesa's parent Siemens Energy had injected over €4bn to cover warranty claims and remediation costs. The insured element of the loss to the offshore wind insurance market is estimated at over £2bn across affected projects globally — the largest serial defect event in the class's history. The crisis triggered widespread policy wording changes, with insurers introducing stricter serial loss definitions, per-turbine sub-limits, and in some cases blanket serial defect exclusions requiring separate facultative placement.

2023US Market — Ørsted US Write-Down & Project CancellationsMarket Event

Ørsted wrote down $4bn on its US offshore wind portfolio in November 2023, citing supply chain cost inflation, rising interest rates, and failure to negotiate revised contract terms with US state offtakers. Multiple other developers (Avangrid, Equinor, BP) cancelled or sought to renegotiate US East Coast projects. The construction market (R3) bore the brunt as project FIDs were delayed — reducing premium volumes and creating uncertainty around how far-advanced projects would be insured during the delay period.

2024Vineyard Wind — Siemens Gamesa Blade Failure; Shutdown (USA)Serial Defect

In January 2024, a blade on a Siemens Gamesa SG 14-222 DD turbine at Vineyard Wind (the first large-scale US offshore wind project) shed a section, prompting federal regulators (BSEE) to order a pause in operations. The incident — affecting America's flagship offshore wind project — attracted significant political attention and intensified scrutiny of Siemens Gamesa's quality issues on the newer, larger 14MW platform. Vineyard Wind remained partially offline as 2024 progressed, with ALOR implications for the insurance programme.
