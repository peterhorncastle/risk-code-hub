R1 — Operational Offshore Wind: Coverage & Rating

R1 — Operational

The R1 policy covers an operational offshore wind farm on an all-risks basis. The primary coverage sections are physical damage to the asset, advanced loss of revenue (ALOR) linked to physical damage, and third-party liability. The key underwriting challenges unique to this class are serial defect risk, technology obsolescence, and the interaction between manufacturer warranties and the primary policy.

Key Rating Factors — R1 Operational

🔧 Turbine Technology & Manufacturer

New turbine platforms with limited operational track record carry higher uncertainty. Manufacturer financial stability (relevant to warranty response) and loss history on the specific turbine model are critical inputs. Post-Siemens Gamesa crisis, SG 8.0 / SG 14 platform risks attract loading.

High Impact

⚡ Serial Defect Exposure

Number of identical turbines, homogeneity of the fleet, and whether the same turbine model has had notified quality issues. Underwriters now require disclosure of any outstanding manufacturer technical bulletins (TBs) or campaign field modifications (CFMs) at inception.

High Impact

🌊 Metocean Environment

Significant wave height (Hs), 50-year and 100-year return period wind speeds, tidal current velocity, and sea temperature (affects corrosion and material fatigue). North Sea and Irish Sea are more severe than German Bight nearshore locations. Tropical cyclone exposure (Taiwan, Australia) requires specific loading.

High Impact

📏 Water Depth & Distance to Shore

Greater water depth increases foundation complexity and repair logistics cost. Distance to shore affects crew transfer vessel access time and impacts ALOR repair duration assumptions. Floating wind is rated significantly differently from fixed-bottom due to mooring and dynamic cable risk.

Medium Impact

🔌 Cable Configuration

Number of cable strings, redundancy (looped vs. radial inter-array), cable age, burial depth, and proximity to shipping lanes, fishing grounds, and anchor zones. Cable faults are the most frequent loss category — cable design and maintenance regime are key underwriting factors.

Medium Impact

🏭 O&M Operator Quality

Whether O&M is performed by the turbine manufacturer (OEM) or an independent operator. OEM O&M typically includes extended warranty components, reducing insurance exposure. Independent O&M operators vary widely in technical capability — survey and capability assessment required.

Medium Impact

📅 Asset Age & Condition

Younger assets (under 5 years) carry technology/teething-period risk; older assets (over 15 years) carry fatigue, corrosion, and obsolescence risk. Life extension studies required for assets beyond original design life (typically 20–25 years).

Lower Impact

🏛 Revenue Mechanism (CfD / ROC / PPA)

Affects ALOR sum insured calculation. CfD contracts provide fixed price certainty; merchant / PPA revenue is market-price dependent. Underwriters calculate ALOR SI as P50 annual revenue × indemnity period — the revenue basis must be verified against the actual offtake contract.

Lower Impact

R3 — Construction Offshore Wind: Coverage & Rating

R3 — Construction

The R3 policy (Marine Contractors' All Risks for offshore renewable construction) covers all works, plant, and equipment during the construction and installation phase — from first component arriving at the offshore site through to practical completion and handover. The most complex construction insurance product in the Lloyd's specialty market, requiring understanding of marine operations, civil engineering, mechanical/electrical plant, and project finance.

⚠ Serial Defect — The Coverage Battleground

The most contested coverage question in offshore wind underwriting: when the same defect affects multiple turbines, is it one event or many? Most Lloyd's policies use a Series Loss Clause limiting recovery to a defined number of turbines (typically the greater of 1 turbine or 3% of the fleet) per series. The definition of "series" — whether by root cause, manufacturer lot, or commissioning sequence — is heavily negotiated. Post-Siemens Gamesa 2021, many underwriters have moved to blanket serial defect exclusions with separate facultative placements available at additional premium.

Information Requirements at Inception

R1 — Operational (Minimum Submission Requirements)

  • Project details: site name, location, water depth, distance to shore, commissioning date
  • Turbine specification: manufacturer, model, unit rating (MW), number of turbines, hub height, rotor diameter
  • Foundation type: monopile, jacket, tripod, gravity base, floating
  • TIV schedule: per-turbine values, cable values, substation value, total TIV
  • Annual P50 revenue figure and offtake contract basis (CfD price, PPA rate, merchant)
  • O&M contractor: OEM or independent; scope of O&M agreement; warranty remaining
  • Loss history: all claims and near-misses in past 5 years
  • Outstanding manufacturer technical bulletins or campaign field modifications
  • Most recent independent technical assessment / owners engineer report
  • Cable inspection and burial depth records (inter-array and export)

R3 — Construction (Minimum Submission Requirements)

  • Project overview: developer, contractor, EPC/EPCI structure, planned start and completion dates
  • Contract value and TIV build schedule (peak offshore exposure)
  • Installation vessel(s): name, flag, class, age, owner, charter terms (BIMCO form), deck crane capacity
  • Wind turbine generator specification and DNV/BV certification status
  • Foundation design: pile diameter, wall thickness, penetration depth, scour protection design
  • Cable installation method: plough, jetting, or pre-trenched; burial depth specification
  • Offshore substation: voltage rating, transformer supplier, cooling system
  • Metocean design criteria: Hs50, Hs100, 50-yr wind speed, current profile
  • Environmental window assumptions: planned operational metocean limits per operation type
  • Marine warranty surveyor (MWS) — appointed? Which firm?
  • DSU sum insured: P50 daily revenue × indemnity period

The ALOR / DSU Calculation — A Common Underwriting Error

The most frequent underwriting error on offshore wind is setting the ALOR or DSU sum insured incorrectly. The SI must reflect the P50 annual revenue multiplied by the indemnity period in months — not TIV, not nameplate capacity, and not the CfD strike price in isolation.

Example: A 500MW farm with 38% capacity factor, generating at £57/MWh under a CfD, earns approximately £95m per year. An 18-month ALOR indemnity period gives an ALOR SI of £142.5m. Many submissions understate this figure — often because the broker uses nameplate capacity × strike price without applying the capacity factor.

Underwriters should always verify: (GWP of project × capacity factor × 8,760 hours × CfD/PPA price) × (indemnity months / 12) = correct ALOR SI. Any significant discrepancy between the submitted SI and this calculation should be queried before binding.
