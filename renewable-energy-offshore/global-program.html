Programme Architecture

Unlike upstream oil and gas, where global IOC programmes with multiple assets are standard, offshore wind insurance programmes are often structured around single projects during construction (R3) and then transition to operational portfolio programmes (R1) as an operator's installed capacity grows. Utilities such as Ørsted, RWE, Vattenfall, and SSE now operate fleets of 10–30 wind farms and place enterprise-level portfolio programmes covering all assets.

🌱 Single Project — Construction (R3)

The most common structure for new-build projects: a bespoke DSU/contract works programme for a single offshore wind farm. Lead underwriter appointed at financial close; coverage tracks project milestones (monopile installation, WTG installation, commissioning). TIV may peak at £1.5–4bn depending on project scale.

⚡ Portfolio Operational Programme (R1)

For utilities with 5+ operational wind farms, a portfolio programme consolidates all assets under a single master policy. Premium is calculated on total installed TIV. Serial defect aggregation and clash exposure across the portfolio are the defining underwriting challenges. Ørsted, RWE, and Vattenfall all operate portfolio programmes.

🔄 Wrap Programme (R1 + R3)

A combined programme covering both operational farms (R1) and construction projects (R3) within a single policy structure — increasingly common for large utilities with concurrent builds. Provides consistency of coverage terms but requires careful delineation of construction vs operational triggers and deductibles.

🏢 Captive Integration

Ørsted, RWE, and Equinor all have captive insurance vehicles. The captive retains the first layer of risk (typically £50–150m per loss), reducing commercial market premiums. Lloyd's provides the excess layer. Captive integration is most efficient for operators with 3GW+ installed capacity where risk diversification within the portfolio is sufficient.

Serial Defect Aggregation — The Portfolio Programme Challenge

Serial defect exposure is unique to offshore wind and fundamentally shapes how global programmes are structured. When a component manufactured to a common design specification fails across multiple turbines simultaneously, the aggregate loss across a portfolio can be catastrophic — as demonstrated by the Siemens Gamesa SG 8.0-167 blade bearing failure (2021–2024).

⚠ The Serial Defect Aggregation Problem

A single defective turbine model deployed across 10 wind farms in a portfolio programme creates a correlated exposure that can breach primary limits simultaneously at each site. Consider: 10 × 100-turbine farms using the SG 8.0-167 — a systemic blade bearing failure affects all 1,000 turbines, triggering ALOR at every farm concurrently. Aggregate losses can reach £1–3bn across a single portfolio before any policy layer responds.

  • Per-turbine sub-limits (typically £5–15m/turbine) limit individual recovery but do not cap aggregate exposure across a fleet
  • Serial defect clauses typically require a minimum number of affected units (often 3–5 turbines) before serial loss provisions trigger
  • Some policies apply a blanket serial defect exclusion — requiring separate wrap cover to be negotiated and separately rated
  • Underwriters increasingly require OEM diversification across a portfolio to limit correlated serial exposure

Key Offshore Wind Markets — Regulatory & Insurance Profiles

Each major offshore wind market has a distinct regulatory framework, Lloyd's access status, and insurance market convention. The table below summarises the key underwriting considerations by jurisdiction for both R1 (operational) and R3 (construction) risks.

🇬🇧 United Kingdom

Lloyd's codes: R1, R3

Key operators: Ørsted, SSE Renewables, RWE, Vattenfall, bp, TotalEnergies

Risk profile: World's largest installed offshore wind capacity (~15GW, 2025); North Sea metocean; ageing Round 1–2 assets (Scroby Sands, North Hoyle, Horns Rev); Round 4–5 gigawatt-scale projects in development; cable corridor congestion

Regulatory: FCA-admitted (Lloyd's fully admitted in UK); no fronting required. MHI Vestas, Siemens Gamesa, GE Vernova as primary OEMs. OFTO model for cable corridors.

Programme notes: Lloyd's leads the UK offshore wind market globally. OFTO (Offshore Transmission Owner) regime separates cable corridor ownership from generation asset — cable corridors may be insured separately under OFTO's own programme. CfD (Contract for Difference) mechanism affects ALOR structuring as revenue is effectively fixed.

Lloyd's codes: R1, R3

Key operators: Ørsted, Shell, Eneco, RWE, Northland Power, OceanWind (Parkwind)

🇺🇸 United States (East Coast)

Lloyd's codes: R1, R3

Key operators: Ørsted, Equinor (Empire Wind), Avangrid, Dominion Energy, bp/Equinor, Vineyard Wind (Avangrid/CIP)

Risk profile: Atlantic OCS (Outer Continental Shelf); hurricane windstorm risk (Cat 3–5 along East Coast); Jones Act compliance for vessel operations; immature regulatory/supply chain environment; severe cost inflation (2022–2024 PPA renegotiations)

Regulatory: BOEM (Bureau of Ocean Energy Management) — federal permitting. US state PUC/PPA agreements. Jones Act: US-flagged installation vessels mandated for cable laying and some installation work. Lloyd's is non-admitted in most US states — surplus lines placement required (Lloyd's via NILS or admitted fronting carrier). State insurance commissioners impose premium taxes on surplus lines.

Programme notes: The Jones Act requirement significantly inflates construction costs ($50–150m per project vs European equivalent). Jones Act-compliant WTIV (Wind Turbine Installation Vessel) availability is extremely limited (1–2 vessels 2024–2026). Vineyard Wind and other projects have faced cancellation or major renegotiation — DSU/delay exposures very material during this period.

🇹🇼 Taiwan

Lloyd's codes: R1, R3

Key operators: Ørsted (Greater Changhua), Vestas/JERA (Formosa), Copenhagen Infrastructure Partners, Northland Power, MacQuarie

Risk profile: Taiwan Strait (one of world's most active typhoon tracks — average 3–5 direct typhoon hits per decade); seismically active region (Taiwan sits on Pacific Ring of Fire); extreme metocean conditions; rapid market growth (5.5GW target by 2025)

Regulatory: Financial Supervisory Commission (FSC) — fronting required: Lloyd's must operate via local Taiwanese admitted insurer (Fubon, Cathay Century, or Taiwan Fire & Marine) with Lloyd's providing reinsurance behind the local front. Local content requirements apply. Bureau of Energy oversees project approvals.

Programme notes: Taiwan is the most complex offshore wind market for Lloyd's due to typhoon exposure (wind speeds up to 65m/s), seismic risk, and fronting requirements. Turbine foundation design must meet DNV-ST-0126 with typhoon-specific load cases. Premium rates for R3 construction in Taiwan are 50–100% higher than equivalent North Sea projects.

The Jones Act — US Market Underwriting Implications

The Merchant Marine Act of 1920 (the "Jones Act") requires that goods transported between US ports be carried on US-built, US-owned, and US-crewed vessels. Its application to offshore wind construction creates some of the most significant cost and risk challenges in the global offshore wind insurance market.

⚠ Jones Act Impact on R3 Construction Cover

European WTIVs (Wind Turbine Installation Vessels) — which carry most of the world's installation capacity — are not Jones Act-compliant. They can operate in US waters only under specific exemptions (e.g., US-flagged feeder barges must be used for component transport). The practical result is that US offshore wind installation relies on a very small number of Jones Act-compliant vessels, driving significant schedule risk:

  • If the sole WTIV suffers a breakdown during an installation weather window, the entire project can lose an entire installation season (6–9 months delay)
  • DSU/delay cover for US offshore wind construction must account for installation vessel unavailability as a primary delay trigger
  • Vessel hire rates for Jones Act WTIVs are 2–3× European equivalents; any damage to the vessel is a critical path event
  • Underwriters are requiring separate delay-in-installation (DII) endorsements for US projects that explicitly address vessel unavailability scenarios

Jones Act — Underwriting Checklist for US R3 Risks

  • Confirm installation vessel name and Jones Act compliance status
  • Confirm vessel availability window and contingency vessel arrangements
  • Review WTIV contract: Who bears delay risk if vessel is damaged or unavailable?
  • Check DSU policy wording: Does it trigger on vessel unavailability as well as physical damage to works?
  • Confirm marine warranty surveyor (MWS) appointment for installation operations
  • Review hurricane contingency plan and vessel withdrawal procedures during Atlantic season (June–November)
  • Confirm cable-lay vessel (CLV) Jones Act status for inter-array and export cable installation
  • Check state-level surplus lines tax position and broker surplus lines licence

R3 Construction Programme Design

Construction programmes for major offshore wind farms are among the most complex insurance placements in the London market, typically placed 12–18 months before the first major marine installation operation commences, and remain live until the farm achieves Commercial Operation Date (COD) and transitions to R1 operational cover.

R3 to R1 Handover — Transition Points

Construction-to-Operational Transition Protocol

The transition from R3 (construction) to R1 (operational) cover is a critical risk management moment that must be carefully managed to avoid coverage gaps:

  • Provisional acceptance: Individual turbines accepted by the developer from the EPC contractor at commissioning — usually triggers transfer of risk from contractor's insurer to developer's R1 programme, on a turbine-by-turbine basis
  • COD (Commercial Operation Date): The point at which the entire farm achieves full operational status and the R3 DSU indemnity period terminates; R1 ALOR coverage begins
  • Defects liability period (DLP): A period (typically 12–24 months post-COD) during which the EPC contractor retains responsibility for defects — warranty claims during the DLP should be channelled to the contractor's professional indemnity/warranty programme, not the R1 policy
  • OEM warranty period: Manufacturer's warranty on turbines (typically 5 years) runs concurrently with R1 cover — the policy must clearly address the interaction between warranty recovery and insurance indemnity

Captive Structures & Alternative Risk Transfer

As the offshore wind market matures, the largest utilities are increasingly using captive insurance and alternative risk transfer (ART) mechanisms to manage their growing asset portfolios. This trend is directly reducing commercial market premium volume, but increasing the sophistication of risks placed at Lloyd's (which is now typically writing excess layers with higher risk-adjusted returns).

🏦 Wholly Owned Captive

Ørsted, RWE, and Vattenfall all operate captives. The captive retains attritional losses (blade erosion repairs, minor cable damage) up to a threshold (£25–100m), with Lloyd's providing excess of loss above. Captives are typically domiciled in Guernsey, Dublin, or Luxembourg for European operators.

📊 ILS / Catastrophe Bond

Insurance-Linked Securities (cat bonds) are increasingly used for upper catastrophe layers — particularly typhoon exposure in Asia-Pacific (Taiwan) and hurricane in the US. A cat bond transfers parametric or indemnity risk to capital markets investors, providing multi-year capacity at defined trigger levels.

🤝 Industry Mutuals

Renewables-focused mutuals (e.g., Renewables Insurance Ltd — a captive/mutual hybrid) are emerging as operators collaborate to pool serial defect risk. Mutual pooling is particularly attractive for managing ALOR aggregation from a common OEM defect where no single operator has sufficient diversification.

⚡ Multi-Year Programme Structures

Some utilities negotiate 3–5 year R1 programmes with Lloyd's syndicates, providing rate stability and administration efficiency. Multi-year structures require careful premium adjustment mechanisms (indexed to installed TIV growth) and claim review clauses if loss ratios breach agreed thresholds.

Key Programme Design Principles — Offshore Wind

Offshore wind global programmes differ fundamentally from upstream oil and gas in four critical dimensions that underwriters must understand and address in programme design:

1. Serial defect aggregation — No other class of insurance has equivalent correlated exposure from a common component defect. Programme design must explicitly address serial event triggers, per-turbine sub-limits, and aggregate serial deductibles.

2. ALOR revenue basis — The basis of ALOR (CfD, PPA, merchant) fundamentally affects both sum insured calculation and claims adjustment. Lloyd's underwriters must understand the revenue support mechanism for each farm in a portfolio.

3. LEE (Leading Edge Erosion) management — Blade erosion is the dominant attritional driver for operational programmes. Premium adequacy at R1 is highly sensitive to LEE inspection frequency and repair programme quality.

4. Jurisdictional complexity — Taiwan, US, Brazil, and Japan all require fronting arrangements and have unique regulatory overlays. Programme design must be built with local counsel and local fronting partners from the outset, not retrofitted at placement.
