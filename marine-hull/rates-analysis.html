Market Pricing Cycle — H&M Rate Index

Marine hull rates have historically been highly cyclical — driven by catastrophic loss events, market capacity, and competitive pressure from Scandinavian mutual clubs. The market experienced a structural shift from 2018 onwards after an extended and damaging soft market, and saw further uplift from the Red Sea war risk crisis in 2024. EST

📈 1997–2002 — Hard Market (Post-1990s Loss Years)

A series of major losses (Sea Empress 1996, Erika 1999, MV Prestige 2002, earlier ferry disasters) combined with 9/11 war risk impact drove a meaningful hardening in hull rates through the late 1990s and early 2000s. Some single-hull tanker rates doubled as regulators accelerated phase-out. EST

📉 2003–2011 — Prolonged Soft Market

Excess global shipping capacity and fierce competition from Norwegian hull clubs drove sustained rate softening. The commodity supercycle (2003–2008) inflated vessel values while rates fell — temporarily masking the deterioration in loss ratios. IHC 2003 adoption was slow; ITC 1983 remained the standard. EST

📈 2012 — Costa Concordia Shock

The Costa Concordia grounding (January 2012, ~$2.4bn total insured loss) was a significant market shock. Passenger vessel rates hardened sharply — 30–50% in some cases. Cruise ship and RoRo rates saw the most pronounced movement. Dry bulk and tanker segments saw less impact. EST

📉 2013–2018 — Deepening Soft Market

Despite the Costa Concordia, overcapacity drove rates lower throughout 2013–2018. Hull combined ratios for many Lloyd's syndicates exceeded 110–120%. Multiple syndicates exited or restricted marine hull. The IUMI Ocean Hull Report (2018) highlighted systemic unprofitability. EST

➡️ 2019–2022 — Market Correction

Following years of poor results, Lloyd's mandated a remediation programme (Decile 10) which forced loss-making syndicates to restructure or exit. Marine hull rates began recovering from 2019 — 10–25% rate increases in 2019–2021 across most segments. Vessel values also inflated during the pandemic supply chain boom, supporting GWP growth. EST

📈 2023–2026 — Firming Market & Red Sea War Risk

H&M rates continue to firm modestly — 5–15% in standard segments (2023–2024). The Red Sea crisis (2024) triggered a sharp war risk premium surge: JWC listed area additional premiums of 0.5–1% per voyage from near-zero. War risk market tightens; specialist capacity reduces. Balancing the H&M and war risk market is the defining challenge of 2024–2025. EST

Return on Equity — Three Scenarios

ROE calculated on a hypothetical $500m GWP marine hull portfolio (H&M and war risk combined). Loss ratio, expense ratio and investment income assumptions vary by scenario. ILLUS

🐂 Bull Case

+19%

Benign loss year (no major catastrophe); continued rate firming +10%; war risk premium income high; strong investment returns on float; expense ratio 30%. Combined ratio ~82%.

📊 Base Case

+9%

Moderate losses (one $200–400m event); stable H&M rates; war risk premium elevated but some capacity returning; expense ratio 32%. Combined ratio ~95%.

🐻 Bear Case

−14%

Multiple large losses (VLCC TL + ULCV structural failure + cruise ship grounding); war risk escalation; soft H&M market pressure returns; expense ratio 34%. Combined ratio ~125%.
