1688

⚓ Edward Lloyd's Coffee House — Lloyd's Market Founded

Edward Lloyd opens his coffee house in Tower Street, London, 1688, becoming the principal gathering place for underwriters, merchants and ship owners. The first formal Lloyd's underwriting society formed 1769; Lloyd's Act 1871 gives statutory basis. Marine hull is the foundational class. VER

1745 · 1906

🏛️ Marine Insurance Acts — Statutory Framework

Marine Insurance Act 1745 prohibits wagering policies and establishes insurable interest. Marine Insurance Act 1906 — drafted by Mackenzie Chalmers — codifies entire common law of marine insurance (total loss, CTL, GA, sue & labour, utmost good faith, warranties). Still the governing statute for London market hull policies. VER

1912

🚢 RMS Titanic — £1m Hull Loss; SOLAS 1914

Titanic strikes iceberg on 15 April 1912; 1,517 lives lost. Hull insured for £1m — paid by Lloyd's syndicate led by Alexander Miller. VER The disaster leads directly to SOLAS 1914 (first International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea), establishing lifeboat requirements and the International Ice Patrol. VER

1967

🛢️ Torrey Canyon — First Supertanker Catastrophe

Torrey Canyon grounds off Cornwall; 119,000 tonnes crude oil spilled — largest oil disaster to date. VER RAF bombs the wreck with napalm to burn off oil. Triggers Civil Liability Convention (CLC) 1969 and MARPOL 1973/78. Permanently changes the tanker hull/P&I interface and pollution endorsement landscape. VER

1982 · 1983

📋 MAR Form & Institute Time Clauses (Hulls) 1983

The Lloyd's SG policy form (in use since 1779) is replaced by the modern MAR form. Simultaneously, the Institute of London Underwriters issues ITC (Hulls) 1/11/83 — still the most widely used hull wording globally. Introduces 4/4 RDC option, sistership clause, updated GA provisions. VER

1984–1988

⚔️ Iran–Iraq Tanker War — War Risk Hard Market

Iraq and Iran attack neutral shipping in the Persian Gulf — over 340 vessels attacked, 63 sunk. VER War risk additional premiums surge to 2–5% of vessel value per voyage in peak periods. EST The Tanker War establishes the modern Joint War Committee (JWC) listed areas framework and the separate war risk underwriting discipline. Benchmark event for the 2024 Red Sea crisis.

1987 · 1994

⛴️ Herald of Free Enterprise & Estonia — RoRo Disasters

Herald of Free Enterprise capsizes off Zeebrugge (1987) — 193 dead. Bow doors left open. VER MV Estonia sinks in Baltic (1994) — 852 dead; bow visor failure. VER Worst European peacetime maritime disaster since WWII. Both losses directly drive ISM Code development and mandatory safety management system requirements for all passenger vessels.

1989

🛢️ Exxon Valdez — OPA 90 & Double Hull Mandate

Exxon Valdez grounds in Prince William Sound, Alaska; 11m gallons crude spilled. VER Exxon's total liability (cleanup + punitive damages) exceeds $5bn over years of litigation. VER US Oil Pollution Act 1990 (OPA 90) mandates double hulls for tankers in US waters — the most significant structural change to tanker design in the 20th century. Transforms tanker hull underwriting globally.

1998 · 2002

🛡️ ISM Code Mandatory — Safety Management Revolution

International Safety Management (ISM) Code enters force under SOLAS Chapter IX — passenger/tanker vessels 1998; all SOLAS vessels 2002. VER Every company must have a documented Safety Management System (SMS), Document of Compliance (DOC) from flag state, Safety Management Certificate (SMC) per vessel. ISM compliance becomes a standard hull underwriting requirement — non-compliance can void policy.

1999

🛢️ MV Erika — EU Single-Hull Phase-Out

Ageing single-hull tanker Erika breaks in two off Brittany; 20,000 tonnes heavy fuel oil pollutes 400km of French coastline. VER EU accelerates single-hull phase-out via Regulation 417/2002. "Erika III" package introduces EMSA (European Maritime Safety Agency) and mandatory places of refuge for ships in distress. VER

2003

📄 International Hull Clauses (IHC) 2003

Joint Hull Committee issues IHC 2003 to replace ITC 1983. Clearer machinery damage provisions, updated war/terrorism exclusions, revised GA provisions (York-Antwerp 2004). VER However, adoption has been slower than anticipated — ITC 1983 remains dominant in global fleet placements as of 2024. IHC 2003 more common in Scandinavian/European markets.

2012

🚢 Costa Concordia — Largest Single Hull Loss in History

114,500 GT cruise ship grounds off Giglio, Italy; 32 killed. Hull insured value ~$512m. VER Wreck removal cost ~$1.5bn — one of the most complex and expensive salvage operations ever. Total insured loss (hull + P&I + wreck removal) estimated ~$2.4bn. EST Drives mandatory passenger ship wreck removal requirements and renewed scrutiny of master authority.

2013

📦 MOL Comfort — ULCV Structural Failure

8,110 TEU ultra-large container vessel breaks in two in the Indian Ocean in moderate weather conditions. Both sections sink; total loss of vessel and cargo. VER Loss reveals that hull girder stress calculations for post-Panamax ULCVs had not adequately modelled ocean flexure loads. Triggers classification society review of ULCV structural standards and cargo distribution monitoring requirements.

2015–2019

📉 Soft Market Trough — Historic Low Hull Rates

Hull premiums reach historic lows; rate on line for standard dry bulk carriers falls below 0.20% in some segments. EST Combined ratios exceed 110% for several years. Numerous Lloyd's syndicates restrict or exit marine hull lines. JHC market performance reviews published. Several Norwegian hull clubs report significant deficits. Market rationalisation accelerates from 2019.

2019

📦 MSC Zoe — 342 Containers Lost at Sea

19,224 TEU MSC Zoe loses 342 containers in North Sea storm, 1–2 January 2019. Several containers hold hazardous materials; coastal pollution affects Dutch and German North Sea islands. VER Loss triggers debate on container securing standards (CSS Code compliance), hazardous cargo mis-declaration and stack load monitoring. IMO subsequently reviews guidance on container securing.

2021

⚓ Ever Given — Suez Canal Blockage, 6 Days

20,388 TEU Ever Given grounds in Suez Canal, 23 March 2021; refloated 29 March. Canal blocked for 6 days — ~$9.6bn/day in disrupted trade. EST Suez Canal Authority claims ~$916m salvage/compensation; ultimately settled. VER Business interruption and consequential loss gaps in hull cover exposed. Highlights systemic risk from single-point global shipping chokepoints.

2019 · 2023

🔒 BIMCO Cyber Security Clauses

BIMCO issues standard cyber security clauses for hull policies (2019, updated 2023) following NotPetya attack on Maersk (2017, ~$300m business loss). VER Clauses distinguish hull cyber (vessel damage from cyber — some H&M policies respond) from consequential cyber loss (typically excluded). LMA5400–5403 exclusion clauses increasingly applied to hull slips. The cyber/hull coverage interface remains contested in 2024 placements.

2022

⚔️ Russia–Ukraine — Vessel Seizures & Black Sea War Risk

Russia's invasion of Ukraine (24 February 2022) leads to naval blockade of Ukrainian ports, vessel seizures, and mine risk in the Black Sea. VER Over 60 foreign vessels trapped in Ukrainian ports at outbreak. JWC adds Black Sea and Sea of Azov to Listed Areas. War risk additional premiums spike. Several vessels hit by drifting mines. Grain corridor agreement (July 2022–July 2023) provides partial relief. VER

March 2024

🌉 Dali / Francis Scott Key Bridge — Baltimore

Container vessel Dali suffers total power loss; strikes Francis Scott Key Bridge, Baltimore; bridge collapses, killing 6 workers. VER Port of Baltimore closed for weeks. Hull loss, P&I liability for bridge reconstruction and economic losses potentially exceeding $2bn — one of the most complex US maritime casualty events in modern history. EST

2024

🚀 Red Sea / Houthi Attacks — War Risk Hard Market

Houthi forces attack 100+ commercial vessels in Red Sea and Gulf of Aden from November 2023. VER Major container lines reroute via Cape of Good Hope (additional 10+ days, significant cost). JWC Red Sea listed area war risk additional premiums surge to 0.5–1%+ of vessel value per voyage from near-zero. EST Estimated annualised war risk premium uplift ~$400m+. EST Most significant marine war risk event since the Iran-Iraq Tanker War.
