Programme Architecture — Layers of a Fleet Hull Programme

Large shipping fleets typically purchase layered hull programmes rather than single-carrier coverage. A layered structure allows the insured to optimise cost by using self-insurance (deductibles / captives) at the lower end, and spreading catastrophic capacity across multiple markets at the top. The London Lloyd's market typically leads or co-leads the upper layers for complex global fleets. EST

Excess Layer 3 — Catastrophe / Top Cover
$200m xs $100m — Specialist catastrophe capacity; Lloyd's syndicates and Bermuda market; responds only on total loss or near-total loss events. Very limited claims frequency. Annual premium low relative to capacity. ILLUS
Excess Layer 2
$50m xs $50m — Lloyd's syndicate panel and European insurer co-insurance; larger partial losses and CTL claims. Responds on major machinery damage, stranding and collision events. ILLUS
Excess Layer 1
$25m xs $25m — Lloyd's H&M market; responds on significant partial losses above the primary layer. Most active on larger hull partial loss events (propeller damage, grounding, fire). ILLUS
Primary Layer
$25m xs $500,000 deductible — Lead Lloyd's syndicate sets the rate and conditions; following market subscribes at agreed terms. Handles the majority of attritional partial losses, machinery claims, and moderate groundings. 3/4 RDC included. ILLUS
Deductible / Self-Retention / Captive
$500,000 each and every loss — Large fleet operators often run a captive insurer for this layer. Absorbs attritional claims (crew negligence, minor machinery, small groundings). Captive may reinsure into the primary market above the fleet aggregate. ILLUS

Tower structure is illustrative only. Actual programme design depends on fleet size, vessel values, loss history, market appetite and insured's risk appetite. ILLUS

Fleet Owner Programme Profiles

🛢️ Major Tanker Fleet (VLCC/Suezmax)

  • 30–80 vessels; hull values $40–120m each; fleet IV $2–6bn
  • Full layered H&M programme; war risk fleet policy + voyage additional premiums for Middle East Gulf, Red Sea, Black Sea
  • TMSA 3 compliance shared with oil major customers
  • SIRE 2.0 inspection programme; class survey tracking system
  • LOH at $50–100,000/day for top vessels; 14-day deductible
  • Captive insurer absorbs deductible layer
  • P&I: International Group club (Gard, UK Club, Steamship); pooling for large pollution claims
  • MARPOL compliance critical — IOPP breach voids both hull and P&I cover

📦 Container Line (ULCV Fleet)

  • 20–150 vessels including ULCVs (18,000–24,000 TEU); values $100–200m each
  • Wreck removal exposure is the key underwriting concern (Ever Given, MOL Comfort precedents)
  • Hull programme includes wreck removal endorsement — cost can dwarf hull value
  • Structural monitoring systems (hull stress sensors) increasingly required by class and underwriters
  • Red Sea war risk (2024): full fleet rerouted via Cape; war risk additional premiums material
  • Cyber: ECDIS and cargo planning systems are high-risk targets; BIMCO clauses attached
  • LOH critical: ULCV earning $100,000+/day; 30-day deductible typical

🚗 Car Carrier (PCTC) Operator

  • 10–30 vessels; values $50–150m each; high risk post-EV battery fire era
  • H&M rates hardened 15–30%+ in 2023–2024 following Felicity Ace, Golden Ray EST
  • Underwriters now require: CO₂ / high-expansion foam systems; EV pre-loading screening (SOC, battery health checks); thermal imaging on loading ramps
  • RoRo stability: high centre of gravity; GM management critical
  • GA risk: any fire on a car carrier results in enormous cargo GA claims (thousands of vehicles)
  • P&I: pollution from burning vehicle fluids; passenger / driver liability
  • War risk: 2024 Red Sea diversion adds 14+ days per voyage; operational cost impact significant

⚓ Single-Vessel SPV Owner

  • Single vessel; no fleet; hull IV $5–30m (often older tonnage)
  • H&M or TLO depending on vessel age and market appetite
  • No captive — deductible self-funded from vessel earnings
  • P&I: likely fixed-premium P&I (non-IG club) for smaller vessels; mutual IG club for larger
  • Broker role critical — must navigate Lloyd's market with no incumbent advantage
  • Voyage trading restrictions common — limited to specific routes
  • LOH: often not purchased — vessel owner cannot sustain daily hire rate cover cost
  • War risk: purchased voyage-by-voyage as required

⚓ P&I Club Interface — How H&M and P&I Work Together on a Major Casualty

When a major casualty occurs (e.g. a VLCC grounds and causes oil pollution), multiple parties are involved simultaneously:

  • Hull underwriters (T): Pay for physical damage to the vessel — hull plating, machinery, equipment. Also respond to 3/4 collision liability (RDC) if the vessel collides with another ship. Sue & Labour charges (reasonable steps to avert or minimise loss) are covered.
  • P&I club: Pays the remaining 1/4 collision liability; cargo damage/shortage; crew claims; wreck removal (if not on the hull policy); pollution liability (CLC / MARPOL — can run to hundreds of millions for a VLCC spill); passenger/third-party bodily injury.
  • War risk underwriters: If the grounding was caused by a war peril (e.g. a mine, missile strike), war risk pays instead of H&M. The FC&S Clause in the H&M policy excludes this.
  • LOH underwriters (TX): Pay the daily charter rate while the vessel is off-hire for repairs — subject to the waiting period deductible. LOH only triggers if H&M first pays the underlying physical damage claim.
  • General Average: If cargo is jettisoned or a deliberate sacrifice is made to save the common venture, the GA Adjuster (specialist firm — Richards Hogg Lindley, Stichting) apportions the costs between hull and cargo underwriters per the York-Antwerp Rules 2016.
  • Reinsurance: Above the retention/deductible, each market participant passes claims to their reinsurers. The International Group (IG) of P&I Clubs operates a pooling arrangement — individual clubs pool losses above $10m; above $100m, the IG purchases excess-of-loss reinsurance in the commercial market.
