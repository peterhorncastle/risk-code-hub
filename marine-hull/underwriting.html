Hull & Machinery (T) Rating Formula

Marine hull (T) premium is calculated as a rate applied to the agreed hull insured value (IV). The rate reflects vessel-specific risk factors including age, vessel type, classification society, trading area, claims experience and management quality. Unlike most other insurance classes, hull underwriting retains significant individual judgement — there is no standard actuarial model.

🧮 Hull Premium Formula — H&M (Code T)

Annual Premium = Insured Value × Base Rate × Age Factor × Type Factor × Class Factor × Trading Area Factor × Claims Factor

Base Rate: market benchmark rate for the vessel segment (typically 0.20–0.80% for standard tonnage) EST

All adjustments are multiplicative. Final rate is subject to market conditions and underwriter judgement. Lloyd's hull underwriters typically quote on a rate-on-line (ROL) basis — annual premium as a % of hull insured value.

Classification Society Assessment

The classification society's role is to establish and maintain technical standards for hull structural integrity and machinery. Classification is a prerequisite for hull insurance — most Lloyd's underwriters will not quote for unclassed vessels or vessels classed with non-IACS societies. VER

Builders' Risk (B) — Special Considerations

Builders' risk (code B) follows the vessel from keel-laying through sea trials and delivery. It is rated on the contract price of the vessel (not market value) and typically placed at the shipyard's location. Key underwriting factors differ from operational hull:

🏗️ Shipyard Quality

Yard track record, capacity utilisation, workforce experience, quality control systems, financial stability of builder. Korean, Japanese and Chinese mega-yards (DSME, HHI, CSSC) are preferred; smaller or financially stressed yards attract loading. EST

⚓ Vessel Complexity

Standard bulk carrier at an established yard — low builders' risk rate. LNG carrier, floating storage unit (FSRU), naval vessel, or large cruise ship — significantly higher rate. LNG membrane tank installation is a specialist builders' risk exposure. EST

📅 Construction Period

Longer construction = greater cumulative exposure. A VLCC may take 24–30 months; a large cruise ship 36–48 months. Rate is applied to the value at risk (accumulated cost of construction), which increases through the build period. Monthly reporting of accumulated value required. EST

🌊 Launching & Sea Trials

Launching (float-out from dry dock) and sea trials represent the highest-risk phases of the builders' risk period. Full machinery and navigation systems are operational for the first time under real conditions. Premium rate often stepped up for these phases by endorsement. EST
