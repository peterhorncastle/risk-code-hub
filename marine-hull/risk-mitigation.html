ISM Code & Safety Management

The International Safety Management (ISM) Code is the foundation of modern ship safety governance. Made mandatory under SOLAS Chapter IX, it requires every shipping company to develop, implement and maintain a Safety Management System (SMS). VER Non-compliance with ISM can void hull cover — it is the single most important underwriting prerequisite.

📋 Safety Management System (SMS)

  • Documented procedures for all routine operations and emergency situations
  • Company Safety Policy signed by senior management
  • Roles and responsibilities clearly defined for all shipboard and shore staff
  • Designated Person Ashore (DPA) — direct link between master and shore management
  • Internal audits and management reviews at defined intervals
  • Incident reporting and near-miss analysis culture

🔖 ISM Certificates & Documentation

  • Document of Compliance (DOC): Issued to the company by flag state — confirms SMS meets ISM Code requirements. VER
  • Safety Management Certificate (SMC): Issued to each vessel — confirms the vessel's SMS is operating effectively. VER
  • DOC and SMC must be current for hull cover to be valid — underwriters check on renewal
  • DOC valid 5 years with annual verification; SMC valid 5 years with intermediate audit
  • Expired DOC/SMC = immediate breach of hull policy condition in most standard wordings

⚠️ Common ISM Deficiencies (PSC)

  • Failure to conduct required drills (fire, abandon ship, SOPEP)
  • Certificates not current (IOPP, SMC, DOC, Certificate of Fitness)
  • Machinery in poor condition — boiler, steering, bilge pumps
  • Inadequate emergency procedures documentation
  • Crew not familiar with SMS procedures
  • Incident/near-miss reporting not functioning
  • Bridge equipment deficiencies (GMDSS, AIS, ECDIS)

🌍 Tanker Management & Self-Assessment (TMSA)

  • OCIMF TMSA — voluntary self-assessment programme for tanker operators VER
  • TMSA 3 (current edition) — 13 KPIs covering SMS, crew management, navigation, cargo, environmental, emergency response, incident investigation
  • TMSA score shared with oil major customers (Shell, BP, ExxonMobil) — low scores result in vessel rejection for cargo lifting
  • Underwriters use TMSA scores as a positive indicator of management quality for tanker fleets
  • SIRE 2.0 (Ship Inspection Report Programme) — detailed vessel inspection regime for tankers VER

Classification Society Surveys & Hull Integrity

🔍 Annual Surveys

  • Annual survey by class society — confirms vessel remains in class
  • Checks safety equipment, certificates, machinery, fire detection/suppression
  • Statutory certificates verified (IOPP, SafetyConstruction, SafetyEquipment, Load Line, Tonnage)
  • Deficiencies issued as conditions of class — must be rectified within set timescales
  • Failure to maintain class = breach of hull cover / P&I cover

🔧 Special Survey (5-year Dry-Dock)

  • Full dry-docking survey every 5 years — enhanced structural inspection
  • Hull plating thickness measurements (ultrasonic gauging) — renewal if below class minima
  • Full inspection of propeller, rudder, sea chests, bilge keels
  • Machinery overhaul: engine, boiler, steering gear, windlass
  • Special survey overdue = class suspended = hull cover suspended
  • Underwriters check special survey due dates on renewal — approach with caution if within 12 months

⚓ Enhanced Survey Programme (ESP)

  • Enhanced Survey Programme applies to tankers and bulk carriers over 5 years old VER
  • More intensive corrosion and structural checks focused on historically vulnerable areas (holds, water ballast tanks, void spaces)
  • Introduced after a series of structural failures in ageing tankers and bulk carriers (Derbyshire 1980, Bulk Carrier Report 1990s)
  • ESP records available to underwriters on request

🏗️ Builders' Risk Survey Programme

  • Class society attends shipyard throughout construction — stage surveys at keel-laying, launch, trials
  • Welding inspection: certified welders, NDT (non-destructive testing) of critical welds
  • Structural steel quality and grade verification
  • Machinery factory acceptance tests (FATs) for main engine, generators, auxiliaries
  • Launching survey — checks buoyancy, trim, stability at launch
  • Sea trials: speed, manoeuvring, engine performance, fire/safety systems

Port State Control (PSC) — Understanding the Inspection Regime

Port State Control inspections are conducted by the coastal state authorities when a foreign vessel visits its ports. PSC records are public and are routinely reviewed by hull underwriters as a proxy for management quality and vessel condition. VER

🌐 PSC Regimes

  • Paris MOU: Europe and N. Atlantic — 27 member states; White/Grey/Black List of flag states based on detention rates VER
  • Tokyo MOU: Asia-Pacific — 21 member states
  • US Coast Guard (USCG): US ports — maintains 3-year vessel targeting database; QUALSHIP 21 excellent programme
  • Indian Ocean MOU, Black Sea MOU, etc.: Regional PSC arrangements
  • All PSC inspection data publicly searchable via national maritime authority websites

🚩 Red Flags for Underwriters

  • Any detention in past 3 years (especially USCG or Paris MOU)
  • Flag state on Paris MOU Black List (consistently high detention rates)
  • USCG port ban — vessel denied entry to US ports
  • Repeat deficiencies in the same category (fire safety, GMDSS)
  • Crew certification deficiencies (STCW violations)
  • ISM/DOC deficiencies noted in PSC records

🔒 BIMCO Cyber Security Clauses — The Hull Cyber Interface

The NotPetya cyberattack on Maersk in June 2017 caused an estimated $300m+ in business losses. VER While Maersk had no direct hull physical damage, the incident exposed the vulnerability of maritime operations to cyber disruption. BIMCO (Baltic and International Maritime Council) subsequently developed standardised cyber security clauses for charter parties and insurance:

  • BIMCO Cyber Security Clause for Vessel Management Agreements (2019, revised 2023): Defines vessel operator's cyber security obligations — patch management, access controls, network segmentation, incident response. VER
  • Hull cyber coverage question: If a cyber attack causes physical damage to the vessel (e.g. disabling navigation systems leading to grounding, or SCADA attack on machinery), does the H&M policy respond? The answer depends on the wording: ITC 1983 does not expressly exclude cyber; IHC 2003 has more explicit provisions. Most modern placements now attach LMA5400 series cyber exclusion clauses.
  • LMA5400–5403 Cyber Exclusion Clauses (Lloyd's Market Association): Exclude losses arising from cyber operations — but carve back cover for physical damage caused by a cyber event when the cyber event is not the dominant cause. The interface between hull cyber and standalone cyber policies remains the most contested coverage question in marine in 2024.
  • IMO Resolution MSC-FAL.1/Circ.3 (2017, revised 2021): IMO guidelines on Maritime Cyber Risk Management — recommend integration of cyber risk management into ISM Code SMS. VER
  • ECDIS / AIS vulnerabilities: GPS spoofing (vessels shown at incorrect positions) and AIS manipulation incidents have been reported in the Persian Gulf and Black Sea — relevant to navigation warranties and trading area exclusions.

🛡️ Top 10 Actions — What Underwriters Look For in a Quality Submission

  1. Current ISM certificates (DOC & SMC): Provide copies at renewal — expired certificates are an immediate concern.
  2. Clean PSC record: Present the past 3 years' PSC inspection history. Zero detentions is the gold standard.
  3. Top-tier IACS classification: LR, DNV, BV, ABS, ClassNK. All surveys current and no outstanding conditions of class.
  4. Vessel condition report / survey: Recent condition survey (especially for older vessels) demonstrates proactive maintenance.
  5. 5-year claims history: All vessels in the fleet, not just the specific vessel(s) being insured.
  6. TMSA score (tankers): High TMSA 3 scores are a strong positive indicator for tanker operators.
  7. Cyber security posture: BIMCO clause compliance; IT/OT network segmentation; confirmation of no cyber incidents.
  8. Trading area compliance: Confirm trading within Institute Warranty Limits (IWL); any JWC listed area trading disclosed upfront.
  9. Voyage plan quality: Evidence of effective voyage planning procedures (especially for ice, restricted waters, high-risk areas).
  10. Fire safety programme: For RoRo/car carriers — EV vehicle fire protocols, CO2/high expansion foam systems, pre-loading screening procedure.
