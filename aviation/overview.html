Coverage Structure

H2 / H3 — Hull

Hull All Risks

Physical damage to the aircraft and its equipment — in-flight, taxiing, ground damage, fire, lightning, ingestion. Agreed value (not indemnity). Deductible typically $250k–$1m for airliners.

L2 / L3 — Liability

Third Party Liability

Bodily injury and property damage to persons and property on the ground. Combined single limit (CSL) per occurrence. Critical for collision with buildings, vehicles, or persons during takeoff/landing incidents.

L2 / L3 — Liability

Passenger Liability

Bodily injury and death to fare-paying passengers. Montreal Convention 1999 governs international carriage — unlimited liability for proven negligence above SDR 128,821. No-fault presumed liability below this threshold.

AO — Products

Aviation Products Liability

Manufacturer liability for defective aircraft, engines, avionics, and components. Includes grounding liability (loss of use of aircraft withdrawn from service following a defect). 737 MAX grounding is the modern benchmark loss.

AP — Airport

Airport & Ground Handler Liability

Third party BI/PD arising from airport operations, fuelling, baggage handling, ground support equipment, snow removal errors. Hangarkeeper's legal liability covers damage to aircraft in the insured's care.

Separate Market

Aviation War Risk

Hull war, liability war and allied perils (terrorism, hijacking, confiscation, strikes, malicious damage). AVN52E excludes war from standard policy. Separate war market — cancellable on 7 days notice. Post-MH17 and post-9/11 a critical coverage gap if not bought.

⚠ Aviation War Risk — 7-Day Cancellation Clause (AVN52E)

Standard hull and liability policies incorporate the AVN52E war, hijacking and other perils exclusion clause. War risk coverage is bought separately in the specialist London war risk market. This coverage is cancellable by underwriters on 7 days notice — meaning in the event of conflict escalation (e.g. Red Sea, Ukraine/Russia), war risk cover can be withdrawn with minimal notice, leaving airlines and their lessors suddenly uninsured for hull war. Airlines operating in or over conflict zones must monitor war risk availability continuously.

Why Aviation Is Lloyd's Oldest Specialist Class

Aviation insurance at Lloyd's predates the first commercial airline. Lloyd's underwrote its first aircraft policy in 1911 for Bleriot's cross-Channel flight. The class grew through the interwar era, the jet age, and the satellite age to become one of the most technically demanding in the market. Today a single major airline may have a fleet valued at $15bn+ and carry 100,000 passengers a day — requiring CSL liability limits of $1.5–2bn per occurrence on a single policy.

The class is characterised by low frequency but extreme severity. A single catastrophic accident involving a wide-body aircraft can generate hull + liability losses exceeding $1bn. The 9/11 attacks produced the single largest aviation loss in history ($3.5bn+), triggering a market restructuring that still shapes pricing today. The Boeing 737 MAX crisis (2018–2020) demonstrated that aviation products liability can exceed $20bn across grounding, third party claims, and compensation settlements.

Despite being a high-severity class, aviation has one of the best long-term safety records of any transport mode — IATA reports 0.07 fatal accidents per million departures in 2023, the safest year on record. This paradox (low frequency, catastrophic severity) makes aviation a challenging class to price and requires specialised capacity backed by deep technical expertise.
