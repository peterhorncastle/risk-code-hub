Key Combined Ratio Drivers in Aviation

Loss ratio volatility: Aviation is characterised by low frequency / very high severity. In a benign year (e.g., 2017), the global market loss ratio may be 40–55%. A single major event (9/11 2001, MH370 + MH17 2014) can push the annual loss ratio to 120–200%+. This binary-outcome profile means capital allocation and reserving discipline are critical.

Expense ratio: Aviation is a specialist market with high acquisition costs (broking fees 10–15%, Lloyd's expenses ~25–30%). Total expense ratio typically 35–42%. The combined ratio must absorb both expenses and losses — ROEs only become attractive when loss ratios are in the 50–65% range.

Long-tail liability: Unlike hull (relatively quick settlement), L2 liability claims from major accidents can take 5–10 years to settle. MH17 claims are still being litigated in Dutch and international courts (2026). Adequate IBNR reserving is essential — some aviation syndicates carry 3–5 year reserve tails.

Bear Scenario Context — What Does 135% Loss Ratio Look Like?

A 135% net loss ratio on a $500m GWP portfolio is approximately the magnitude of a 9/11-equivalent event hitting the portfolio proportionally. In absolute terms, the London aviation market absorbed c.$3.5bn in total claims from 9/11 against a then-total GWP of approximately $5–6bn — producing a combined ratio well above 200% for the year. The 2022 Russia leasing dispute threatens a similar systemic loss outcome, with $8bn+ in claims against a global GWP of only $6bn. The bear scenario illustrates why aviation underwriters must maintain very significant capital buffers and purchase outward reinsurance aggressively.

IBNR reserving note: Aviation liability losses develop slowly — a major crash in Year 1 may not be fully settled until Year 8–10. Reserve development risk is a significant feature of aviation P&L. Syndicates writing aviation must carry substantial IBNR reserves, which depress short-term reported ROE even in good trading years.

⚠ Russia Leasing Dispute — Systemic Threat to Aviation Market Returns

The $8bn+ in H2 hull claims filed by aircraft lessors following Russia's retention of leased aircraft (2022) represents approximately 1.3× the global aviation market GWP. If claims are upheld in full, several syndicates and companies face solvency risk. The market disputes coverage on grounds that aircraft are not "lost" (they exist but cannot be retrieved). English courts and LCIA arbitrations are ongoing as of April 2026. A significant adverse ruling could produce the largest single-year aviation loss in market history — exceeding 9/11 in absolute dollar terms. Underwriters are now scrutinising leasing programme structures, jurisdiction of lessees, and sanctions exposure at every renewal.
