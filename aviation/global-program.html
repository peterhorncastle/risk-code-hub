Aviation Programme Architecture Overview

Aviation global programmes differ from most other commercial lines in three key ways: (1) Combined Single Limit (CSL) rather than split per-claimant/per-occurrence structures; (2) war risk placed separately with a distinct market on 7-day cancellable terms; and (3) mandatory admitted local policies in many jurisdictions under EU Regulation 785/2004 and ICAO Annex 6 operator certificate requirements. Understanding these structural features is essential before constructing a programme tower.

Programme Tower — Major Network Airline (International, $2bn CSL)

Illustrative structure for a major international airline with 120 aircraft ($9bn fleet value), operating to 80 countries including US, EU, Middle East, and Asia-Pacific.

Self-Insured Retention
$5m hull SIR + $2m liability SIR per occurrence Retained by airline. Hull SIR: attritional ground damage, minor structural repairs. Liability SIR: small baggage/personal injury claims. Captive insurer often used to hold SIR.
Primary Aviation Layer
H2 Hull $9bn agreed value + L2 CSL $500m xs $2m SIR Lead Lloyd's syndicate(s) + London market co-insurers. Hull all-risks physical damage. Liability covers passengers, third parties, cargo. Primary layer sets the terms — all excess layers follow form.
Excess Layer 1
$500m xs $500m (liability only) London and international (Zurich, Munich Re, Hannover Re) market. Follows primary form. Activated in mid-size catastrophic events — full hull loss + significant passenger death + some third-party ground damage.
Excess Layer 2
$500m xs $1bn Broader reinsurance market participation. Lockerbie (1988, c.$1bn total) would have reached into this layer. Post-9/11, airlines in this layer found capacity very tight — government backstops covered the gap.
Excess Layer 3
$500m xs $1.5bn → Total CSL $2bn Top-of-tower capacity. Only the most severe multi-aircraft or ground impact events (9/11-equivalent) would exhaust a $2bn CSL. Some airlines buy $2.5bn CSL, particularly US domestic carriers facing US tort system exposure.
War Risk (Separate Placement)
AVN52H — Hull + Liability war risk. Typically $1.5bn–$2bn. 7-day cancellation. Placed with London aviation war risk market (specialist underwriters, Pool participants). Covers: war, terrorism, sabotage, confiscation, political interference. AVN52E cancellation clause — market can withdraw in 7 days in elevated conflict periods. MH17, Lockerbie, 9/11 — all war risk claims.
AO Products (if applicable)
AO aggregate limit $500m–$2bn (fleet operators with MRO programmes) AO placed for operators running MRO facilities or with approved ground handling operations. Covers products defect liability. Grounding sublimit (e.g., $100m) standard. Boeing/Airbus tier buy $5bn–$15bn AO separately as OEMs.

Jurisdiction Profiles — Key Markets for Admitted Insurance

Unlike most commercial lines where freedom-of-services placement is broad, aviation has specific admitted insurance requirements driven by EU Regulation 785/2004 (minimum liability limits for all operators to/from EU) and ICAO Annex 6 operator certificate requirements. Fronting arrangements are required in many jurisdictions.

War Risk — Programme Structure

War risk is the single biggest gap in standard aviation policies and the structuring element most often misunderstood by non-specialist brokers. The AVN52H war risk policy (hull + liability) sits alongside — not within — the main H2/L2 programme and is placed with a completely separate market. The 7-day cancellation clause (AVN52E) is the defining feature: the market can withdraw cover at 7 days' notice, leaving airlines exposed if a conflict escalates rapidly.

AVN52E — 7-Day Cancellation: How It Works in Practice

The clause: Underwriters can cancel war risk cover on 7 days' written notice, regardless of the policy period remaining. This exists because war risk loss events (military shoot-downs, terrorist attacks) can materialise without warning and accumulate catastrophically.

2001 precedent: After 9/11, the war risk market cancelled immediately (some gave less than the required 7 days). US, UK, EU, and Australian governments stepped in within 24–48 hours to provide temporary government reinsurance backstops allowing airlines to continue operating. The US Air Transportation Stabilisation Board (ATSB) provided $100bn in government-backed war risk cover.

2022 Ukraine: Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, the war risk market dramatically reduced capacity for operators with Ukrainian, Russian, or Russian-routing exposure within days. Airlines operating into Ukrainian airspace had cover cancelled. The 7-day clause exposed airlines to a brief period of uninsured war risk exposure.

Airline obligations: AOC (Airline Operating Certificate) holders must maintain continuous insurance. Airlines with routes near conflict zones need contingency plans for rapid war risk cover withdrawal — including route suspension, alternative routings, and government backstop activation procedures.

Case Study: Emirates — Major Network Airline Programme

Case Study

Emirates Airlines — Global Programme Architecture (Illustrative)

Emirates is the world's largest international airline by passengers carried (58.5m in FY2024), operating a fleet of approximately 260 wide-body aircraft (A380, B777 family) to 140 destinations in 80 countries. Its insurance programme represents the top tier of global aviation insurance complexity.

Fleet profile: ~260 aircraft (A380: 113, B777 variants: ~150). Average fleet value approximately $100m per aircraft (mixed new delivery / mid-life). Total agreed fleet value approximately $26bn.

H2 Hull: Largest fleet hull value in the global market. Hull programme led by London market with significant participation from Munich Re, Zurich, and specialist aviation markets. ROL estimated at approximately 0.22–0.28% on a programme of this quality (IOSA registered, excellent safety record, modern fleet). Hull premium estimate $57m–$73m per annum.

L2 Liability: CSL approximately $2.0bn–$2.5bn given US route network, transatlantic operations, and exposure to US tort jurisdiction. CSL premium reflects volume of seat departures (260 aircraft × 365 days × ~1.2 flights/aircraft = ~114,000 departures/yr × 400 seats avg = 45.5m seat departures). L2 premium estimate $22m–$35m per annum.

War risk (AVN52H): Critical given route network — Dubai hub is geographically proximate to Iran, Yemen, and Iraq conflict zones. AVN52E cancellation clause exposure is significant. Emirates maintains relationship with the London war risk market and monitors NOTAM advisories continuously. War risk premium: estimated $6m–$12m per annum.

Programme total: Estimated $85m–$120m total aviation programme cost per annum. Exactly comparable to premium levels of a major Nat-CAT property risk programme for a large oil major — illustrating the scale of the global aviation insurance market relative to premium volume.

Leasing exposure: Emirates' fleet is approximately 50% owned, 50% leased. Lessors (AerCap, Air Lease Corp, SMBC Aviation Capital) all named as additional insureds on the hull programme with AVLA endorsements. The Russia leasing dispute (2022) affected Emirates' lessor relationships — some lessors have UAE-registered aircraft operating in Emirates' fleet that have become subject to sanctions review.

⚠ Russia Leasing Dispute — Programme Structuring Lesson

The 2022 Russia aircraft retention dispute has produced the most important aviation programme structuring lesson in decades. Approximately 400–500 Western-owned aircraft worth $10bn+ were seized by Russian operators following sanctions. Lessors filed H2 hull claims totalling $8bn+. Aviation insurers are contesting coverage on the grounds that the aircraft are not physically "lost" — they exist but cannot be recovered. The dispute turns on the definition of "confiscation" and "requisition" under the war risk policy (AVN52H) vs the all-risks hull policy (H2). The lesson for programme structuring: (1) War risk and all-risks hull policies must both be reviewed for conflict zone aircraft exposure. (2) Lessor additional insured endorsements must be unambiguous. (3) Sanctions clauses in aviation policies are now heavily negotiated. (4) Operators with leasing portfolios exposed to sanctioned territories must review both insurance adequacy and sanctions compliance simultaneously.
