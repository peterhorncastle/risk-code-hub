Aviation Underwriting

Coverage Structure by Lloyd's Code

Aviation at Lloyd's is divided into six active risk codes covering the full spectrum from commercial airline hull through to aerospace products liability. Each code has a distinct rating approach, market, and coverage trigger.

CodeClassCoverage TriggerBasisKey Exclusions (standard)
H2 Airline Hull — All Risks Physical damage to aircraft operated under AOC (airline operating certificate). Covers ground, taxi, flight, ingestion, crash, fire, theft. Agreed value — insured/insurer agree hull value at inception. No depreciation argument on total loss. Wear & tear; mechanical breakdown (separate breakdown policy); war risk (separate AVN52H market); nuclear; unlawful seizure by crew/passengers
H3 General Aviation Hull Physical damage to non-AOC aircraft: corporate jets, helicopters, turboprops, piston GA, drones (UAV). Same all-risks basis as H2. Agreed value or market value depending on aircraft age. AVAC (Agreed Value Aircraft Clause) standard. As H2 plus: student pilot restriction; territory exclusion common; navigational limits clause (AVN1)
L2 Airline Liability — CSL Third-party bodily injury and property damage; passenger injury/death (Montreal Convention); cargo liability; mail liability; consequential losses to third parties from accidents. Combined Single Limit (CSL) — one limit covers all third-party claims arising from one occurrence. Airlines buy $1.5bn–$2.5bn CSL standard. War risk (AVN52H / government-backed pools); intentional acts by crew; nuclear; unlawful cargo; contractual liability beyond convention limits
L3 GA / Corporate Liability Same trigger as L2 but for non-airline operators. Passenger liability, third-party property damage, hangarkeepers liability, grounding liability. CSL or split limits (passenger / property). GA operators typically $20m–$500m CSL depending on aircraft type. As L2; charter operations often require endorsement; training exclusion standard for student pilots
AO Aviation Products Liability Bodily injury / property damage arising from design, manufacture, assembly, installation or servicing of aircraft, engines, components or equipment supplied by aerospace manufacturers, MRO facilities, parts suppliers. Claims-made basis. Products aggregate limit. Exposure rated on fleet-in-service, aircraft deliveries, part criticality (flight-critical vs ground-support). Grounding losses sublimited; recall / withdrawal costs sublimited or excluded; war; nuclear; employers liability; property in care/custody/control
AP Airport Owners & Operators Third-party BI & PD arising from airport operations: runway incursions, ground handling, refuelling, snow removal, ATC provision (if not government), terminal operations, fire service response failures. Claims-made or occurrence. CSL or split limits. Premium based on annual passenger throughput and airport category (international / domestic / GA). Aircraft operation exclusion (AP covers ground side only); war; air traffic control professional liability (separate policy); environmental pollution

Hull Rating Methodology (H2 / H3)

Aviation hull is rated on a rate on line (ROL) basis — a percentage applied to the agreed fleet value. The ROL reflects fleet composition, operator quality, route network, and loss history. Airlines buy on a fleet basis; GA insureds buy aircraft by aircraft.

H2 / H3 Hull Premium Formula
Hull Premium = (Total Fleet Agreed Value) × ROL%

Where: ROL% = Base Rate × Fleet Factor × Route Factor × Age Factor × Loss Loading

Base Rate range = 0.25% – 1.50% of fleet value per annum (wide-body scheduled airlines at lower end; piston GA at upper end)
Example: A320 fleet of 40 aircraft, agreed value $80m per aircraft ($3.2bn total) at ROL 0.30% = $9.6m hull premium. Individual aircraft excesses typically $500k–$2m per occurrence for airline hull.

Fleet Composition & Type High Impact

Wide-body jets (777, 787, A350) trade at tighter ROLs than narrow-body (737/A320) due to better loss record per departure. Regional jets and turboprops attract higher rates; piston GA highest of all. Helicopter hull is rated separately with higher base rates (0.8%–3.0%) due to rotor system exposure.

Fleet Age & Ageing Aircraft High Impact

Aircraft over 15 years attract age loading (+10–25%). Post-20 years: +25–50%. Ageing aircraft programmes (CPCP) and corrosion findings are key underwriting questions. Aircraft near end of life with heavy operator maintenance oversight may be rated more favourably than younger aircraft with weak MRO programmes.

Operator Safety Record — Loss History High Impact

3–5 year claims experience is primary modifying factor. Airlines with IOSA registration (IATA Operational Safety Audit) receive rate credit (typically 5–15%). Operators on the EU Air Safety List (banned from EU airspace) face significant load or declination. IOSHA certification viewed positively.

Route Network & Operating Environment Medium Impact

Routes traversing conflict zones (Ukraine/Russia, Middle East, sub-Saharan Africa) incur war risk loading or require separate AVN52H war hull policy. High-altitude mountainous airports (Kathmandu, Lukla, Innsbruck) attract CFIT surcharge. Night operations at challenging airfields rated individually.

Maintenance Programme & MRO Quality Medium Impact

EASA Part 145 or FAA 14 CFR Part 145 approved MRO is baseline expectation. Heavy maintenance intervals, corrosion protection programmes, and OEM service bulletin compliance all reviewed. Third-party MRO vs in-house is a factor — strong in-house programmes (Lufthansa Technik, Air France Industries standard) viewed positively.

Deductibles & Retention Structure Rate Credit

Standard airline hull excess: $500k–$2m per occurrence. Large airlines often take $5m–$20m retention on hull to reduce premium. Higher retentions = meaningful rate credit. GA operators: $5,000–$50,000 depending on aircraft value. In-flight deductible vs all-risk deductible structure matters.

Liability Rating Methodology (L2 / L3)

Airline liability (L2) is rated on a per seat per departure basis modified for CSL level, route type, and operator quality. The rating produces an annual premium covering all third-party liability exposures arising from aircraft operations.

L2 Airline Liability — Rating Formula
Liability Premium = (Seat Departures × Rate per Seat Departure) × CSL Factor

Where: Seat Departures = Fleet seats × Annual departures per aircraft
Rate per Seat Departure = $0.015 – $0.08 (varies by operator, route, safety record)
CSL Factor = scaling factor for limit purchased (1.0 at $1bn; 1.25 at $1.5bn; 1.5 at $2bn+)
Montreal Convention 1999 requires minimum passenger liability of ~SDR 128,821 per passenger (~$175,000 USD). Practical commercial pressures mean airlines carry $500m–$2.5bn CSL. Post-9/11: US domestic carriers typically $1.5bn CSL minimum; international carriers $2bn+.

CSL Limit Purchased High Impact

Combined Single Limit drives premium significantly. A320 operator with 150-seat aircraft at $1bn CSL vs $2bn CSL sees ~35–45% premium increase for the additional $1bn of limit. Catastrophic events (full hull loss, passenger death + third-party ground damage) can exhaust $1bn limits — Lockerbie ultimately cost c.$1bn total.

Passenger Load Factor & Seat Configuration High Impact

Higher seat counts = higher per-departure exposure. All-economy 737-800 with 189 seats vs business-class configuration (165 seats) rated differently. Seat departures is the primary exposure metric. A $200m wide-body accident with 300 passengers on board exhausts more limit than a regional jet with 50 passengers.

Route Type — Domestic vs International Medium Impact

US domestic routes attract US tort jurisdiction risk (higher jury awards). International routes subject to Montreal Convention (two-tier liability: strict to SDR 128,821; fault-based above). Middle Eastern and African routes attract additional country/political risk loading. European intra-EU routes: EC 785/2004 mandatory insurance applies.

Cargo Liability Lower Impact

Montreal Convention limits cargo liability to SDR 22 per kg (c.$30/kg). Most airlines carry cargo liability within CSL. Freighter operators and combi aircraft require careful review — DG (dangerous goods) carriage, hazmat, lithium battery cargo attracts specific underwriting questions. IATA DGR compliance is baseline.

Grounding Liability Medium Impact

737 MAX grounding (20 months, 2019–2020) created a new underwriting focus on systemic fleet grounding liability. L2 policies now routinely address grounding exposure — financial loss to airlines from regulatory grounding. Boeing AO policy absorbed the manufacturer's indemnity exposure. Sublimits of $100m–$250m per grounding event are standard on AO.

War Risk — Separate Market (AVN52H) High Impact

Standard L2/H2 policies exclude war risk per AVN48B/52D. War risk hull and liability placed separately with the London aviation war risk market (Pool and XL reinsurance). War risk premium quoted as ROL separately. 7-day cancellation clause (AVN52E) means war risk market can withdraw cover at short notice — airlines must maintain government backstop awareness.

Aviation Products Liability Rating (AO)

AO is the most technically complex aviation line. It covers aerospace manufacturers (Boeing, Airbus, Rolls-Royce, Pratt & Whitney, Collins Aerospace, Safran) and MRO/maintenance organisations against claims arising from defective products incorporated into aircraft. Rated on a products exposure basis.

AO Products Liability — Exposure Rating Approach
AO Premium = (Fleet-in-Service Exposure + Deliveries Exposure) × Rate × Grounding Sublimit Factor

Fleet-in-Service: Units in service × Aircraft value × risk factor (flight-critical = 1.5; avionics = 1.2; cabin = 0.5)
Deliveries: Annual new aircraft deliveries × delivery value × new-product loading
737 MAX MCAS failure (2018–2019) is the defining modern AO loss. Boeing's total AO exposure from two crashes + grounding exceeded $20bn — illustrating why OEM AO limits of $5bn–$15bn are now considered baseline for large manufacturers. MRO organisations carry $100m–$2bn AO depending on scope of operations.

Airport Liability Rating (AP)

AP covers airports and ground handlers against third-party claims arising from airport operations — runway incursions, refuelling errors, jetway incidents, snow clearance failures, fire service response failures, and ground vehicle accidents on the airfield.

AP Rating Parameters

Annual passenger throughput: Primary exposure metric. International hub airports (Heathrow 80m pax/yr) vs regional airports (5m pax/yr) priced very differently. Rate per 1,000 passengers is a common starting metric.

Cargo volume: Cargo operations (DHL, FedEx hubs) carry additional products-in-care exposure — damaged or lost cargo is an AP trigger at cargo handling facilities.

Ground handling scope: Airports providing own ground handling (fuelling, pushback, baggage) carry broader AP exposure than airports that license third-party handlers. Third-party handlers should carry their own GL policy with AP insured as additional insured.

CSL level: International airports: $1bn–$2bn CSL. Regional airports: $250m–$750m CSL. Major cargo hubs with dangerous goods handling: bespoke sublimit schedule.

⚠ Russia Aircraft Leasing Dispute — Underwriting Implications (2022–Present)

Following 2022 sanctions, 400–500 Western-owned leased aircraft ($10bn+ hull value) were retained in Russia. Lessors filed claims with H2 hull insurers for total loss. Insurers argue aircraft are not lost — they exist but cannot be repossessed. The dispute (currently through English courts and international arbitration) is the largest unresolved aviation insurance claim in history and is reshaping how leased aircraft hull policies are worded. Underwriters now ask specifically about leasing structures, lessee jurisdiction, and sanctions exposure at renewal.
