Module 07

Global Insurance Program Structuring

How to design and place a multinational product recall insurance programme covering multiple production sites, cross-border distribution, and claims scenarios ranging from $10M through to $100M and above.

Home History Timeline Recall Database Underwriting Rates & ROE Risk Mitigation Global Programs

Structuring a global product recall insurance programme for a multinational food and beverage company requires careful coordination across regulatory environments, insurance market capacity constraints, local admitted policy requirements, and claim jurisdiction challenges. A poorly structured programme can leave significant gaps in coverage or create costly double-insurance scenarios. This module walks through the principles of global programme construction and illustrates programme structures at four key limit scenarios: $10M, $25M, $50M, and $100M+.

1. The Challenge of Multinational Product Recall Exposure

A global food manufacturer faces recall exposure across multiple dimensions simultaneously:

  • Multi-jurisdictional regulatory regimes — an FDA-mandated recall in the US follows different procedures and costs than an EFSA/RASFF-triggered recall in the EU or a FSANZ recall in Australia
  • Multi-currency recall costs — logistics, personnel, waste disposal, and customer credits are denominated in local currencies; a single global limit in USD can understate exposure in high-cost jurisdictions
  • Local admitted insurance requirements — some countries (e.g., Brazil, Argentina, China, Saudi Arabia) require insurance to be placed locally with admitted carriers; a master policy issued offshore may not be legally enforceable
  • Distribution lag and product in transit — at any given moment, a global food manufacturer may have product in transit, in customs, in distributor warehouses, and on retail shelves across dozens of countries simultaneously
  • Supply chain aggregation — a single contaminated ingredient supplier may trigger simultaneous recalls across multiple markets and multiple finished product brands
  • Varying product liability regimes — third-party product liability claims arising from a recall event are handled under local tort and product liability law, which varies significantly by jurisdiction

2. Programme Architecture: The Master Policy + Local Policy Structure

The standard approach for large multinational insureds is a Master Policy + Local Policy structure, sometimes referred to as a "Controlled Master Programme" (CMP). This provides consistent coverage globally while satisfying local regulatory requirements.

3. Layer Structure: The Insurance Tower

For significant limit requirements ($25M+), a layered "tower" programme distributes risk across multiple insurers, each taking a defined layer of exposure. This provides capacity beyond what any single insurer can offer and allows pricing to reflect the reduced probability of loss at higher layers.

Illustrative $100M Programme Tower
Catastrophe / Aggregate XS Layer 4

$25M xs $75M  ·  Market: Lloyd's syndicates / specialist capacity

Accessed only in a major catastrophic event. Rate on line (ROL): 1.5–3%. Aggregate protection for extraordinary scenarios. Multiple insurers typically sharing this layer.

3rd Excess Layer — Layer 3

$25M xs $50M  ·  Market: Lloyd's / AXA XL / Zurich / Beazley

Second excess layer. Accessed in severe events only. ROL typically 3–6%. Capacity at this level requires strong underwriting relationships and clear programme architecture.

2nd Excess Layer — Layer 2

$25M xs $25M  ·  Market: AXA XL / Zurich / Starr / Beazley

First excess layer. Accessed in significant events. ROL typically 6–12%. Usually 3–5 co-insurers sharing capacity at this level.

1st Excess Layer — Layer 1

$15M xs $10M  ·  Market: AXA XL / Crum & Forster / Lloyd's

First follow layer above the primary. Priced relative to the primary; ROL typically 10–18%. Lead underwriter terms typically "follow form" to primary on key coverage provisions.

Primary Layer

$10M Limit  ·  Lead Market: Lloyd's / AXA XL / Zurich

The foundation of the programme. Sets coverage terms, triggers, deductible, and sub-limits. Lead underwriter drives the underwriting assessment. Most attritional losses are absorbed here. Premium typically represents 50–65% of total programme cost.

Self-Insured Retention (SIR)

$250K – $2M (insured's own deductible)

The insured absorbs losses within the SIR before insurance responds. Higher SIR = lower premium. For a $100M programme a well-rated insured may carry $500K–$1M SIR.

5. Country-by-Country Considerations

Key Regulatory Jurisdictions for Admitted Coverage

🇺🇸
United States Non-admitted (surplus lines) placement permissible in most states for specialty lines including product recall. Lloyd's and offshore carriers can write directly via surplus lines brokers. However, large US exposures are best placed on admitted paper (through fronting) for maximum legal enforceability. FSMA compliance required; FDA/USDA regulatory environment is the most active globally for recall enforcement.
🇬🇧 🇪🇺
UK / European Union Lloyd's of London is the dominant global market for large and complex product recall placements. Post-Brexit, UK and EU policies are separate. Within the EU, locally admitted policies are generally required for each member state or a cross-border EU passport policy may be available through a licensed EU carrier. RASFF (Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed) drives EU recall notifications.
🇧🇷
Brazil Compulsory admitted insurance requirement. Non-admitted placements are not permitted. Requires a locally licensed Brazilian insurer to issue policy. Fronting arrangements via MAPFRE, Porto Seguro, or Zurich Brazil are typical. SUSEP (insurance regulator) strictly enforces admitted requirements. Premium tax and IOF (financial operations tax) apply.
🇨🇳
China Non-admitted insurance is generally prohibited. Locally issued policies from PICC, Ping An, China Pacific, or licensed foreign JV carriers required. FINC (Financial Interest Cover) can be used under the master policy for losses where local admitted coverage is insufficient. Cross-border reinsurance is permitted, allowing risk to flow back to the global programme.
🇮🇳
India IRDAI (Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India) requires admitted policies for Indian risks. Product recall cover is available from Indian insurers (New India Assurance, ICICI Lombard, HDFC Ergo) but terms may be narrower than global master. DIC from master policy covers the gap in Indian policy terms. Growing food safety regulatory environment (FSSAI) increasing recall frequency.
🇦🇺 🇳🇿
Australia / New Zealand Non-admitted (cross-border) placements permissible under Australia's foreign financial services framework for wholesale (large) insureds. Lloyd's and offshore carriers can write Australian risks directly. APRA-regulated admitted carriers (Allianz AU, QBE, Zurich AU) available for those preferring admitted paper. Food Standards Australia New Zealand (FSANZ) joint recall regime.
🇸🇦 🇦🇪
Middle East (KSA / UAE) Saudi Arabia requires admitted insurance (SAMA-supervised). UAE permits the DIFC and ADGM financial free zone insurers to write international risks. Local Takaful (Islamic insurance) options available in some markets. Product recall insurance penetration is low but growing rapidly as Gulf food safety regulation (Saudi SFDA; UAE ESMA) strengthens.

6. Captive Insurance Strategies

Very large food and beverage corporations frequently use a captive insurance company to retain part of their product recall exposure, particularly in the primary layer. A captive is a wholly-owned insurance subsidiary, typically domiciled in Bermuda, Cayman Islands, Guernsey, Vermont, or Dublin, that writes risk on behalf of its parent group.

  • Primary layer captive retention — the captive retains the first $5M–$10M of each recall loss, reducing premium paid to commercial insurers and retaining underwriting profit within the group in low-loss years
  • Captive fronting — the captive issues locally admitted policies in key markets (fronted by a licensed carrier) and reinsures the risk back to the global programme, streamlining the admitted network management
  • Group aggregate protection — captives can be used to manage aggregate annual recall losses across all subsidiaries globally, with the commercial market providing catastrophe protection above a defined group aggregate threshold
  • Premium tax efficiency — in some domiciles, premiums paid to a captive may receive more favourable tax treatment than premiums paid to third-party insurers; legal and tax advice is essential
  • Risk management incentive — a captive creates a direct financial incentive for subsidiaries to invest in loss prevention, since the captive's performance directly impacts group earnings
Captive Considerations: Captives are most cost-effective for groups with annual GWP of $2M+ in product recall premium. The costs of captive establishment (typically $50K–$150K) and ongoing management ($75K–$200K pa) must be weighed against premium savings and retained underwriting profit. Bermuda-domiciled captives writing US risks require careful analysis of US federal excise tax (FET) obligations.

8. Key Programme Design Principles

  • Set the limit relative to the Estimated Maximum Loss (EML), not just the average expected loss — model the worst credible scenario for your product and distribution footprint
  • Ensure the Business Interruption indemnity period is long enough — for large manufacturers requiring regulatory approval to restart, 12 months is often insufficient; 24–36 months is appropriate for complex or infant nutrition facilities
  • Check for sub-limits that could cap a real loss — a $50M programme with a $10M BI sub-limit does not provide $50M of real protection for a production closure scenario
  • Confirm the trigger wording covers your most likely recall scenario — voluntary recall, government-mandated recall, and allergen mislabelling triggers should all be explicitly included
  • Align the geographic scope with your actual distribution footprint — a policy limited to the US/Canada will not respond to losses in EU markets; confirm all territories where product is distributed are named or explicitly covered
  • Engage a specialist product recall broker — not all brokers have access to the full Lloyd's and company market recall capacity and specialist expertise. Firms with dedicated product recall teams (Marsh Specialty, Aon Crisis Management, WTW Food & Beverage, and specialist boutiques) will deliver meaningfully better programme design and market access
  • Test the programme annually — review coverage limits, sub-limits, and insured distribution territories annually; the programme should evolve as the business grows
  • Notify the insurer immediately when a potential recall scenario emerges — most policies require verbal notification within 72 hours; early engagement enables the insurer to provide crisis management support and preserves coverage

🌍 The Role of the Specialist Broker

For any product recall programme above $25M in limit, or for any multinational insured with operations in more than five countries, the appointment of a specialist product recall broker is essential. The specialist broker brings three critical capabilities that a generalist broker cannot replicate: (1) deep market relationships with the Lloyd's underwriters and company market capacity that writes this line; (2) global fronting network access enabling locally admitted policies to be placed efficiently in 20–40+ territories through a single point of coordination; and (3) claims expertise — when a recall occurs, the broker's crisis response team can mobilise insurer-approved crisis consultants, guide the insured through the notification and documentation process, and advocate for the insured's claim position with underwriters.

The difference between a well-structured programme placed by a specialist broker and an inadequately structured programme placed by a generalist can represent tens of millions of dollars in uncovered loss at the point of a major recall claim. For a food manufacturer, this is not a theoretical risk — it is a practical certainty that the programme will be tested eventually.
