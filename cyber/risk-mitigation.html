Cyber Risk Mitigation

The underwriter's framework for assessing an insured's security controls: MFA types and their relative strength, EDR/XDR deployment, backup architecture (3-2-1 rule), patch management cadence, privileged access management, and incident response planning. The quality of these controls directly drives rate, limit availability and coverage conditions.

The Three Tiers of Cyber Security Controls

Cyber underwriters broadly organise security controls into three tiers based on their demonstrated impact on ransomware and breach outcomes. Tier 1 controls are binary — their absence is typically a decline or major loading condition. Tier 2 controls inform rate. Tier 3 controls command credits but their absence alone rarely blocks coverage.

Tier 1 — Critical

Multi-Factor Authentication

Required on email, VPN/remote access, and privileged accounts. Absence = no cover or 2–3× rate loading. Phishing-resistant MFA (FIDO2) is gold standard post-2023.

Tier 1 — Critical

EDR on Endpoints

Active endpoint detection covering ≥90% of devices. Must be monitored (SOC or managed service). Unmonitored AV-only endpoints treated as equivalent to no EDR by most underwriters.

Tier 1 — Critical

Offline / Immutable Backups

At least one backup copy that ransomware cannot reach or encrypt. Air-gapped or immutable (write-once) storage. Untested backups are treated with significant scepticism — recovery failures are common.

Tier 2 — Important

Privileged Access Management

PAM tools controlling admin credentials (CyberArk, BeyondTrust, Delinea). JIT (just-in-time) privilege is best practice. Shared admin passwords without vaulting = significant loading.

Tier 2 — Important

Patch Management

Formalised process with SLA: critical CVEs ≤72hrs, high CVEs ≤14 days. Internet-facing systems patched first. Asset inventory required — you can't patch what you don't know exists.

Tier 2 — Important

Email Security (DMARC)

DMARC in enforce mode prevents domain spoofing BEC attacks. SPF + DKIM are minimum. Anti-phishing training with simulated tests within 12 months expected. SMS phishing increasingly targeted.

Tier 2 — Important

Network Segmentation

VLAN or micro-segmentation limits ransomware blast radius. OT/IT separation critical for manufacturers (flat network = entire factory down). Zero-trust architecture commands meaningful rate credit.

Tier 3 — Best Practice

Vulnerability Management / ASM

External attack surface management (Bitsight, SecurityScorecard, Tenable). Continuous scanning vs. point-in-time. Underwriters use external scans to validate application responses.

Tier 3 — Best Practice

Zero Trust Architecture

"Never trust, always verify" — assumes breach. Identity-based access, no implicit trust from network location. SASE/SSE frameworks. Early adoption; commands premium credit of 10–20%.

MFA: Type Matters

Not all MFA is equal. The rise of adversary-in-the-middle (AiTM) phishing toolkits (Evilginx, Modlishka) and SIM-swapping attacks has rendered SMS OTP and push notification MFA increasingly vulnerable. Underwriters at leading Lloyd's syndicates are now distinguishing between MFA types when assessing risk quality — phishing-resistant FIDO2 hardware keys command a credit that SMS MFA does not.

FIDO2 / Hardware Keys

Physical security keys (YubiKey, Google Titan). Cryptographic binding to the specific website prevents AiTM relay attacks. Cannot be phished. NIST 800-63B AAL3. Required by the most security-conscious underwriters for financial services accounts above £50m limit.

Strongest — Rate Credit

Authenticator App (TOTP)

Time-based one-time password (Google Authenticator, Microsoft Authenticator). Vulnerable to real-time phishing relay attacks but significantly better than SMS. Widely accepted as market-standard MFA by Lloyd's underwriters since 2021.

Strong — Market Standard

Push Notification MFA

Approval prompt sent to registered device (Duo, Okta Verify). Subject to "MFA fatigue" attacks — bombarding the user with approvals until they click accept. Microsoft's October 2022 breach used MFA fatigue. Now viewed cautiously without number matching enabled.

Moderate — With Number Matching

SMS One-Time Password

Code sent via text message. Vulnerable to SIM swapping, SS7 interception, and AiTM phishing. NIST deprecated SMS OTP in NIST SP 800-63B (2017). Still accepted by underwriters where higher-grade MFA cannot be deployed (e.g. field worker populations) but does not attract credit.

Weak — No Credit

Passkeys (FIDO2 Platform)

Device-bound passkeys (Touch ID, Windows Hello, Face ID). Phishing-resistant; no hardware key needed. Rapidly becoming the consumer-grade FIDO2 standard. Increasingly accepted for lower-limit risks. Not yet widely deployed in enterprise contexts (2024).

Strong — Growing Acceptance

No MFA

Single-factor authentication only. For email or remote access, this is a binary condition failure at most Lloyd's CY underwriters. For privileged accounts (domain admin, cloud root), absence of MFA is arguably the single highest-risk control gap in the market today.

Unacceptable — Decline / Loading

Backup Architecture: The 3-2-1 Rule

The 3-2-1 backup rule has been the industry standard since before ransomware — but ransomware stress-tested it comprehensively. The key vulnerability: if the backup system is connected to the production network, ransomware can encrypt the backups along with everything else. Immutability (write-once storage) and air-gapping are the two approaches that reliably defeat ransomware backup destruction.

3

Copies of Data

Production + 2 backups. At least one must survive the worst-case attack scenario.

2

Different Media Types

Separate physical or logical media — not just two folders on the same SAN. Cloud + tape is ideal.

1

Offsite (Immutable)

One copy must be unreachable by ransomware — air-gapped tape, immutable cloud storage (S3 Object Lock), or WORM media.

3-2-1 rule met with immutable/air-gapped copy●Ransomware BI exposure significantly reduced; full-limit ransomware available; rate credit.
Offsite backup, not immutable (e.g. cloud synced)●Moderate protection; cloud-synced backups may be accessible to attacker via cloud credentials. Ransomware sub-limit considered.
Local backup only (NAS/tape in same facility)●High BI risk — ransomware commonly destroys local backups. Sub-limit imposed; higher rate.

Incident Response Planning

An insured with a tested IR plan, a named IR retainer, and legal counsel on standby responds to a cyber incident materially faster than one that calls an underwriter for vendor recommendations. Speed of response directly correlates with ultimate loss size: each hour of uncontrolled ransomware spread increases both the recovery cost and the BI duration. The IR plan is also a legal necessity — GDPR requires notification within 72 hours of becoming aware of a breach.

🔍

Detection

EDR alert / SIEM trigger / user report. MTTD (mean time to detect) target: <1 hour

🚨

Triage

Confirm attack type and scope. Engage IR retainer. Notify insurer/broker.

🔒

Containment

Isolate affected systems. Preserve evidence. Stop lateral spread.

🧹

Eradication

Remove malware/attacker persistence. Identify and patch initial access vector.

🔄

Recovery

Restore from clean backups. Validate integrity. Phased return-to-service.

⚖️

Legal / Notify

Data breach assessment. GDPR 72-hr notification. Regulatory reporting. Class action defence prep.

📊

Post-Incident

Root cause analysis. Control gap remediation. Insurance claim submission. Lessons learned.

⚠️ OT/ICS Cyber Risk (CH Code) — Additional Considerations

For healthcare (CH) and critical infrastructure accounts with operational technology environments, standard IT security controls are necessary but insufficient. OT/ICS environments (SCADA, DCS, PLCs, medical devices) have distinct risk characteristics: legacy systems that cannot be patched without manufacturer approval, air-gapping requirements that conflict with modern monitoring, and direct physical consequences from a cyber attack (patient harm, industrial incident).

OT-specific underwriting questions: Is the OT network fully segmented from IT? Is there a dedicated OT security monitoring solution (Claroty, Dragos, Nozomi)? Has a Purdue Model network architecture review been conducted? Are remote access sessions to OT systems MFA-protected? For healthcare: has a medical device cybersecurity inventory been completed under FDA guidance (post-2023 FDA Cybersecurity requirements)?

The IR Retainer: Why Pre-Approved Vendors Matter

When a ransomware attack hits at 3am on a Sunday, the insured needs a forensic IR firm on the phone within minutes, not hours spent negotiating vendor agreements. Most leading cyber insurers operate a panel of pre-approved IR firms (Mandiant/Google, CrowdStrike Services, Coveware, Kivu Consulting, Sygnia) that policyholders can engage immediately on a claim without requiring separate underwriter approval.

Policyholders who deviate from the panel — typically because they've already hired a preferred vendor before notifying the insurer — frequently face two problems: higher costs that may not be fully reimbursable, and slower claims settlement while the insurer assesses the non-panel vendor's invoices. The underwriter's practical advice: engage the insurer or broker hotline as the first call, not the last.

Pre-placement IR retainers — where the policyholder contracts an IR firm before any incident — are increasingly valued by underwriters and can attract rate credits of 5–10%. They also dramatically reduce MTTD (mean time to detect) and MTTC (mean time to contain) because the retainer firm already has contextual knowledge of the insured's environment.
