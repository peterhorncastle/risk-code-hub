2004 – 2024: Twenty Years of Landmark Cyber Events

Red dots indicate market-defining events that triggered broad underwriting changes; purple dots indicate confirmed or attributed nation-state attacks; blue dots indicate significant private threat actor events.

2005–06USA (TJX Companies)$250m+ total costs

TJX Companies Payment Card Breach

Hackers exploited unencrypted WEP Wi-Fi at a Marshalls store car park to access TJX's payment processing network, ultimately stealing 94 million payment card records over 18 months. At the time, the largest retail breach ever disclosed. TJX's total costs — card replacement, litigation, settlements, security upgrades — exceeded $250m, largely uninsured.

Insurance impact: Triggered the first wave of Fortune 500 cyber insurance buying. Risk managers recognised that a breach affecting tens of millions of customers could generate costs that dwarfed the entire IT security budget.

2009USA (Heartland Payment Systems)$145m total costs

Heartland Payment Systems — 134 Million Cards

SQL injection attack on payment processor Heartland resulted in 134 million credit cards being exposed — the largest payment card breach in history at the time. Heartland had passed a PCI-DSS audit weeks before the breach was discovered. Total costs including Visa/Mastercard fines, legal settlements and remediation exceeded $145m.

Insurance impact: Demonstrated that PCI compliance does not equal security; cyber underwriters began looking beyond compliance certifications to actual security architecture.

2013USA (Target Corporation)~$200m+ total; ~$90m insured

Target Data Breach — Supply Chain Attack

Attackers stole credentials from Fazio Mechanical, a Target HVAC vendor, and used them to access Target's network via the vendor portal. 40 million payment cards and 70 million personal records were stolen during the 2013 holiday shopping season. Target CEO and CIO resigned. Total costs exceeded $200m; cyber insurer paid approximately $90m — the first landmark cyber insurance payout.

The Target breach crystallised supply chain risk as a primary cyber attack vector and drove adoption of vendor risk management programmes across corporate America. It also established the precedent that a cyber insurer could pay a nine-figure claim — validating the product for large enterprise buyers.

2014USA (Sony Pictures)$100m+ estimated

Sony Pictures Hack — Destructive Nation-State Attack

Attributed to North Korean Lazarus Group, the attack destroyed approximately 70% of Sony Pictures' computer infrastructure, leaked unreleased films, executive salary data and embarrassing emails, and left a "Guardians of Peace" skull as a calling card. Sony's total costs exceeded $100m; its cyber insurer denied coverage under a war exclusion — triggering years of litigation and policy wording debate.

Insurance impact: The first high-profile war exclusion dispute in cyber insurance. Exposed that "system damage and restoration" was not covered under early data breach policies. New policy sections covering infrastructure destruction and recovery were introduced market-wide within 18 months.

2016Bangladesh (SWIFT Network)$81m stolen

Bangladesh Bank SWIFT Heist

Attackers (attributed to Lazarus Group) compromised Bangladesh Bank's SWIFT terminal and submitted fraudulent payment instructions totalling $951m to the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. $81m was successfully transferred before errors in later instructions raised suspicion. Most of the $81m was funnelled through Philippines casinos and never recovered.

Insurance impact: SWIFT-related fraud became a critical sublimited coverage in cyber policies for financial institutions. "Funds transfer fraud" and "social engineering fraud" sublimits were added to most financial sector cyber placements. Drove adoption of SWIFT Customer Security Programme controls as underwriting requirements.

2017Global — WannaCry$4–8bn economic loss

WannaCry Ransomware — NHS and 200,000 Victims

WannaCry — exploiting the leaked NSA EternalBlue exploit for unpatched Windows SMB — infected 200,000 machines in 150 countries within 72 hours. The UK NHS suffered the most visible damage: 80 hospital trusts affected, 19,000 appointments cancelled, total NHS cost £92m. Renault, Deutsche Bahn, FedEx, Telefonica and hundreds of others hit globally. A kill switch discovered by security researcher Marcus Hutchins halted the spread after 7 hours — preventing a potentially far worse outcome.

Insurance impact: WannaCry demonstrated true systemic cyber risk — simultaneous losses across multiple policyholders worldwide from a single malware strain. Accelerated cyber accumulation modelling and drove Lloyd's to begin systemic risk discussions that culminated in the 2022 war exclusion mandates.

2017Global — NotPetya$10bn+ economic loss

NotPetya — The Most Destructive Cyber Attack in History

Disguised as ransomware but a pure wiper, NotPetya spread via a trojanised update to Ukrainian accounting software M.E.Doc. Within hours, it had propagated across corporate networks globally via EternalBlue and credential harvesting. Maersk (global shipping): $300m; Merck: $1.4bn; FedEx/TNT: $400m; Mondelez: $100m; Reckitt Benckiser: £100m; Rosneft: unknown. Total economic loss estimated at $10bn+. Attributed to Russian GRU military intelligence.

Merck's insurer denied the $1.4bn claim under a war exclusion; a New Jersey court ruled in Merck's favour in January 2023, finding that NotPetya was not a "hostile or warlike action" by a government for traditional war exclusion purposes. The Mondelez/Zurich case was settled confidentially. These disputes directly drove Lloyd's 2022 mandatory war exclusion reform.

2017USA (Equifax)$700m+ settlement

Equifax Data Breach — 147 Million Records

Exploitation of an unpatched Apache Struts vulnerability exposed the personal data of 147 million Americans — including Social Security numbers, dates of birth, addresses, and in some cases driving licence numbers. The most damaging consumer data breach in US history. Equifax's $700m FTC/CFPB settlement was the largest data privacy settlement in US history at the time. Total costs exceeded $1.4bn.

Insurance impact: Demonstrated the scale of CISO accountability for patch management failures. Drove standardisation of "patch criticality" as a cyber underwriting question — unpatched critical CVEs became a non-renewal indicator.

2019–20UK (Travelex) / USA (Cognizant)£30m+ / $70m+

Travelex & Cognizant — Big-Game Hunting Begins

Travelex, the foreign exchange company, was hit by Sodinokibi/REvil ransomware on New Year's Eve 2019. Its systems were offline for weeks; the attacker demanded $6m. Travelex paid $2.3m. The disruption contributed to the company entering administration in August 2020. Cognizant, the IT services giant, was hit by Maze ransomware in April 2020, costing $50–70m in remediation. These attacks marked the transition from opportunistic mass ransomware to targeted "big-game hunting" of large, well-capitalised organisations.

2020Global (SolarWinds)$100m+ (US Govt only)

SolarWinds Supply Chain Attack

Russian SVR intelligence inserted malicious code into a SolarWinds Orion software update, distributed to 18,000+ customers including the US Treasury, State Department, DHS, and multiple Fortune 500 companies. The attackers — operating undetected for 9 months — had persistent access to victim networks globally. Total remediation cost to US government alone exceeded $100m; corporate remediation costs were rarely disclosed. Attributed to APT29/Cozy Bear (SVR).

Insurance impact: SolarWinds established software supply chain compromise as a systemic cyber risk. Drove development of "software supply chain" coverage sublimits and the adoption of SBOM (Software Bill of Materials) requirements as future underwriting conditions.

2021USA (Colonial Pipeline)~$4.4m ransom + $50m+ BI

Colonial Pipeline — Ransomware Shuts the US East Coast

DarkSide ransomware — obtained via a compromised VPN password with no MFA — forced Colonial Pipeline to shut 5,500 miles of pipeline for 6 days, disrupting 45% of US East Coast fuel supply. Fuel shortages, panic buying and aviation disruption followed. Colonial paid a $4.4m Bitcoin ransom (DOJ later recovered $2.3m). The event prompted the Biden Administration's critical infrastructure cybersecurity executive order and made MFA a non-negotiable cyber insurance requirement.

Insurance impact: MFA on remote access became a binary underwriting condition market-wide within 6 months. No MFA = no cyber cover, or a material exclusion/warranty. Also highlighted OT/pipeline cyber exposure previously treated as hypothetical.

2021USA (Kaseya VSA)1,000+ companies affected

Kaseya VSA — REvil's $70m Ransom Demand

REvil exploited a zero-day in Kaseya VSA remote management software — used by managed service providers (MSPs) — to push ransomware to over 1,000 end-customer businesses simultaneously. REvil demanded $70m for a universal decryptor. The attack occurred on 4 July US Independence Day weekend, maximising disruption. Kaseya obtained a decryptor (reportedly from the FBI, who had infiltrated REvil) and distributed it without paying ransom. REvil members were subsequently arrested by Russian FSB in January 2022.

Insurance impact: MSP/software supply chain became a critical aggregation concern for cyber underwriters. Many syndicates introduced MSP exclusions or sublimits and began requiring end-to-end security attestations for technology companies providing services to multiple insured customers.

2023Global (MOVEit)$1bn+ estimated across all victims

MOVEit Transfer — Mass Exploitation of File Transfer Software

Cl0p ransomware group exploited a SQL injection zero-day in Progress Software's MOVEit Transfer tool, affecting over 2,500 organisations including the UK's Ofcom, BBC, BA, Boots, US government contractors (DOE, USAID), Shell, and hundreds of others. Unlike traditional ransomware, Cl0p did not encrypt systems — it exfiltrated data and threatened to publish it unless ransoms were paid. Estimated total insured losses exceeded $1bn; aggregate economic impact $10bn+.

Insurance impact: MOVEit drove adoption of "managed file transfer" software patching as an explicit underwriting question. The scale of the event (2,500+ affected organisations) made it the cyber industry's first true accumulation event of the post-war-exclusion era, directly testing Lloyd's new aggregation reporting requirements.

2023USA (MGM Resorts / Caesars)~$100m (MGM); $15m ransom (Caesars)

MGM & Caesars — Social Engineering the Help Desk

Scattered Spider attacked MGM Resorts by social-engineering the IT help desk via a LinkedIn-sourced employee impersonation call — bypassing MFA through a help desk reset. Slot machines, hotel check-in, digital room keys and MGM's website went offline for 10 days. Estimated $100m BI loss. Caesars paid a $15m ransom to ALPHV/BlackCat to prevent stolen loyalty data being published. Both attacks used no technical exploits — purely social engineering and identity manipulation.

Insurance impact: Social engineering of IT help desks became a primary attack vector concern. Underwriters added "help desk social engineering" to questionnaires; identity verification procedures for help desk credential resets became an underwriting requirement.

2024USA (Change Healthcare / UnitedHealth Group)$1.5bn+ total costs

Change Healthcare — The Largest Single Cyber Insurance Loss

ALPHV/BlackCat ransomware attacked Change Healthcare (a UnitedHealth subsidiary processing ~40% of US medical claims) via a compromised Citrix account with no MFA. The 9-day outage disrupted payments to hospitals, pharmacies and physicians across the US healthcare system — the most significant disruption to US healthcare infrastructure since the system's digitisation. UnitedHealth paid a $22m ransom; ALPHV then exit-scammed its own affiliates. Total UnitedHealth costs: $1.5bn+. The affiliate responsible (RansomHub) published the stolen data anyway.

Insurance impact: The largest single cyber insurance loss in history. Demonstrated the catastrophic concentration risk of healthcare payment processors. CH (healthcare cyber) rates hardened 20–40% in H1 2024. Sparked Congressional investigations into healthcare sector cyber resilience and CISA guidance on third-party concentration risk.

2024Global (CrowdStrike)$5.4bn economic loss estimated

CrowdStrike Falcon Update Outage — 8.5 Million Blue Screens

A faulty content configuration update to CrowdStrike's Falcon sensor caused 8.5 million Windows devices to crash with a Blue Screen of Death simultaneously on 19 July 2024 — the largest IT outage in history. Airlines (Delta: $500m+ losses), hospitals, banks, emergency services, and broadcasters were affected globally. Total economic loss estimated at $5.4bn (Parametrix). Critically, this was not a cyber attack — it was an IT error by a security vendor.

Insurance impact: CrowdStrike tested whether "system failure" extensions in cyber policies covered non-malicious IT outages caused by a third-party security vendor. Initial market view: most CY policies with "dependent system failure" extensions were triggered; property BI policies were not. Estimated cyber insurance payout ~$400–600m (far below economic loss). Highlighted the coverage gap between cyber and property BI for systemic technology failures.
