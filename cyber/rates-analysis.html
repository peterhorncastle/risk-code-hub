Cyber Rates & ROE Analysis

The 2019–2022 ransomware hardening cycle produced the most dramatic rate increases of any Lloyd's class in the modern era — some accounts saw premiums triple or quadruple in 18 months. This module tracks the full pricing cycle from 2015 to 2026, current rate adequacy by sector, and return-on-equity scenarios under different loss assumptions.

Rate Adequacy by Sector (2024)

Rate adequacy varies significantly by sector. Healthcare remains the most stressed class — Change Healthcare ($1.5bn+ total loss), the 2021 HSE Ireland attack, and persistent NHS ransomware incidents have kept healthcare rates elevated and coverage conditions tight. Financial services has broadly achieved adequacy following heavy 2020–2022 hardening. Manufacturing and retail have seen the most aggressive softening from new capacity.

Return on Equity Scenarios

The ROE model below uses a £100m premium portfolio as the base. The key variables are the attritional loss ratio (frequency × severity of sub-£5m losses), the large loss ratio (single events above £5m), and the catastrophic loss assumption (systemic events like WannaCry-scale). Operating expense ratios are assumed at 30% (acquisition 18%, management 12%). Capital loading for Lloyd's cyber is approximately 40–45% of premium.

⚠️ Pricing Cycle Watch: AI-Accelerated Attack Threat (2025–2026)

Generative AI tools are lowering the barrier to entry for sophisticated phishing, credential stuffing and social engineering attacks. The security community has identified AI-generated spear-phishing emails that defeat traditional training-based detection. BEC losses — already a sublimited exposure — may accelerate materially as AI makes targeted fraud indistinguishable from legitimate communication.

More concerning to systemic risk modellers is the potential for AI to accelerate vulnerability exploitation cycles: the time from CVE disclosure to widespread exploitation may compress from days to hours. This would significantly reduce the value of patch management as a risk control, destabilising current rate adequacy assumptions. Most underwriters are watching 2025 loss data closely before committing to further rate reductions.

The Capacity Pendulum: Why Cyber Hardening Was So Extreme

The 2019–2022 cyber hardening cycle was the most dramatic in any modern Lloyd's class for a structural reason: cyber capacity is almost entirely provided by specialist paper, not by reinsurance-backed capacity that can easily be redeployed. When ransomware losses mounted in 2020, underwriters couldn't simply diversify; they had to either exit the class or radically reprice.

The absence of mature ILS structures for cyber meant there was no catastrophe bond capacity to absorb tail losses. Instead, every Lloyd's cyber syndicate simultaneously increased rates, imposed MFA conditions, and reduced capacity — creating a compounding hardening effect that far exceeded what any individual loss year would have justified in isolation.

The lesson for underwriters is that cyber's pricing cycle is more volatile than property catastrophe precisely because the correlation of losses across the portfolio is imperfectly modelled. A ransomware wave doesn't follow hurricane seasonality — it can hit any sector, any geography, any time — making it structurally difficult to price on a mean-reversion basis.
