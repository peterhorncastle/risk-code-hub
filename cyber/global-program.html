Module 07 — Global Programme Structuring

Cyber Global Programme Structuring

Multinational companies face a patchwork of cyber notification laws, data sovereignty requirements and local admitted insurance obligations. This module covers programme structures for global cyber placements, key jurisdiction profiles, the Lloyd's mandatory systemic risk exclusions (LMA 5564/5565), cyber aggregation management, and emerging ILS/parametric structures for systemic cyber risk.

Cyber Programme Architecture

A global cyber programme for a large multinational typically layers a Lloyd's/London market master policy with local admitted policies or DIC/DIL (difference-in-conditions / difference-in-limits) wraps in jurisdictions with admitted requirements. Unlike property programmes, cyber has relatively few hard local admitted requirements globally — but where they exist (Brazil, China, Argentina), they must be addressed carefully.

Lloyd's Systemic Risk Exclusions: LMA 5564 & LMA 5565

Following concern that a single nation-state cyber attack could simultaneously trigger thousands of policies — creating unquantifiable market-wide accumulation — Lloyd's mandated that all standalone cyber policies include one of two war/systemic exclusion clauses effective 31 March 2023. The choice between LMA 5564 and LMA 5565 affects the scope of what is excluded and has been the subject of significant coverage dispute analysis.

LMA 5564

Cyber War Exclusion

Excludes losses from cyber operations that form part of a war between "major powers" (defined as a subset of nation-states with significant cyber capability). The exclusion is narrower: it requires an active state of war between specified states. Criminal ransomware — even if state-sponsored — may not trigger this exclusion.

Wider coverage for policyholders; preferred by risk managers. The attribution debate — was an attack truly part of "war"? — is partially answered by the high threshold of "major power war."

LMA 5565

Cyber Infrastructure Attack Exclusion

Excludes losses from cyber operations that: (i) form part of a war between any states, OR (ii) are directed at critical national infrastructure (power, water, financial systems, transport, health). The exclusion is broader and applies even without a formal state of war if CNI is targeted.

More problematic for healthcare (CH) and CNI operators since an attack targeting their sector could trigger the exclusion even if the policyholder is a collateral victim, not a direct target.

⚠️ Attribution Problem: Is NotPetya Covered? The Merck Precedent

NotPetya (2017) was attributed to Russian GRU military intelligence. Merck filed a $1.4bn property claim; insurers invoked war exclusions. A New Jersey Superior Court ruled in Merck's favour in January 2023: the war exclusion in the property policy (written before the cyber era) required a "physical" hostile or warlike action and did not contemplate state-sponsored cyber operations.

LMA 5564 and LMA 5565 were written specifically to address this gap — they are cyber-specific and designed to exclude state-sponsored cyber attacks from cyber policies. Whether a future attack on the scale of NotPetya would be excluded under these new clauses (rather than the old property war exclusions) remains untested. The breadth of "state-sponsored" and whether a criminal group acting with state tolerance qualifies is the key open question — and will likely drive major coverage litigation in the next decade.

Aggregation Management: The Systemic Risk Problem

Systemic cyber risk — a single event triggering simultaneous losses across thousands of policies — is the existential challenge of cyber underwriting. Lloyd's requires managing agents to run realistic disaster scenarios (RDS) for cyber accumulation, stress-testing their portfolio against events including: mass ransomware campaign; cloud provider outage; DNS infrastructure attack; financial system cyber attack; coordinated critical infrastructure attack.

ILS and Parametric Cyber Structures

Traditional insurance-linked securities (cat bonds) have been slow to enter the cyber market because cyber lacks the actuarially defined modelling that underpins property cat ILS. However, parametric and index-based structures are emerging to provide capital market capacity for systemic cyber risk — the tail that traditional reinsurance is most reluctant to price.

Parametric Cyber Triggers

Payment triggered by a defined index (e.g. number of organisations reporting ransomware above a threshold in a 30-day window, measured by a named third-party data provider). Removes attribution and adjustment disputes. Basis risk: payout may not correlate with insured's specific loss.

Industry Loss Warranties (ILWs)

Pay when total industry loss from a specified cyber event exceeds a trigger (e.g. $5bn total industry loss from a single ransomware campaign). Modelled on catastrophe ILW structure. Cyber ILWs trading in limited volume in Bermuda market; pricing highly uncertain.

Cyber Cat Bonds

Early-stage; Beazley issued the first cyber catastrophe bond ($45m, 2023) using a modelled loss trigger. Represents proof-of-concept for capital market participation in cyber tail risk. Premium savings vs. traditional reinsurance; requires sophisticated investors comfortable with cyber model uncertainty.

Cyber Sidecars

Third-party capital participates alongside a cyber underwriter's portfolio on a quota-share basis. Provides capacity but requires investors to accept cyber's non-modelled tail — attractive yield premium compensates for model uncertainty. Growing in Bermuda alongside cat-linked reinsurance sidecars.

Case Study: Global Manufacturer — Programme Restructure Post-MOVEit

A €5bn revenue European industrial conglomerate with operations in 22 countries had a £75m standalone CY policy at Lloyd's when MOVEit was exploited in May 2023. Their HR software vendor used MOVEit for file transfer; 280,000 employee records were exfiltrated. The claim was covered under their data breach response section but the incident drove a programme review.

Before: Single Lloyd's policy with £75m limit, unnamed contingent BI at 20% sub-limit, no local policies, SIR of £1m.

After restructure: £50m SIR (self-insurance layer using captive), £150m Lloyd's master policy (CY), local admitted policies in Germany, France, Brazil and China (DIC/DIL structure), unnamed contingent BI reduced to 5% with named cloud endorsement for AWS and Microsoft 365, formal third-party vendor cyber risk assessment programme required as policy condition.

The restructure increased total premium from £1.8m to £2.4m (+33%) but doubled the limit from £75m to £200m effective coverage, dramatically improved local regulatory compliance, and eliminated the most problematic accumulation exposures. The SIR increase to £50m was offset by captive dividend income and improved pricing on the excess layers.
