What Cyber Insurance Covers

💻

First-Party Cyber BI

Lost revenue and extra costs during a network outage caused by a cyber attack. Often the largest single component of a major cyber loss.

🔒

Ransomware / Extortion

Ransom payment (where legally permissible), negotiation costs, decryption and system restoration. RaaS groups have made this the dominant peril.

🗃️

Data Breach Response

Forensics, notification costs, credit monitoring, PR/crisis management, regulatory defence following a personal data breach.

⚖️

Privacy & Network Security Liability

Third-party claims from individuals or businesses whose data was compromised or who suffered loss due to the insured's network failure.

🏛️

Regulatory Defence & Fines

Legal defence costs and, where insurable by law, regulatory fines/penalties under GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA, FCA. Fine coverage varies by jurisdiction.

💸

Social Engineering / Cyber Crime

Funds transfer fraud (BEC), invoice redirection fraud, telephonic impersonation. Often sublimited; separate crime policy may be needed.

🖥️

Tech E&O (CZ)

Claims against technology companies for software bugs, system failures or IT services errors that cause third-party financial loss.

🏥

Healthcare / OT Cyber (CH)

Clinical disruption BI, patient diversion costs, medical device compromise, OT/ICS attack response for healthcare and critical infrastructure.

⚠️ Systemic Risk: The Lloyd's Mandatory Exclusions (LMA 5564 / LMA 5565)

In 2022, Lloyd's mandated that all standalone cyber policies include exclusions for state-sponsored cyber attacks causing catastrophic systemic losses. This followed concern that a single nation-state attack (e.g. a NotPetya-scale event) could simultaneously trigger thousands of cyber policies across the market, creating an unquantifiable accumulation exposure.

The Lloyd's cyber war exclusion clauses (LMA 5564 "war" and LMA 5565 "infrastructure attacks") exclude losses from cyber operations forming part of a war between major powers, and attacks on critical national infrastructure. Underwriters must attach one of these clauses to all standalone cyber policies bound after 31 March 2023. This remains a live debate — several large coverage disputes hinge on whether an attack was "state-sponsored" or criminal.

Cyber's Position in the Lloyd's Market

Cyber is now consistently one of Lloyd's top five classes by premium growth rate. From near-zero in 2012, the Lloyd's cyber market has grown to an estimated £2.5bn+ GWP in 2024, written by approximately 50+ active syndicates. Beazley (the originator of the modern data breach product), Hiscox, Tokio Marine Kiln, CFC Underwriting (via Lloyd's), Coalition, and At-Bay are among the most prominent lead markets.

The class experienced its defining crisis in 2019–2021 when ransomware-as-a-service groups industrialised extortion attacks, driving combined ratios above 130% across the market. The subsequent 2020–2022 hardening cycle — rates more than doubled on some accounts — stabilised profitability and drove wholesale adoption of minimum security control requirements as binding underwriting conditions.

The "Silent Cyber" Problem — Now Resolved

"Silent cyber" refers to the unintentional presence of cyber coverage (or exclusion) in non-cyber policies — property, marine, liability, energy — where the policy wording predates the cyber era and neither explicitly includes nor excludes cyber-triggered losses. The NotPetya attack of 2017 crystallised this problem: Merck's $1.4bn property damage claim was denied by insurers citing a war exclusion; a New Jersey court ultimately ruled in Merck's favour in 2023.

Lloyd's mandated that all policies either explicitly include or explicitly exclude cyber risk by 1 January 2022 (Market Bulletin Y5258). This "affirmative cyber" requirement has driven a significant transfer of cyber premium from property/casualty/marine books into standalone CY policies — one of the structural drivers of cyber premium growth that will continue for years.
