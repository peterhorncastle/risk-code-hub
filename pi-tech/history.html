Seven Eras of Tech & Telecom Professional Indemnity

1. Pre-1984 — Mainframe Era: No Dedicated Product

Early computing (1950s–1983) had no dedicated technology PI product. Liability for computer errors fell under general professional negligence principles — courts struggled to classify software as product or service (critical for product liability vs professional liability). IBM System/360 (1964) and mainframe era created first large-scale financial dependence on computer systems. "Computer error" clauses in general liability policies were exclusionary — insurers viewed computer errors as inherently unpredictable. Lloyd's market had no dedicated technology PI class. First US tech errors and omissions policies emerged around 1981–1984 from St Paul Companies and Hartford Steam Boiler.

2. 1984–1995 — First Technology E&O Policies

US market develops standalone technology E&O product. Key driver: business software market expands rapidly post-IBM PC (1981). Courts clarify: custom software = professional service (negligence standard applies); shrink-wrap software = product (product liability may apply). Lloyd's market begins writing technology PI for US tech exporters and UK software houses. Y2K awareness growing by mid-1990s — date-format programming shortcut built into legacy COBOL systems from 1960s–1980s. Insurers begin excluding Y2K on new policies by 1995. Key cases: Computer Horizons Corp v American Guarantee (1990s) — establishes tech E&O claims trigger principles in US.

3. 1996–2003 — Y2K Crisis & Dot-Com Bubble

Y2K (Year 2000) — global concern that date-rollover from 1999→2000 would corrupt COBOL-based legacy systems. Insurance industry pays ~$1bn in Y2K-related claims (far less than feared — $600bn+ was spent on remediation). Lloyd's market hard on tech PI 1999–2001. Dot-com boom (1996–2000) creates massive expansion of technology service providers — many under-capitalised and under-insured. Dot-com bust (2000–2001) causes wave of failed IT projects and E&O claims as companies collapse mid-implementation. Enron/Andersen collapse affects IT consultancy arms of Big 5 accounting firms.

4. 2004–2012 — SaaS Revolution & First Major Claims

Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) model emerges (Salesforce founded 1999; AWS launched 2006). Cloud computing shifts software from product to ongoing service — deepens professional liability exposure. Outsourcing wave: Accenture, IBM, EDS, CSC win massive government IT contracts — NHS National Programme for IT (2003–2011, £12.4bn, largely abandoned — major E&O exposure). Knight Capital Group (Aug 2012): defective trading algorithm deployed causing $440m loss in 45 minutes. Paradigm tech E&O event: code defect → catastrophic financial loss. Market responds: algo-trading exclusions, financial services sector tech E&O tightest rated sub-class.

5. 2013–2019 — Cloud Dominance, Cyber Interface

AWS, Azure, GCP dominate cloud infrastructure. SaaS market grows to $100bn+ annually. Tech E&O and cyber insurance boundaries blur significantly. Insurers develop "combined" technology E&O + cyber products (initially for SME market). TSB banking IT migration failure (April 2018): £330m customer compensation, FCA £48.6m fine. Markit (now IHS Markit) data platform errors generate E&O claims. NotPetya cyberattack (June 2017): $10bn+ economic damage — treated as warlike act by many insurers; war exclusion dispute tests tech E&O/cyber boundary. GDPR (May 2018) — data breach notifications surge; privacy-related PI claims emerge.

6. 2020–2022 — COVID Acceleration, Supply Chain Software Risk

COVID-19 accelerates digital transformation — cloud adoption surges; remote working tools (Zoom, Teams, Slack) see explosive growth. SolarWinds supply chain attack (Dec 2020): 18,000 organisations compromised via malicious update. Kaseya VSA attack (July 2021): MSP management software compromised; ransomware deployed to 1,500 businesses via single MSP. Tech E&O/cyber interface: was Kaseya a security failure (cyber) or defective software delivery (tech E&O)? Market evolves: separate tech E&O and cyber towers with specific coordination language. Log4Shell (Dec 2021): widely deployed Apache logging library; critical vulnerability; emergency patching costs borne by thousands of tech firms.

7. 2023–Present — CrowdStrike, AI Liability

CrowdStrike Falcon sensor update (19 July 2024): defective content configuration update pushed to 8.5 million Windows devices globally; systems crash (BSOD). Airlines, hospitals, banks, broadcasters grounded. Estimated $10bn+ in total economic losses; insured losses ~$800m–1bn (most uninsured). Delta Air Lines alone: $500m loss; files $500m lawsuit against CrowdStrike. Tech E&O paradigm event of the decade — single vendor update causes global systemic outage. AI/ML liability: generative AI models hallucinating incorrect professional advice create novel tech E&O exposure. Algorithmic bias claims emerge. EU AI Act (2024) creates regulatory liability framework for high-risk AI systems.
