Coverage Structure — The Four Sections

Offshore energy policies are typically structured with four main coverage sections, each with its own insured value, rate, deductible and sublimits. The sections interact — physical damage under Section 1 triggers OEE under Section 2 — but each is rated and managed independently.

SectionCoverageLloyd's CodesBasis of CoverTypical Limit
Section 1Physical Damage (Property)EC, EM, ENAll risks of physical loss or damage; replacement value or agreed valueFull asset replacement value (can exceed $5bn for FPSO)
Section 2Operators Extra Expense (OEE) / Control of WellEY, EZNamed perils — well blowout, cratering, seepage & pollution, re-drill$50m–$500m+ depending on deepwater complexity
Section 3Seepage, Pollution & Contamination (S&P&C)EY, EZPollution cleanup costs arising from covered well control eventSublimit within OEE, typically $100m–$250m

Section 1 — Physical Damage Rating Factors

Physical damage rating for offshore energy assets is fundamentally about quantifying the probability and severity of loss to a complex, high-value asset in a hostile environment. The key rating factors are set out below.

Asset Type & Configuration

Fixed steel jacket platforms rate most favourably. FPSOs and semi-submersibles carry higher rates due to process complexity and weathervaning exposure. Jack-ups are rated by water depth and ground conditions. Deepwater floating production systems attract a loading for operational complexity.

Very High Impact

Geographic Location / Basin

North Sea (UK and Norwegian sectors) is the benchmark. Gulf of Mexico carries a named windstorm loading — particularly EM code (GOM Wind) — which can double the physical damage rate. West Africa, Brazil and Southeast Asia each carry their own geographic modifiers reflecting local peril exposure and emergency response capability.

Very High Impact

Water Depth

Deepwater (300–1,500m) and ultra-deepwater (>1,500m) assets attract significant loadings. Access for emergency response is restricted; well control costs escalate exponentially; subsea intervention in event of damage is far more complex and costly than in shallow water environments.

Very High Impact

Asset Age & Condition

Ageing assets — particularly those past their original design life — attract loadings. The underwriter will examine fabric maintenance reports, inspection records, integrity management plans and any regulatory compliance issues. Assets with deferred maintenance or known structural concerns may be declined or rated with exclusions.

High Impact

Named Windstorm Exposure (GOM)

For GOM risks (code EM), named windstorm is the primary peril driver. Underwriters will assess return period exposure using industry cat models, platform design wind speeds, water depth and distance from historical storm tracks. Named windstorm sublimits are common; separate NWS deductibles are standard post-Katrina.

High Impact

Process Complexity & Hydrocarbon Inventory

High-pressure, high-temperature (HP/HT) reservoirs increase the risk of catastrophic failure. Large gas inventories on production platforms increase fire and explosion severity. Process safety management quality — including SIL assessments, pressure safety valve testing and blowdown systems — is scrutinised.

High Impact

Operator Track Record

Five-year loss history is standard submission information. National Oil Companies (NOCs) with limited loss disclosure, or operators with known asset integrity or HSE compliance issues, carry premium loadings. Major integrated oil companies with robust safety management systems typically attract the most favourable terms.

Moderate Impact

Deductible Structure

Higher deductibles reduce the pure premium rate. Standard physical damage deductibles range from $1m to $25m+ depending on asset value. Named windstorm deductibles are typically higher — often 2–5% of sum insured. Operational deductibles (covering routine operational losses below the main deductible) are also common.

Moderate Impact

Section 2 — OEE / Control of Well Rating Factors

OEE and control of well coverage is the most technically demanding rating challenge in the offshore energy class. The potential maximum loss from a deepwater blowout is extremely large and difficult to model accurately, while the frequency of blowout events — though low — is not zero even for the best-managed operators.

Wellbore Characteristics

H2S-containing reservoirs (sour gas) significantly increase blowout risk and make well control operations far more hazardous and expensive. HP/HT wells require specialist equipment and expertise. Water depth determines re-drill cost and timescale — a deepwater re-drill can take 4–6 months and cost $200–$500m.

Very High Impact

Well Control Programme Quality

Underwriters review BOP type and certification, kill-weight mud inventory, well control procedures and driller competency. Post-Macondo, BOP certification to API standards (including shear rams capable of cutting all well components) is now a standard underwriting requirement for deepwater risks.

Very High Impact

Re-Drill Cost Estimate

The policy limit for OEE should be informed by a realistic re-drill cost estimate, which takes account of rig day rate, re-drill duration (depth-dependent), consumables, specialist well control contractors (BOOTS & Coots, Wild Well Control) and regulatory compliance costs. Underwriters will challenge limits that appear materially under-estimated.

High Impact

Seepage & Pollution Exposure

Post-Macondo, pollution cleanup cost exposure for large deepwater blowouts has been dramatically re-rated. Underwriters now model worst-case spill scenarios using flow rate estimates, reservoir volume, water depth and proximity to environmentally sensitive areas. GoM deepwater S&P exposure is among the highest in the world.

High Impact

Information Requirements for Submission

📋 Standard Submission Information — Physical Damage & OEE

  • Asset description: type, design, age, construction year, water depth, field location, installation date
  • Total Insured Value (TIV) breakdown: topside, hull/structure, subsea, pipelines, drilling equipment
  • Production data: daily production rates, hydrocarbon type, reservoir pressure and temperature
  • Asset integrity: most recent third-party inspection reports, fabric maintenance budget, outstanding action items
  • Safety Case / Safety Management System summary (UK/Norwegian North Sea)
  • Wellbore data (OEE): number of wells, H2S presence, reservoir pressure, water depth, BOP specification and certification date
  • Re-drill cost estimate with basis of calculation
  • Five-year loss history (reported claims, near-misses if available)
  • Named windstorm exposure data (GOM risks): design wind speed, mooring system, evacuation procedures
  • Operator HSE statistics: LTIR, TRIR, environmental incidents

Key Underwriting Principles — Summary

1. Asset complexity drives rate, not just value. An FPSO with HP/HT wells and a large process inventory is a fundamentally different risk to a simple jacket platform of the same TIV.

2. OEE is the tail risk. The physical damage section produces the most premium but the OEE section can produce losses that dwarf the physical damage loss — Macondo's insured OEE loss exceeded $2 billion alone.

3. Location is fundamental. The GOM wind zone (EM code) warrants an entirely separate underwriting approach from non-GOM (EN code) — different models, different deductibles, different sublimit structures.

4. Information quality matters. The offshore energy underwriter who accepts a risk without inspecting the Safety Case, integrity reports and well control programme is not pricing the risk — they are guessing at it.
