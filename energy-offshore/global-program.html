International Programme Architecture

Offshore energy risks operated by international oil companies (IOCs) and national oil companies (NOCs) are typically placed as global or regional programmes at Lloyd's. Rather than placing each individual asset separately, a programme consolidates exposures across countries, basins, and asset types into a coherent insurance structure — providing economies of scale, consistent coverage, and a single policy interface for the insured's risk management team.

🌍 Global Programme

A single master policy covering all of an operator's worldwide offshore assets. Premium is calculated on the global portfolio. Coverage is typically written at Lloyd's or the London market as the lead, with local admitted policies (fronted) issued where local regulations require local insurance.

🌐 Regional Programme

Covers a defined geographic region — e.g., a European/North Sea programme or a West Africa deepwater programme. Common where an IOC has significantly different risk profiles across regions that warrant separate market treatment or different reinsurance structures.

🏢 Asset-Specific Placement

Stand-alone policy for a single major asset — typically an FPSO, LNG platform, or major pipeline system where TIV justifies a dedicated lead underwriter relationship and bespoke limit tower. Common for ultra-deepwater assets with TIV exceeding $2bn.

🏭 Captive Integration

Many majors (BP, Shell, TotalEnergies, Equinor) retain a significant proportion of their offshore exposure through wholly owned captive insurers, using the commercial market only for catastrophic excess layers. The captive sits as the primary insurer; Lloyd's provides excess capacity.

Key Offshore Basins — Risk & Regulatory Profiles

Each major offshore basin has a distinct combination of geological risk, metocean environment, regulatory framework, and insurance market requirements. Understanding these differences is fundamental to pricing and programme design.

🇬🇧 UK Continental Shelf (UKCS)

Lloyd's codes: EN (property), EH (liability)

Key operators: Harbour Energy, Equinor, Shell, bp, Ithaca, EnQuest

Risk profile: Ageing infrastructure (many platforms >25 years), high decommissioning liability, North Sea metocean (wave height up to 30m), well-regulated (NSTA/HSE)

Regulatory: Safety Case Regulations; PFEER; OPOL mandatory ($250m); NSTA field development consents

Programme notes: Lloyd's market lead for most UKCS risks. Full admitted programme — no fronting required (Lloyd's admitted UK). OPOL replaces first layer of pollution liability.

Lloyd's Lead No Fronting Ageing Assets Decom Exposure
🇳🇴 Norwegian Continental Shelf (NCS)

Lloyd's codes: EN (property), EH (liability)

Key operators: Equinor (dominant), Aker BP, TotalEnergies, Vår Energi

Risk profile: Highest safety standards globally (PSA Norway); NORSOK-compliant assets; newer infrastructure; deepwater frontier exploration in Barents Sea

Regulatory: PSA Norway; NORSOK D-010 mandatory; government co-insurance (SDØE) on state equity; Norwegian Pollution Control Authority for spill response

Programme notes: Norwegian State holds 67–78% equity in most fields via Equinor/SDØE. NCS rates historically lower than UKCS due to superior safety record. Lloyd's participates as following market — Norwegian insurance market (Gjensidige, IF P&C) often leads locally.

Best-in-Class SMS NORSOK Compliant Local Lead Often State Co-insured
🇺🇸 Gulf of Mexico (GoM)

Lloyd's codes: EM (property GoM), EY (OEE GoM)

Key operators: Shell, bp, Chevron, BHP, Murphy Oil, Walter Oil & Gas

Risk profile: Hurricane wind zone (Category 4–5 risk); deepwater dominates (post-shallow-water decline); Macondo legacy — BSEE oversight; SEMS mandatory

Regulatory: BSEE/BOEM; Drilling Safety Rule; Well Control Rule; SEMS; MWCC membership required for deepwater drilling permits

Programme notes: GoM is the highest-rated offshore basin per dollar of TIV due to hurricane windstorm. Wind modelling (AIR/RMS) required by underwriters. Mandatory catastrophe deductible — typically 5–10% of TIV or $50m min. OEE limits for single wells often $250–500m for deepwater GoM. Lloyd's leads this market globally.

Hurricane Windstorm Highest-Rated Basin Lloyd's Lead CAT Deductible
🇧🇷 Brazil — Campos & Santos Basins

Lloyd's codes: EN (property), EH/EG (liability), EZ (OEE)

Key operators: Petrobras (dominant), Shell, TotalEnergies, bp, Equinor

Risk profile: Ultra-deepwater pre-salt (water depths to 2,200m); FPSO-dominated fleet; Petrobras self-insures heavily; riser and mooring exposure in deep water

Regulatory: ANP (National Petroleum Agency); IBAMA for environmental licensing; SUSEP requires local admitted insurance — fronting by local Brazilian insurer (IRB, Sompo, Chubb) mandatory

Programme notes: Lloyd's cannot write Brazilian risks directly — must front through local admitted insurer with Lloyd's providing reinsurance behind. Complex regulatory environment; ANP limits the use of offshore fronts. Petrobras typically self-retains $500m+ and uses Lloyd's for catastrophe excess only.

Fronting Required Ultra-Deepwater Complex Regulatory FPSO-Dominant
🌍 West Africa — Nigeria, Angola, Ghana

Lloyd's codes: EN (property), EG/EH (liability), EZ (OEE)

Key operators: Shell, TotalEnergies, Eni, bp, Chevron, Equinor, Tullow

Risk profile: Deepwater FPSOs (West Africa is world's largest FPSO concentration); piracy/theft risk in GoG (Gulf of Guinea); third-party government take (local content); political risk overlay

Regulatory: Nigeria: NAICOM/NIA — mandatory local content (minimum 70% local) and Nigerian Re participation. Angola: ENSA (state reinsurer) participation required. Ghana: National Insurance Commission

Programme notes: Local content regulations in Nigeria and Angola require a proportion of premium to be placed with local insurers — capacity is limited. Lloyd's participates as XS market above local layer, or via fronting arrangement. Piracy/theft (GoG) typically excluded from standard form; war/piracy cover arranged separately via London market.

Local Content Mandated Piracy Risk Fronting Needed FPSO Expertise
🌏 SE Asia — Malaysia, Indonesia, Vietnam, Brunei

Lloyd's codes: EN (property), EG/EH (liability), EZ (OEE)

Key operators: PETRONAS, Pertamina, Shell, TotalEnergies, ConocoPhillips

Risk profile: Mix of ageing shallow-water (Malaysian shelf, Mahakam Delta) and newer deepwater (South China Sea); earthquake risk (Indonesia — Pacific Ring of Fire); typhoon/tropical cyclone (South China Sea, Philippines)

Regulatory: Indonesia: OJK local admitted requirement; strong national oil company (Pertamina) control. Malaysia: BNM — Lloyd's has no direct access; fronting through Labuan Lloyd's platform possible. Singapore: Lloyd's has SG operations (LLMIA) — some SE Asia risks writable direct.

Programme notes: Singapore is the regional hub for SE Asia offshore energy programmes. Lloyd's Singapore (LLMIA) can write regional risks with proper licensing. Indonesia and some Vietnam risks require local fronts. Earthquake zone risks require geo-hazard assessment — particularly for fixed platform structural exposure.

Earthquake Zone Typhoon Risk Singapore Hub Indonesia Fronting
🇦🇺 Australia — NW Shelf & Browse Basin

Lloyd's codes: EN (property), EG/EH (liability), EZ (OEE)

Key operators: Woodside, Shell (Prelude FLNG), Chevron (Gorgon/Wheatstone), Santos, Inpex (Ichthys)

Risk profile: World's largest LNG operations; extreme cyclone risk (Category 4–5, e.g., Cyclone Veronica 2019 impacted Woodside); remote location — limited rescue and intervention capability

Regulatory: NOPSEMA (National Offshore Petroleum Safety and Environmental Management Authority); Australian Petroleum Production and Exploration Association (APPEA) standards. Lloyd's is admitted in Australia — can write direct.

Programme notes: LNG plant onshore/nearshore components often written under separate industrial all-risks or energy property policy; offshore upstream portion under energy codes. Cyclone modelling essential for NW Shelf. Large single-location TIVs (Prelude FLNG ~$12bn) require multi-market limit towers.

Cyclone Zone LNG Dominant Lloyd's Direct Remote Location

Fronting Requirements & Lloyd's Global Licensing

A fronting arrangement is necessary where local regulations require risks to be insured by a locally admitted insurer, but the actual risk-carrying capacity sits elsewhere (typically at Lloyd's). The fronting insurer issues the local policy, collects premium, and immediately reinsures the entire risk back to Lloyd's under a reinsurance agreement.

How Fronting Works in Offshore Energy

Step 1: Operator requires insurance in a country where Lloyd's has no admitted status (e.g., Brazil, Indonesia, Angola).

Step 2: A locally admitted insurer (the "fronter") issues a local policy compliant with national insurance law.

Step 3: The fronting insurer enters into a reinsurance treaty with Lloyd's syndicates, transferring 90–100% of the risk. Lloyd's syndicates are the true risk carriers.

Step 4: Premium flows: Operator → Fronter (less local taxes and fronting fee of typically 1–3%) → Lloyd's via reinsurance.

Step 5: Claims: paid by fronter, who is then reimbursed by Lloyd's. Fronter retains credit risk on Lloyd's — hence fronters require Lloyd's 'A' financial strength rating.

Captive Insurance Structures in Offshore Energy

The largest international oil companies operate sophisticated captive insurance structures, using the commercial market (including Lloyd's) primarily for catastrophic excess exposure. Understanding the captive layer is important when underwriting as a following or excess market.

Pure Captive

Wholly owned subsidiary insurer of the parent company — typically domiciled in Guernsey, Isle of Man, Bermuda, or Cayman Islands. Covers only the parent's risks. Major operators: Shell, bp, Equinor, TotalEnergies each operate large captives retaining $500m–$1bn+ of offshore exposure.

Group Captive

Jointly owned by multiple non-competing companies to share risk. Less common in offshore energy than in other lines, but some E&P JV structures incorporate shared captive arrangements for the joint venture's portfolio, particularly in remote basins (e.g., deepwater West Africa JVs).

Protected Cell Company (PCC)

Single corporate entity where each cell has separate assets and liabilities — allows smaller operators to access captive benefits without the capital requirement of a standalone captive. Guernsey PCCs popular for mid-size E&P companies seeking to retain the working layer of their UKCS portfolio.

Fronted Captive Programme

Captive issues the local policy (using a fronter where needed), then cedes the excess above its retention to Lloyd's or the commercial market. Provides the operator with tax-efficient premium flow, loss reserve control, and local regulatory compliance simultaneously.

Programme Design Considerations

Coverage Aggregation and Per-Occurrence Limits

A critical programme design question is whether limits apply per-occurrence or in aggregate across all locations. Offshore energy programmes typically operate on a per-occurrence basis with separate limits for each coverage section (S1, S2, S3, S4) to avoid single large events exhausting all programme capacity.

Coverage SectionTypical Limit BasisAggregation RiskUnderwriter Concern
S1 – Physical DamagePer-occurrence, per-location sublimits for GoM windstormHigh in GoM (hurricane affects multiple platforms simultaneously)PML aggregation across operator's portfolio if multi-location risk
S2 – OEE/Control of WellPer-well, per-occurrenceLow (each well is an independent blowout event)Single well limit adequacy for deepwater GoM; $250–500m min for deepwater
S3 – Seepage & PollutionPer-occurrence, often sub-limited vs. S1Medium (a blowout or pipeline failure can cause extended pollution event)Time element and cleanup cost modelling; OSCP adequacy
S4 – LiabilityPer-occurrence; separate from property towerMedium (a Macondo-type event involves liability and property simultaneously)OPA 90 limits for GoM; liability not capped under UK law

Deductible Structures in Global Programmes

Straight Deductible

Fixed monetary amount per occurrence (e.g., $10m per event). Most common for UKCS and non-GoM risks. Provides predictable cost to the insured; underwriters comfortable with clear threshold.

Percentage (TIV) Deductible

GoM windstorm event: typically 5–10% of TIV per location. For a platform TIV of $500m, this is $25–50m insured retention. Prevents underwriters absorbing attritional windstorm damage to the vast number of small Gulf of Mexico structures.

Annual Aggregate Deductible

Used on large portfolio programmes — the insured retains the first X million of losses in aggregate across the year before any insurance recovery. Encourages loss prevention; common in UKCS maintenance/inspection-class claims.

Franchise Deductible

If loss exceeds the franchise threshold, the full loss (including the deductible amount) is recovered. Used occasionally in deepwater liability to distinguish between minor and significant pollution events.

Reinsurance and ILS Participation

The upper layers of large offshore energy limit towers are increasingly placed with Insurance-Linked Securities (ILS) funds — catastrophe bonds and collateralised reinsurance vehicles domiciled in Bermuda and Cayman. These ILS vehicles provide capacity at upper tower layers where traditional reinsurers reach capacity limits, particularly for GoM windstorm and deepwater blowout scenarios.

Lloyd's syndicates both access ILS capacity as reinsurance for their own books (outward reinsurance) and compete as risk carriers against ILS at upper tower layers. Understanding whether ILS is participating in the programme above Lloyd's affects aggregation and claims-paying certainty analysis.
