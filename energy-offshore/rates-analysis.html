The Offshore Energy Pricing Cycle (2000–2026)

The offshore energy market is among the most volatile in the Lloyd's specialty book. Three major loss events — the 2005 hurricane season ($9.3bn), Macondo (2010, $3.5–4bn) and the 2017 Atlantic season ($2.5bn) — have each triggered sharp market corrections. Between those events, excess capacity and competitive pressure drove rates to levels that proved inadequate for the risks underwritten.

Key Conclusions — Rate Adequacy & Market Outlook

Non-GOM offshore property (EN) and OEE (EZ) are currently at or close to technical rate adequacy — the first sustained period of adequate pricing since the post-Macondo hard market of 2010–2013. Underwriters should not sacrifice this through excessive competition on renewal of well-managed, loss-free portfolios.

GOM wind zone risks (EM and EY) remain structurally challenging. The 10-year loss ratios remain above 100% for most GOM-wind exposed portfolios. Named windstorm sublimits and higher deductibles are essential underwriting tools, not optional enhancements.

The market is currently softening, not hardening. New capacity entering from 2023 onwards is exerting competitive pressure. The discipline of the 2018–2022 period is at risk of eroding. Underwriters should maintain minimum rate thresholds and resist pressure to reduce deductibles or expand coverage without commensurate premium adjustment.

Energy transition creates medium-term headwinds. As E&P investment declines, the pool of offshore hydrocarbon risks will shrink. This creates competitive pressure to deploy capacity, which historically leads to poor underwriting outcomes. Offshore wind (R1/R3) and other renewable energy classes are the natural growth area but require new technical frameworks.
