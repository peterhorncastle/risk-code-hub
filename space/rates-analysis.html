2. Market Cycle Phases

1990–1997 — Soft to Moderate Market

Growing Commercial Market; Manageable Loss Experience

The post-Challenger commercial launch market expanded. Ariane 4 was the dominant commercial launcher; Atlas, Titan, and early Proton competed. Loss experience was manageable — individual satellite failures occurred but did not overwhelm the market. Global GWP grew from ~$100m to ~$500m. Rates were moderate and competitive. EST

1998–2001 — Catastrophe Hard Market

1998–99 Loss Year Drives 3–5× Rate Increases

The aggregate insured loss of approximately $2.5–3.5bn against a GWP of ~$600m produced a combined ratio estimated at 500%+. EST Launch rates tripled or quadrupled. Terms tightened: deductibles increased, CTL thresholds raised, exclusions broadened. Several underwriters left the market permanently. The market contracted in both capacity and GWP as operators deferred discretionary insurance or self-insured.

2002–2008 — Hard Market Recovery

Best Underwriting Years; Gradual Softening

With rates elevated and loss experience improved, the market produced strong underwriting profits through approximately 2002–2006. The Sea Launch/NSS-8 failure (2007) and a cluster of in-orbit anomalies maintained some discipline, but by 2008 competitive pressure was driving rates lower. New capital entered attracted by the profitable years. EST

2009–2014 — Soft Market; ITAR Restructuring

Competition, ITAR Effects, and Continued Rate Decline

The market softened steadily. ITAR restrictions pushed some non-US satellite placements away from US-market participants, growing the European (AXA XL Space, AMOS/Allianz Space) and eventually the Asian market share. Loss experience was mixed: the Iridium-Cosmos collision (2009) was significant but not a large insured loss; several GEO partial losses maintained some pricing discipline. GWP plateaued at $500–600m. EST

2015–2020 — Falcon 9 Disruption

Reusable Launch Compression; New Market Structure

SpaceX Falcon 9 established reusable operations from 2017. Launch costs dropped dramatically. The Amos-6 pad explosion (2016) briefly hardened the market, but the overall trajectory was compressive. Launch rates compressed. The emergence of mega-constellations (Starlink, OneWeb) required new portfolio underwriting approaches that did not exist in the traditional per-satellite model. EST

2020–2026 — Selective Hardening; Structural Change

Boeing 702MP Anomalies; Debris Risk; New Vehicle Premium

The pattern of Boeing 702MP satellite propulsion anomalies (Intelsat IS-33e, IS-35e and others) drove specific underwriting loading for that bus type. The Viasat-3 Americas constructive total loss (2023, ~$420m) was the largest single space insurance claim in years and reminded the market of catastrophic tail risk. New launch vehicles (Ariane 6, New Glenn, Starship development) attracted higher rates. EST
