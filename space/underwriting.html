Space Insurance Underwriting Methodology

1. Launch Insurance (SL) — Rate Methodology

Launch insurance premium is expressed as a rate on line (RoL) — a percentage of the insured value. The primary underwriting inputs are launch vehicle reliability, satellite bus heritage, mission profile complexity, and launch site/range conditions. EST

Launch Insurance Premium Formula
Launch Premium = Insured Value ($) × Base Launch Rate (%) × Vehicle Reliability Factor × Mission Complexity Factor × Satellite Heritage Factor
Expressed as % of insured value. Base rate driven by launch vehicle success history. All factors multiplicative.

Launch Vehicle Reliability

Success rate over last 20+ missions. New vehicles or post-failure returns carry higher loadings. SpaceX Falcon 9 (98%+ success) attracts lowest commercial rates. New vehicles (Starship, Neutron) attract very high initial rates until track record established.

Base rate range: 1.5% – 12% of insured value EST

Launch Vehicle — Reused Stage

SpaceX Falcon 9 reused first stages were initially loaded ~0.5–1% above new stage. As reuse data accumulated (15+ flights on individual boosters), the market largely converged on par pricing with new stages. Some underwriters still apply a modest reuse loading beyond booster flight 10.

Reuse loading (if applied): +0.0% – +0.5% EST

LEOP Complexity Factor

LEOP (Launch and Early Orbit Phase) complexity: number of deployment steps, apogee kick motor / electric propulsion orbit-raising duration (weeks to months for ion propulsion), antenna deployment, solar array deployment. More steps = higher risk.

Factor: 1.0 (simple LEOP) – 1.40 (multi-step complex) EST

Satellite Bus Heritage

Bus flight heritage (number of successfully-operating satellites of same design): first flight of new bus = highest loading. Established bus with 10+ in-orbit successes = lowest loading. Boeing 702MP series attracted specific loading after propulsion anomaly pattern. Airbus Eurostar, Boeing 601, SSL 1300 are established buses.

Heritage discount: 0% (new bus) to −15% (mature bus >15 in-orbit) EST

Launch Site / Range

Established ranges (Kourou, Cape Canaveral, Vandenberg) attract lowest rates. New/emerging launch sites carry additional loading. Weather delays at launch sites can affect coverage period and must be managed within policy terms.

Range loading: 0% – +15% for non-standard sites EST

Orbit Type

GTO (Geostationary Transfer Orbit) missions require apogee kick motor firing — a high-risk phase. Direct GEO injection (few vehicles available) reduces this risk. LEO deployment simpler but radiation environment issues in some LEO bands (Van Allen belts crossing).

GTO loading vs. GEO direct: +10% – +20% EST

2. In-Orbit Insurance (SO) — Annual Rate Methodology

In-orbit annual insurance (SO) is rated differently from launch — it is based on the probability and severity of failure during normal operations. The primary loss drivers are subsystem reliability, remaining operational life, and in-orbit environment (orbit altitude, solar activity, debris environment). EST

In-Orbit Annual Premium Formula
Annual Premium = Current Insured Value ($) × Base In-Orbit Rate (%) × Age/Life Factor × Subsystem Redundancy Factor × Orbit Environment Factor
Insured value typically declines annually with remaining life (residual economic value basis or declining replacement cost).

GEO Telecom — Standard Annual Rate

A mature, well-heritage GEO telecom satellite in years 3–10 of a 15–18 year life. Standard bus design with documented flight heritage. No prior anomalies. Good subsystem redundancy.

Approx. 1.5% – 2.5% p.a. of insured value EST

Age Factor

New satellites (years 1–3) may attract slightly higher rates as design issues manifest early. Mid-life (years 4–12) lowest rate period. Late-life (years 12+) increasing rate as fuel depletion and component aging elevate failure probability.

Late-life loading (>12 years): +20% – +50% EST

LEO Small Satellite

Higher radiation exposure at certain LEO altitudes; shorter design life (3–7 years typical); less redundancy in small satellite designs; lower insured values per satellite. Many small satellite operators self-insure or carry fleet/portfolio policies.

Approx. 2.0% – 4.0% p.a. (if insured individually) EST

Prior Anomaly Loading

A satellite that experienced an anomaly in a prior policy year (but recovered) carries a specific loading in subsequent years. Anomaly type matters: recoverable attitude anomaly vs. permanent partial subsystem failure have different loading implications.

Anomaly loading: +15% – +100%+ depending on type EST

3. Partial Loss Valuation — Transponder/Capacity Approach

In-orbit partial losses are the most complex valuation challenge in space insurance. A satellite that loses 30% of its transponder capacity is not simply worth 30% less — the residual value depends on which transponders failed (coverage region, frequency band), operator contracts, and the capacity market. EST

Partial Loss ScenarioValuation MethodNotes
Transponder Capacity LossRevenue stream approach: PV of lost transponder lease revenue over remaining life × discount rate. Or: replacement capacity cost × fraction lost.Most GEO telecom satellites. Basis of loss is typically the capacity the satellite can no longer provide vs. design specification.
Life Reduction (Fuel Loss)Original insured value × (years of life lost / original design life) = partial loss quantumCommon in orbit-raising anomalies where satellite uses its own propellant to compensate for launch vehicle undershoot. Yamal-402 (2013) set key precedent.
Power DegradationPower output × revenue per watt of capacity = economic loss per year × remaining lifeSolar array degradation beyond design spec. Relevant where power directly limits transponder count.
CTL ThresholdConstructive Total Loss typically defined in policy at 60–80% loss of design capacity. Below this threshold: partial loss. Above: CTL paid at full insured value.CTL threshold is a key negotiation point at inception. Operators prefer lower CTL thresholds (lower bar to collect full SI); underwriters prefer higher thresholds.

5. Worked Examples

Worked Example A — GEO Telecom Satellite Launch (SL)

Risk profile: New-build GEO communications satellite (Ku-band, 32 transponders). Bus: Airbus Eurostar 3000EOR (electric orbit-raising; 6-month LEOP). Launch vehicle: Ariane 6 (maiden commercial mission, limited flight history). Launch site: Kourou, French Guiana. Insured value: $280m. Operator: established Tier-1 satellite operator with 8 in-orbit satellites.
Rating ElementFactor / RateNote
Base launch rate (Ariane 6 — new vehicle)4.50% of IVNew vehicle premium; limited heritage
LEOP complexity (electric orbit-raising)× 1.256-month EOR adds significant risk window
Bus heritage (Eurostar 3000EOR — mature bus variant)× 0.90Established bus with heritage discount
Operator experience (Tier-1)× 0.95Experienced operator credit
Indicated Launch Rate4.50% × 1.25 × 0.90 × 0.95 = 4.81%
Indicative launch premium: $280m × 4.81% = ~$13.5m ILLUS

Note: Electric orbit-raising significantly elevates LEOP premium relative to chemical propulsion equivalent. Ariane 6 rate will compress as flight history accumulates. First in-orbit annual (SO) policy typically placed at inception alongside SL coverage.

Worked Example B — GEO Satellite In-Orbit Annual Renewal (SO)

Risk profile: Intelsat GEO communications satellite, year 8 of 15-year design life. Boeing 601HP bus (mature heritage). All primary subsystems nominal. One transponder failed (replaced by redundant unit) — no capacity loss. Fuel: 7 years remaining per plan. Insured value: $195m (declining from original $260m on residual-life basis). No prior claims.
Rating ElementFactor / RateNote
Base in-orbit rate (GEO, mid-life, established bus)2.00% p.a. of IVMid-life Boeing 601HP — mature design
Age factor (year 8, within designed life window)× 1.00No loading — within normal operating window
Prior anomaly (1 transponder failure — on redundancy)× 1.10Nominal loading — system on backup but functional
Operator credit (Intelsat — experienced operator)× 0.95Tier-1 operator, experienced ground team
Indicated Annual Rate2.00% × 1.00 × 1.10 × 0.95 = 2.09%
Indicative annual in-orbit premium: $195m × 2.09% = ~$4.1m ILLUS

Note: Insured value of $195m reflects residual economic value after 8 years of 15-year design life (insured value typically reviewed annually with operator and broker). The failed-then-recovered transponder is noted but the satellite is fully operational — modest anomaly loading appropriate.
