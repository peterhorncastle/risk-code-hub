Space Insurance

Satellite launch, construction-to-in-orbit, and operational transponder insurance. Covers expendable and reusable launch vehicles, satellite manufacturing risk, in-orbit anomalies, on-orbit life extension, and space liability under the Outer Space Treaty and Liability Convention.

Lloyd's Risk Code Structure

CodeClassCoverage ScopeTypical Limit
SC Satellite — Construction / Pre-Launch Physical damage to satellite during manufacture, testing, transportation (air/sea) and launch campaign. Coverage period: factory → launch pad. Key perils: handling damage, vibration/acoustic testing failure, contamination, launch pad fire. Analogous to marine cargo but for high-value, one-of-a-kind spacecraft. $50m – $600m+ per satellite
SL Satellite — Launch & Early Orbit All-risks coverage from ignition through launch vehicle separation, orbit raising (apogee kick motor), and in-orbit testing phase (LEOP — Launch & Early Orbit Phase). Typically 12 months from launch. Covers total loss, constructive total loss, and partial loss of satellite capability. $100m – $600m+ per launch
SO Satellite — In-Orbit / Operational Annual operational coverage for in-orbit satellites against total loss, partial loss (reduced transponder capacity/power), and in-orbit life loss. Key perils: solar events, orbital debris collision, power subsystem failure, thruster anomaly, software failure, ASAT/cyber interference. Typically renewed annually. $50m – $500m+ per satellite

Coverage Architecture

🛰️ Construction Risk (SC)

  • All-risks physical damage during manufacture
  • Factory acceptance testing (vibration, thermal vacuum)
  • Transportation to launch site — air freight or sea
  • Launch campaign handling and fuelling
  • Contamination, ESD, handling error
  • Typically written as inland marine / cargo hybrid

🚀 Launch Risk (SL)

  • Launch vehicle failure / range safety destruct
  • Separation failure (fairing, payload separation)
  • Propulsion anomaly in orbit-raising phase
  • Apogee kick motor / upper stage failure
  • LEOP phase anomalies (antenna deployment, solar array)
  • Partial loss: reduced capacity/power at end of LEOP

🛸 In-Orbit Operations (SO)

  • Total loss: catastrophic failure, loss of control
  • Constructive total loss: >75% capacity loss (typical)
  • Partial loss: transponder failure, power degradation
  • Gradual revenue loss from life reduction
  • On-orbit servicing gap (emerging market)
  • Annual renewable; life expectancy typically 15–18 years GEO VER

⚖️ Space Liability

  • Third party liability for damage caused by spacecraft
  • Outer Space Treaty 1967: launching state bears absolute liability VER
  • Liability Convention 1972: governs international claims between states
  • Launch range and ground operations liability
  • Re-entry debris / ASAT debris field liability (emerging)
  • Typically written as a separate liability slip

🌌 The NewSpace Revolution — Underwriting Context

The space insurance market is undergoing its most significant structural transformation since the late 1990s hard market. Three factors are reshaping the risk landscape: EST

  • Reusable launch vehicles: SpaceX Falcon 9 (first flight 2010; operational reusability from 2017) has launched over 250 missions as of 2024 with a near-perfect success rate, dramatically reducing launch cost (from ~$60,000/kg on traditional ELVs to ~$2,700/kg on Falcon 9) and compressing launch risk premiums.
  • Mega-constellations: SpaceX Starlink (6,500+ satellites by 2024), Amazon Kuiper, OneWeb, and Chinese LEO constellation programmes are placing hundreds of small satellites at a time. Traditional per-satellite underwriting is replaced by portfolio/fleet approaches; debris accumulation risk is unprecedented.
  • Miniaturisation and commoditisation: CubeSats and small sats (1–500kg) have democratised space access. Insurance value per satellite has dropped dramatically; the risk pool has expanded but average premium has shrunk. Some nano/micro-sat operators self-insure.

The traditional space insurance market — dominated by AXA XL, Global Aerospace, AMOS, Atrium/Tokio Marine, and Lloyd's syndicates — is adjusting to a market where launch frequency has increased 5× since 2016 but premium per launch has compressed significantly.
