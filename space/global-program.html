1. Space Insurance Programme Architecture

A commercial satellite programme typically involves three discrete insurance phases — construction, launch/LEOP and in-orbit — each with separate policy periods, limits and risk carriers. Large operators may place all three phases as an integrated programme with a single lead underwriter providing continuity across the satellite lifecycle. EST

CAT XL / Umbrella

🌐 Catastrophe / Umbrella Layer

Aggregate excess-of-loss protection for fleet-wide events; solar storm, ASAT debris field, cascading collision. Specialist reinsurance market. Limits: $100m–$500m+ EST

XL Layer 2

📈 Excess of Loss — Upper Layer

Excess $100m xs $50m (illustrative). London market and continental European reinsurers. Annual aggregate or per-occurrence. ILLUS

XL Layer 1

📈 Excess of Loss — Lower Layer

Excess $50m xs $25m (illustrative). Lloyd's syndicates as primary market with capacity from global specialty. ILLUS

SO · In-Orbit

🛰️ In-Orbit Annual Cover

Per-satellite or fleet annual policy. Physical damage / CTL / partial loss. Typical limits $50m–$500m per satellite. Key carriers: Lloyd's syndicates (Atrium, Brit, Hiscox), Starr, AXA XL. EST

SL · Launch + LEOP

🚀 Launch & Early Orbit Phase Cover

Covers launch through LEOP (typically 180 days). Per-launch limits $100m–$600m. Rate on Line driven by vehicle reliability. Co-insurance market with Lloyd's lead. EST

SC · Construction

🏗️ Pre-Launch / Construction Cover

All-risks cover during manufacture, AIT and transport. Limits scale from $50m (smallsats) to $600m+ (large GEO). Ground risk and pre-launch testing included. EST

🛡️ Self-Insured Retention / Deductible

Varies by operator. Large primes (Boeing, Airbus Defence) typically self-insure up to $10–25m per event EST. National agencies (NASA, ESA) operate partial self-insurance models.

📌 Integrated Lifecycle Programme Advantages

  • Single lead underwriter providing policy continuity from factory floor to EOL
  • Eliminates coverage gaps at launch transition (construction → launch handoff risk)
  • Fleet-wide data benefits underwriter's actuarial view; loyalty discount available for long-term clients EST
  • Simplified claims handling — no dispute over which phase a loss occurred
  • Co-insurance line slip enables broker to build consistent market panel efficiently EST

3. Mega-Constellation Portfolio Cover

Traditional per-satellite underwriting is impractical for constellations of hundreds or thousands of smallsats. The market has evolved fleet/portfolio models. EST

🚀 Batch Launch Cover

  • Group of satellites (20–60 per Falcon 9 rideshare) insured on a single launch policy
  • Launch premium: aggregate insured value × batch launch rate
  • Per-satellite value: $500k–$5m for smallsats vs. $150–600m for GEO EST
  • Pro-rata loss settlement for percentage of batch lost

🛰️ Annual Fleet Blanket

  • Single annual policy covering entire deployed constellation
  • Per-event limit (loss of X satellites in one collision/event) + annual aggregate
  • Premium: aggregate fleet value × negotiated annual rate (0.5-2% est.) EST
  • CTL trigger at fleet level: loss of Y% of constellation capacity

📊 Portfolio Loss Model

  • Underwriters model expected annual loss frequency from: debris collision, launch failure, solar storm, cybersecurity
  • Expected value loss model vs. binary per-satellite model
  • Correlation risk: debris event could eliminate multiple satellites in same orbital shell
  • Reinsurance essential for catastrophe correlation loss scenarios EST

⚡ Starlink — Market Reference

  • SpaceX self-insures Starlink constellation — insufficient private market capacity EST
  • ~6,500 operational satellites (Q1 2024); 1,000+ more planned VER
  • Third-party liability: US Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) licence requires indemnification VER
  • Market watching for any future move to partial private insurance as constellation matures

4. Third-Party Liability in Space

Space liability is governed by the Liability Convention 1972 (absolute state liability for surface damage; fault-based for space object collisions) and national licensing regimes. Commercial operators must hold adequate third-party liability insurance as a condition of their operating licence. VER

🌍 Surface / Air Damage (Absolute Liability)

  • Launching state absolutely liable for damage caused on Earth's surface or to aircraft VER
  • No fault required — operator must demonstrate causal link only
  • Example: Cosmos 954 (1978) — USSR paid Canada CAD $3m for radioactive debris VER
  • Uncontrolled reentries increasing as mega-constellations deploy

🚀 Launch Third-Party Liability

  • US: Commercial Space Launch Act — FAA maximum probable loss (MPL) calculation determines required coverage VER
  • UK: Space Industry Act 2018 — operators must carry TPL as condition of licence VER
  • Coverage: bodily injury and property damage to third parties on ground and in air during launch operations
  • Typical limit: $500m–$1.5bn third-party liability (US) EST

🛰️ In-Orbit Collision Liability

  • Fault-based under Liability Convention Art. III for in-orbit collisions VER
  • Iridium-Cosmos 2009: no liability claim made — Iridium at fault, Cosmos derelict VER
  • FCC debris rules create quasi-liability framework for debris non-compliance
  • In-orbit liability cover available on specialist space market; typically low limits vs. hull values EST

🏛️ Indemnification / Government Backstop

  • US: Government indemnifies above the required insurance limit up to $1.5bn above the MPL — for US-licensed launches VER
  • UK: CAA provides government indemnity backstop for licensed launches
  • Backstop critical for commercial viability — private market cannot cover full catastrophic liability
  • Government backstop does NOT cover hull/payload losses — physical damage insurance remains private market

🚀 FAA Maximum Probable Loss (MPL) Framework

The FAA calculates the MPL for each US launch based on the launch vehicle's debris model, trajectory and overflown populations. This determines the minimum third-party liability insurance the operator must carry. The MPL calculation considers expected casualty rates from uncontrolled reentry, debris dispersion and range safety. VER For Falcon 9 launches from Cape Canaveral, the typical required TPL coverage is in the range of $500m–$1.5bn depending on trajectory and overflight population. EST

5. Captive & Alternative Programme Structures

🏦 Captive Insurance

  • Large operators ($50m+ annual premium) may form captive to retain primary layer
  • Captive retains predictable lower layers; purchases excess / catastrophe reinsurance
  • Advantages: premium retention, risk data accumulation, customised coverage
  • Satellite industry captives less common than aviation — binary loss nature reduces actuarial benefits EST

📊 Parametric Cover

  • Emerging: parametric triggers for space weather events (Dst index threshold, S4+ proton event)
  • Rapid claim payment without lengthy satellite health assessment
  • Basis risk: parametric trigger may not correlate perfectly with actual satellite loss
  • Swiss Re, Aon, Eutelsat have explored parametric space weather structures EST

💼 Profit Sharing / Long-Term Agreement

  • Multi-year deals (3–5yr) with agreed rate bands and profit/loss commissions
  • Premium certainty for operator; retention alignment for underwriter
  • Lloyd's market supports LTA structures for established fleet operators
  • Typical profit commission: 15–25% of underwriting profit if CR <80% EST

🛰️ In-Orbit Life Extension Insurance

  • Niche product for satellites beyond design life receiving life extension services
  • Northrop Grumman MEV (Mission Extension Vehicle) docking with Intelsat satellites VER
  • Underwriters must assess grapple/docking risk + extended operations risk
  • Very limited market; bespoke negotiation required EST
