2004 – 2024: Twenty Years of Major Downstream Losses

Red dots indicate market-defining events that triggered broad re-rating; orange dots indicate significant individual losses. Insured loss estimates are market consensus figures and may differ from total economic losses.

2005Texas City, Texas, USA~$1.5bn insured

Texas City BP Refinery Explosion

A vapour cloud explosion (VCE) in the isomerisation unit killed 15 workers and injured 180. A raffinate splitter tower was overfilled during restart following maintenance turnaround; the resulting vapour cloud ignited from a running vehicle engine. The explosion destroyed the isomerisation unit and caused extensive damage to surrounding process units.

The Baker Panel investigation (2007) identified systematic failures in BP's process safety culture and leadership. The eight Baker Panel recommendations became the foundation of modern PSM best practice internationally. The US Chemical Safety Board (CSB) investigation ran concurrently. BP paid over $2.1bn in criminal fines, civil settlements and compensation.

Underwriting impact: US refinery rates increased 25–40% in 2005–2006. Process safety audits (OSHA PSM compliance) became a standard pre-binding requirement for US refinery risks. Baker Panel report adoption became a condition of cover on BP's global energy programme.

2005Buncefield, Hertfordshire, UK~£900m insured

Buncefield Fuel Storage Terminal Explosion

A massive vapour cloud explosion at the HOSL (Hertfordshire Oil Storage Ltd) terminal — one of the UK's largest fuel storage depots — caused the most costly peacetime explosion in UK history. An overflow of unleaded petrol from a tank overflow due to a stuck level gauge created a vapour cloud that ignited at 06:01 on 11 December 2005.

Although only 43 people suffered injuries (many from nearby offices), the explosion destroyed 23 large storage tanks and caused widespread blast damage to surrounding commercial and residential properties up to 800m away. The site was not rebuilt; it was permanently decommissioned.

Underwriting impact: Triggered a complete overhaul of European fuel storage underwriting. Independent overflow prevention (IOP) systems became a near-universal underwriting requirement. The bund capacity assessment methodology was revised. The PADHI (Planning Advice for Developments near Hazardous Installations) buffer zones around UK fuel terminals were extended.

2005Gulf Coast, USA~$10bn energy insured losses (all sectors)

Hurricanes Katrina & Rita — Gulf Coast Energy Infrastructure

Back-to-back major hurricanes in August and September 2005 caused catastrophic damage to Gulf Coast energy infrastructure. Onshore downstream impacts included: flooding of multiple refineries in the greater New Orleans area; wind and storm surge damage to storage tank farms; and a 30-day outage of over 25% of US refining capacity (mainly Louisiana).

Key insured losses: Murphy Oil spill (Meraux, LA) — 25,000 barrels released, ~$170m cleanup; Chalmette Refinery (St Bernard Parish) — total flood loss, >$500m; numerous tank farms in Belle Chasse, Norco and Baton Rouge corridors.

Underwriting impact: The event triggered the largest single repricing in Lloyd's energy market history. Gulf Coast windstorm loadings increased 200–400%. Nat-CAT aggregate management became a board-level requirement at Lloyd's syndicates. RMS and AIR energy-specific cat models were adopted as standard. Post-Katrina, Lloyd's set maximum individual line sizes on Gulf Coast refinery risks.

2009Jaipur, Rajasthan, India~$60m insured

Jaipur Indian Oil Corporation Terminal Fire

A major fire at the Indian Oil Corporation fuel storage terminal burned for 11 days, destroying 11 tanks holding approximately 60,000 tonnes of petroleum products. The fire began during a pipeline transfer operation when a valve flange failed. Thirteen IOC employees and emergency responders died.

The incident highlighted the risks of ageing infrastructure in fast-growing emerging market downstream sectors — the Jaipur terminal's fire suppression systems were inadequate for a fire of this scale. It prompted IOC and the Indian government to commission a national review of fuel storage safety standards.

2010Anacortes, Washington, USA~$100m insured

Tesoro Anacortes Refinery Explosion

A heat exchanger failure at Tesoro's Anacortes refinery caused a naphtha release that ignited, killing 7 workers in the immediate explosion. Investigation found high-temperature hydrogen attack (HTHA) had degraded the carbon steel exchanger shell beyond its rated limits over many years without detection.

The CSB investigation led to new API 941 guidance on HTHA inspection intervals and the development of Nelson Curves as mandatory inspection tools. The event catalysed the adoption of fitness-for-service (FFS) assessments as underwriting requirements for heat exchangers in hydrogen service above 260°C.

2012Amuay, Falcón State, Venezuela~$1.5bn insured

Amuay Refinery Complex Explosion

A gas cloud explosion at PDVSA's Amuay refinery — the third-largest in the world — killed 48 people (including soldiers, civilians and refinery workers) and injured over 150. The explosion was caused by a gas leak from a faulty propane pump that accumulated overnight before igniting.

Amuay had been suffering from chronic underinvestment under PDVSA's nationalised management; the explosion was one of a series of incidents in Venezuelan downstream infrastructure in the early 2010s reflecting deteriorating maintenance standards. The BI loss was compounded by Venezuela's inability to source replacement parts under US sanctions.

Underwriting impact: Triggered a reassessment of Venezuelan sovereign credit risk on energy premiums and a hardening of terms on emerging market downstream risks with state-owned operator profiles and politicised maintenance regimes.

2013Geismar, Louisiana, USA~$200m insured

Williams Olefins Plant Explosion

A reboiler heat exchanger rupture at Williams' propylene/ethylene plant released a large propylene vapour cloud that ignited, killing 2 workers and injuring 167. The exchanger had been repeatedly bypassed during a hot work procedure due to inadequate MOC (Management of Change) processes.

The CSB found that the catastrophic failure resulted from overpressure of the heat exchanger when liquefied propylene was heated by steam while isolated — a classic "blocked-in liquid overpressure" scenario. The incident reinforced the importance of PSV (pressure safety valve) sizing reviews following plant modifications.

2015Torrance, California, USA~$300m insured

ExxonMobil Torrance Refinery Explosion

An explosion in the electrostatic precipitator (ESP) of the fluid catalytic cracker (FCC) at ExxonMobil's Torrance refinery caused significant damage and triggered a partial plant shutdown lasting 14 months. Three workers were injured. The explosion occurred when catalyst fragments from the FCC impacted the ESP during a pressure fluctuation.

The BI loss — from a 14-month partial outage at a key California gasoline supplier — was the dominant component of the total loss. California's limited refinery capacity meant that local gasoline prices increased 20–30 cents/gallon during the outage, creating significant economic impact beyond the insured loss.

2016Moss Point, Mississippi, USA~$150m insured

Enterprise Products Pipeline Explosion

A natural gas liquids pipeline operated by Enterprise Products Partners ruptured near Moss Point, Mississippi, releasing a vapour cloud that ignited. The rupture resulted from internal corrosion caused by water ingress at a low point in the pipeline — a failure mode that post-event inspection revealed was widespread at multiple low-lying points across the pipeline system.

2017Gulf Coast, USA~$7–9bn energy insured losses

Hurricane Harvey — Gulf Coast Refinery Flooding

Hurricane Harvey made landfall near Rockport, Texas on 25 August 2017 and stalled over the Houston area for 4 days, dropping an unprecedented 60+ inches of rain in some locations — the most rainfall from any US weather event on record. The Houston Ship Channel area — home to the world's largest petrochemical complex — experienced catastrophic flooding.

Key downstream losses: Arkema Crosby chemical plant — organic peroxides caught fire when backup generators failed in floodwater ($700m+ environmental/liability); ExxonMobil Baytown refinery — partial shutdown, flood damage; Huntsman, LyondellBasell, Dow Chemical and virtually every facility in the Greater Houston complex suffered varying degrees of flood damage or precautionary shutdown. Total US refining capacity lost: approximately 4.4 million b/d at peak (22% of US total).

Underwriting impact: Post-Harvey, Gulf Coast refinery policies introduced mandatory flood sub-limits (typically 25–50% of PD limit), 72-hour flood deductibles, and requirements for site-specific flood elevation certificates. Combined ratio for Gulf Coast downstream risks in 2017 exceeded 300%.

2019Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, USA~$500m insured

Philadelphia Energy Solutions Refinery Fire

An alkylation unit at the 335,000 b/d Philadelphia Energy Solutions refinery suffered a catastrophic pipe failure at a corroded elbow, releasing a large cloud of hydrofluoric acid (HF) and hydrocarbons. The resulting fire and explosion destroyed the alkylation unit and caused extensive damage to adjacent process units. Miraculously, there were no fatalities.

PES had been operating under Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection and had deferred significant maintenance. The fire ultimately led to the permanent closure of the refinery — the oldest and largest on the US East Coast. The loss of 335,000 b/d of East Coast refining capacity drove regional gasoline price increases and supply concerns that persisted for over a year.

Underwriting impact: Reinforced focus on insured's financial health and maintenance deferral as a key underwriting risk indicator. HF alkylation units identified as high-risk items requiring specific risk engineering review — several syndicates subsequently excluded or sub-limited HF alkylation exposures.

2019Abqaiq & Khurais, Saudi Arabia~$1bn+ repair cost

Saudi Aramco Abqaiq Drone Attack

A coordinated drone and cruise missile attack on Saudi Aramco's Abqaiq crude processing facility and Khurais oilfield on 14 September 2019 temporarily halved Saudi Arabia's crude production — removing approximately 5.7 million barrels/day from global supply (c.5% of world production). Abqaiq is the world's largest crude oil stabilisation complex, processing approximately 7% of global supply.

Eighteen drones and seven cruise missiles struck processing units, storage spheres and pipelines at Abqaiq. The attack was attributed to Houthi forces (with suspected Iranian backing). Saudi Aramco restored full capacity within 10 days — faster than initially feared — limiting the BI loss. However, the event was a watershed moment for geopolitical risk assessment in energy insurance.

Underwriting impact: Triggered a market-wide review of war and terrorism exclusions in energy policies. The event fell into a grey zone between "war" (excluded) and "terrorism" (potentially covered under some policies). Lloyds published updated guidance on the interpretation of war exclusions for drone and missile attacks. Many syndicates subsequently tightened terrorism sub-limits and added specific drone/UAV exclusions.

2021Colonial Pipeline, Eastern USA~$50m direct loss

Colonial Pipeline Ransomware Attack

A ransomware attack by the DarkSide group forced Colonial Pipeline to shut down its 5,500-mile pipeline system — the largest fuel pipeline in the US, supplying ~45% of East Coast fuel — for 6 days in May 2021. While the direct insured loss was relatively modest, the event caused fuel shortages across the US Southeast, with retail panic-buying and aviation fuel shortages at major airports.

Colonial paid an $4.4m Bitcoin ransom (later partially recovered by the FBI). The event was a defining moment for cyber risk in energy infrastructure and triggered a market-wide review of the intersection between cyber risk and physical energy insurance. Most EF (pipeline) policies contain cyber exclusions following this event.

2022Freeport LNG, Texas, USA~$400m insured

Freeport LNG Terminal Explosion and Shutdown

An explosion at Freeport LNG's liquefaction terminal in Texas in June 2022 — caused by over-pressurisation and rupture of an LNG transfer pipe — forced the terminal (the US's second-largest LNG export facility, capacity 2.8 bcf/day) offline for approximately 9 months. The timing, during the European energy crisis following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, significantly impacted global LNG supply and European gas prices.

The extended shutdown — caused by regulatory compliance requirements as much as physical damage — demonstrated the enormous BI potential of LNG export infrastructure and the growing link between LNG terminal outages and geopolitical commodity price volatility.

2023East Palestine, Ohio, USA~$800m+ total (liability-dominated)

Norfolk Southern Train Derailment — Vinyl Chloride Release

A Norfolk Southern freight train derailment near East Palestine caused the release and controlled burn of vinyl chloride and other hazardous chemicals, triggering a major environmental and public health incident. While not a conventional downstream insurance loss (primarily a liability/pollution event), it highlighted the risks of hazardous chemical transportation adjacent to residential areas and triggered class action litigation exceeding $600m in settlements.

The event prompted legislative reviews of hazardous material transportation regulations and raised the profile of pipeline transportation as a safer alternative to rail for chemicals — potentially positive for EF (pipeline) premium volume.

2024Deer Park, Texas, USA~$200m estimated

LyondellBasell Houston Refinery Fire

A major fire in the crude unit of LyondellBasell's 268,000 b/d Houston refinery (formerly Lyondell Chemical / Basell Polyolefins) caused significant damage in 2024. The refinery — already announced for closure as part of LyondellBasell's energy transition strategy — suffered damage that accelerated its shutdown timeline. The insured loss was compounded by the BI impact on the integrated petrochemical complex adjacent to the refinery.
