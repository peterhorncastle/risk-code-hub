Programme Architecture: The Big Oil Model

The world's largest downstream energy companies — Shell, ExxonMobil, Chevron, TotalEnergies, BP, Saudi Aramco, Reliance — operate through centralised global insurance programmes that are architected to provide seamless coverage across dozens of countries while meeting local regulatory requirements and managing catastrophe aggregation.

A typical major IOC global downstream programme has four structural layers: a self-insured retention (captive), a Lloyd's/London market lead layer, an international reinsurance layer, and a catastrophe/clash layer for extreme scenarios. Each layer serves a specific risk transfer purpose and is purchased from different markets.

Typical Downstream Energy Limit Tower — Major IOC ($5bn TIV refinery complex)

Clash / Cat Reinsurance: $500m xs $1bn (Munich Re / Swiss Re / Hannover / ILS)
Layer 4: Extreme scenario/cat agg cover — infrequently triggered; highly reinsured; ILS cat bonds sometimes used
International Reinsurance Layer: $500m xs $500m (Munich Re, Swiss Re, Zurich, AXA XL, Hannover Re)
Layer 3: Large single-risk losses and Nat-CAT scenarios; priced by lead markets at Lloyd's terms + margin
Lloyd's Lead Layer: $250m xs $250m (TMK 510, Beazley 623, Chaucer 1084, Convex, Lancashire)
Layer 2: The critical "risk layer" — Lloyd's syndicates set technical terms; following market prices off lead; risk engineering reports required
Working Layer: $250m xs $50m SIR (supported by commercial insurers + Lloyd's following market)
Layer 1: "Working layer" where most attritional and mid-size losses are absorbed; loss-sensitive pricing with profit commission/sliding scale
Self-Insured Retention (SIR) / Captive: $50m (insured's own captive, e.g. Shell Insurance Ltd, ExxonMobil Insurance)
Base layer: Captive absorbs attritional losses and retains risk for routine incidents. Captive premium accumulates into reserve for larger losses.

Key Jurisdiction Profiles

🇺🇸

United States

  • Largest downstream insurance market globally — Gulf Coast refinery complex alone > $500bn aggregate TIV
  • US admitted coverage required for US-domiciled assets (Lloyd's US licensed through Lloyd's Illinois surplus lines)
  • Nat-CAT modelling (AIR, RMS) mandatory for Gulf Coast risks; PML/AAL benchmarking used
  • OSHA PSM compliance certificate required at underwriting; EPA RMP filing cross-referenced
  • Post-Harvey: flood sub-limits (25–50% of PD), 72-hour deductibles, flood elevation certificates now market standard
  • Texas: winterisation programme required post-Uri (Winter Storm 2021)
🇬🇧

United Kingdom

  • Lloyd's is the natural lead market for UK downstream risks; most large UK refineries placed directly in Lloyd's
  • COMAH regulations (SI 2015 No. 483) require Safety Reports for upper tier sites; operators must cooperate with Competent Authority (HSE + Environment Agency)
  • Post-Buncefield: IOP systems and bund integrity assessment mandatory underwriting requirement for fuel storage > 10,000 m³
  • Brexit impact: UK insurers now need local entities for EU risks; Lloyd's Brussels fronts EU risks back to London syndicates
  • Key UK downstream assets: Essar Stanlow (Cheshire), Ineos Grangemouth (Scotland), Valero Pembroke (Wales), Phillips 66 Humber
🇩🇪

Germany / Netherlands

  • Rotterdam (Netherlands) is Europe's largest refining and petrochemical hub — Shell Pernis (world's largest single refinery), ExxonMobil Rotterdam, LyondellBasell Maasvlakte
  • EU SEVESO III / NL BRZO compliance required; Dutch Safety Board (OVV) oversees major industrial incidents
  • Lloyd's Brussels entity (post-Brexit) fronts EU downstream risks back to London syndicates under freedom of services
  • LNG import terminal boom post-Ukraine: Deutsche ReGas (Lubmin), Brunsbüttel FSRU — new class of large EA risks entering European market 2023–2025
  • German downstream insurance market served by Munich Re, Allianz, AXA XL alongside Lloyd's panels
🇸🇦

Middle East (Saudi Arabia / UAE)

  • Saudi Aramco — the world's largest oil company — operates the world's largest downstream complex (Abqaiq, Ras Tanura, Yanbu). Insurance is partially self-insured via Saudi Aramco Insurance Company (SAICO) captive
  • ADNOC (UAE), Kuwait Oil, QatarEnergy operate similar captive + Lloyd's/reinsurance programme structures
  • Post-Abqaiq 2019: geopolitical/terrorism risk loadings applied to Middle East downstream; war exclusion vs terrorism ambiguity actively debated
  • Local content rules: Saudi Arabia requires 30%+ of insurance premium placed with local insurers (Cooperative Insurance Co., etc.) via compulsory cession
  • NFPA-based fire codes applied on most Gulf downstream assets; risk engineering by FM Global and Zurich RS standard on major NOC programmes
🇮🇳

India

  • Reliance Jamnagar: world's largest refinery complex (1.24 million b/d) — one of the world's largest single insured values; placed via London market with Indian insurance fronting
  • IRDA (Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority) requires Indian licensed insurer as front for large industrial risks; reinsurance placed back to London
  • IOC, HPCL, BPCL (state-owned refiners) operate large government-backed captive programmes with GIC Re as reinsurer
  • India's downstream sector expanding: new Ratnagiri refinery (1.2m b/d) proposed — would be world's largest single site if built
  • Post-Jaipur 2009 terminal fire: Indian fire protection standards for petroleum terminals revised; FM Global-equivalent inspections increasingly required
🌎

Latin America

  • Venezuela (PDVSA Amuay, Cardon): state ownership and chronic underinvestment make Venezuelan downstream nearly uninsurable in the international market post-Amuay 2012; US sanctions further restrict coverage
  • Brazil (Petrobras): operates South America's largest refinery network (Replan, Reduc, etc.); strong captive via Petrobras Seguradora; reinsurance placed via IRB Brasil Re + London market
  • Mexico (Pemex): nationalised downstream insured via compulsory cession to Agroasemex + international reinsurance; Pemex loss experience among worst in global market
  • Colombia, Ecuador, Peru: growing Pacific LNG export infrastructure creating new downstream premium
  • Local content requirements vary: Brazil (SUSEP 30%+ local cession), Mexico (Comision Nacional de Seguros)

Captive Insurance Strategies in Downstream Energy

The largest downstream energy companies — Shell, ExxonMobil, BP, Chevron, Saudi Aramco, TotalEnergies — all operate substantial captive insurance subsidiaries as a cornerstone of their risk financing strategy. Captive structures in downstream energy serve three primary functions: retaining attritional losses at lower cost than commercial insurance; accumulating capital to self-fund medium-sized losses; and optimising the placement of reinsurance above a financially meaningful retention.

Case Study: Shell's Global Downstream Insurance Programme Structure

Shell Insurance Ltd (SIL), domiciled in Guernsey, is the captive insurer for the Shell Group. SIL retains the first $100–250m of each downstream loss directly, ceding the balance to a London market programme led by a panel of Lloyd's and company market leaders (TMK, Beazley, Zurich, Munich Re).

SIL's retained premium accumulates over years, building a loss reserve that can fund multiple $50–200m events in a bad year without depleting the commercial insurance programme. SIL's parent Shell Group provides stop-loss protection to SIL, meaning SIL itself doesn't face insolvency risk — it is a risk aggregation vehicle, not a standalone insurer.

The London market programme above SIL's retention covers Shell's refineries, petrochemical plants, LNG terminals, pipelines and power generation assets globally — one of the largest and most complex single placements in the Lloyd's market. The lead underwriter on Shell's programme sets technical terms that are then followed by 30+ participating syndicates and reinsurers globally.

A key design feature of Shell's programme is the use of "difference in conditions" (DIC) / "difference in limits" (DIL) covers — allowing local policies written to local regulatory standards in each jurisdiction to be backed up by the London master policy where local cover falls short, ensuring consistent global coverage.

Alternative Risk Transfer: ILS and Parametric Solutions

The largest downstream energy companies are increasingly exploring insurance-linked securities (ILS) and parametric insurance to complement their traditional reinsurance programmes, particularly for Nat-CAT exposures where commercial reinsurance pricing has increased substantially post-Harvey.

Gulf Coast Parametric: Several major energy companies have purchased parametric wind and flood covers triggered by specific hurricane parameters (e.g. Category 4+ landfall within 50 miles of a facility) rather than actual loss. Parametric solutions pay quickly (within 30 days of trigger) and avoid lengthy adjustment processes, addressing a key BI concern where cash-flow certainty post-hurricane is critical.

Energy Cat Bonds: A small number of very large downstream programmes (primarily major NOCs) have issued catastrophe bonds to transfer Gulf Coast hurricane and earthquake risk to the capital markets. These typically cover the tail of the loss distribution — losses above $500m in aggregate — where reinsurance capacity is thin and expensive.

Captive Cells and Protected Cell Companies (PCCs): Mid-tier energy companies without sufficient scale for a wholly-owned captive use PCC structures (typically Guernsey or Cayman-domiciled) to achieve similar risk retention economics, pooling their retention with peer energy companies via a rent-a-captive arrangement.
