Seven Eras of Transactional Risk Insurance

Pre-1990
The Escrow Era

M&A transactions relied entirely on seller indemnities secured by escrow holdback (typically 10–15% of deal value for 12–24 months). No insurance product existed. Sellers faced long tail liability; buyers had direct recourse only.

Lloyd's market focused on traditional property/casualty; financial lines nascent. PE fund structures increasingly incompatible with long indemnity tails — funds needed to distribute capital to LPs after exits.

1990–1999
First Policies, Niche Product

First W&I policies written in the London/Lloyd's market in the early 1990s, primarily for continental European deals where seller escrow was culturally uncommon. Key: AIG and early Lloyd's syndicates pioneer product.

Premiums extremely high (8–12% of limit); retention large (5%+ of EV); coverage narrow. Use mainly limited to large-cap strategic M&A in UK and Benelux.

Tax liability products begin emerging for specific identified risks (e.g., Luxembourg restructurings, Irish group relief). Product regarded as "nice to have" not structurally important. EST

2000–2007
European PE Adoption

W&I becomes standard in pan-European private equity buyouts as PE dry powder grows and fund structures demand clean exits. BVCA/EVCA best practice guides reference W&I as best practice.

Lloyd's market capacity grows — Canopius, Catlin, Amlin write W&I lines. Premium rates fall to 3–5% of limit; retention narrows to 1–2% EV.

Tax liability products expand: HMRC EBT/EBT schemes, Irish Section 110, Luxembourg SICAR structures generate demand for specific tax risk cover. US market virtually non-existent — US M&A still relies on escrow and survival periods. EST

2008–2012
GFC & First Major Claims

Global financial crisis collapses M&A volumes by ~60%. W&I claims emerge as deals close at inflated 2006–07 valuations unravel. First significant claims paid — financial statement representation breaches predominate.

Insurers tighten underwriting: financial statement reps excluded or sublimited; material adverse change (MAC) reps scrutinised. Post-GFC recovery sees market rebuild with better data on claims.

Sandoz/Novartis-related deal structures generate early tax claims. Lloyd's W&I specialist underwriters develop distinct class underwriting expertise. EST

2013–2018
The NBI Revolution & US Emergence

"No-box indemnity" (NBI) structure emerges as market standard in European PE: seller gives nil or nominal (£1) indemnity cap; buyer's sole recourse is insurer. Transforms deal economics — sellers can return 100% capital to LPs at closing.

US R&W insurance market explodes: deal count grows from ~200 in 2013 to ~3,000+ by 2018. American Insurance Group, Tokio Marine, Liberty, Berkshire Hathaway Specialty all launch dedicated R&W teams.

Retention narrows to 0.5% EV in Europe; US retention compresses to $500k floor. Premium rates fall to historic lows of 2–2.5% of limit by 2018. Lloyd's largest TL market globally by GWP.

Tax insurance for GILTI, BEAT (US Tax Cuts and Jobs Act 2017) creates new TA/TB demand. EST

2019–2022
COVID, SPAC Boom, Hardening

COVID-19 (March 2020) causes largest M&A deal collapse since 2008; ~$300bn of announced deals withdrawn or renegotiated. W&I claims spike: material adverse change reps, revenue/EBITDA representation breaches, supply chain warranty claims.

Insurers add COVID/pandemic exclusions to new policies (2020–21). SPAC boom 2020–21: ~$160bn raised; de-SPAC mergers create new R&W/W&I demand, particularly for forward-looking projection reps (unusual).

Market rate hardening 2021–22: premium rates rise 10–20%; retention floors increase; cyber representation exclusions added. ESG/greenwashing representations emerge as new exposure. EST

2023–Present
Claims Reality, AI Reps, Pillar Two

Claims frequency reaches all-time high as 2020–21 vintage deals season into claims window. Financial statement reps remain #1 claim type (~40% of all claims). Cyber/data privacy rep claims rising sharply.

AI/IP representation risk emerges — target companies' use of generative AI creates novel warranty exposure. OECD Pillar Two global minimum tax (15%) creates demand for new TA/TB tax insurance covering deferred tax positions in multinational groups.

PFAS contingent liability cover (CE) in demand for US/EU chemical, automotive, and textile M&A. Market stable to slightly softening in 2024 as capacity increases but claims experience matures. EST
