1968Escott v. BarChris Construction CorpLandmark Case

New York federal court rules that multiple directors and officers are personally liable under Section 11 of the Securities Act for material omissions in a 1961 debenture prospectus. BarChris had gone bankrupt; directors faced personal judgements. The ruling established "due diligence" as the primary defence for directors in securities offerings and triggered a 10-fold increase in D&O insurance demand across corporate America.

1970Penn Central Railroad BankruptcyMajor Loss

Penn Central Transportation Company — the largest US railroad — filed for bankruptcy protection. Shareholders and bondholders alleged directors had concealed the company's deteriorating financial position. The case was the first major test of D&O coverage in a blue-chip corporate bankruptcy and established that large-company directors were not insulated from personal liability. Settlement payments exceeded $50m across multiple D&O carriers.

Settlement: $50m+
1984–1986First D&O Hard Market / S&L CrisisMarket Crisis

The savings and loan crisis — caused by deregulation, interest rate volatility, and widespread fraud by S&L management — resulted in over 1,000 thrift failures. The FDIC and Resolution Trust Corporation pursued directors of failed institutions aggressively. Combined with exploding securities class action volumes, the D&O market faced its first capacity crisis: premiums rose 300–500% on large US accounts in 1985–1986. Several Lloyd's syndicates withdrew from D&O temporarily.

1995Private Securities Litigation Reform Act (PSLRA)Legislative Reform

PSLRA raised pleading standards for securities class actions, imposed a discovery stay during motions to dismiss, created a lead plaintiff process favouring institutional investors, and capped plaintiff attorney fees. Though designed to reduce frivolous suits, PSLRA paradoxically professionalised the plaintiff bar: those who survived the reform brought better-prepared, larger cases. Average D&O settlements grew through the late 1990s despite lower filing volumes.

1998Cendant Corporation FraudLargest Settlement (at time)

Cendant (formed from the merger of HFS and CUC International) disclosed that CUC had been inflating revenues by over $500m for a decade. The accounting fraud was the largest in US history at the time, wiping $14bn from Cendant's market cap in a single day. The D&O securities class action settled for $3.18bn — a record that stood for years. Cendant's auditors (Ernst & Young) separately paid $335m. The case drove demand for higher policy limits across large-cap US public companies.

Settlement: $3.18bn (record at time)
2001Enron CollapseDefining Event

Enron Corporation — then the seventh-largest US company — filed for bankruptcy in December 2001 after disclosing the use of off-balance-sheet entities to conceal $1bn+ in debt. Multiple securities class actions were filed against Enron directors and officers; the SEC pursued enforcement against Chairman Kenneth Lay and CEO Jeffrey Skilling. Total D&O insurance recoveries exceeded $200m across the primary and excess layers. Enron triggered an immediate revaluation of large-cap US public company D&O pricing and drove the creation of Side A DIC policies.

D&O insurance payout: $200m+
2002WorldCom / Sarbanes-OxleyFraud + Reform

WorldCom disclosed an $11bn accounting fraud — then the largest in history — in June 2002. CFO Scott Sullivan and CEO Bernie Ebbers were criminally convicted. Congress responded within weeks with the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX), which created Section 906 criminal penalties (up to 20 years) for CFOs and CEOs who certify false financial statements. WorldCom securities class action settled for $6.1bn (including $2.6bn from Citigroup underwriters) — the largest at the time. SOX transformed D&O underwriting criteria globally, with audit committee quality and internal controls now central to risk assessment.

Settlement: $6.1bn (largest at time)
2006Backdating Scandal (150+ Companies)Sector Wave

A wave of stock option backdating investigations affected over 150 US companies in 2006. Executives had been manipulating the grant dates of stock options to increase their compensation — a practice that, when concealed, constituted securities fraud. Companies including Apple (Steve Jobs), Broadcom, Comverse Technology, and Monster Worldwide faced SEC enforcement and D&O claims. The backdating scandal generated significant D&O claims volumes as companies restated financials and executives faced personal charges.

2008Global Financial Crisis — Financial Institutions D&O WaveGFC

Bear Stearns collapse (March 2008), Lehman Brothers bankruptcy (September 2008), Washington Mutual seizure, and the near-failure of Citigroup, AIG, Merrill Lynch and dozens of regional banks generated the largest concentration of D&O claims in financial sector history. The FDIC pursued over 300 failed bank director groups for negligence. Bear Stearns D&O settled for $275m; Lehman Brothers for $90m (limited by available insurance). The GFC permanently expanded the FI D&O (D5) market and drove demand for significantly higher policy limits at systemically important financial institutions.

Bear Stearns: $275m · Lehman: $90m
2010BP Deepwater Horizon — Securities Class ActionCross-Line Event

Beyond the property and liability losses, BP's Deepwater Horizon disaster triggered a major D&O securities class action. Shareholders alleged that BP executives had misrepresented the company's safety culture, well integrity procedures and the scale of the spill response. BP's share price fell 55% in the weeks following the blowout. The D&O securities class action settled for $175m (2016). The case established that operational safety failures could trigger D&O claims via securities market disclosure duties — a template increasingly relevant for ESG and climate risk.

Securities settlement: $175m
2016Volkswagen "Dieselgate" D&O ClaimsNon-US Public D2

VW's emissions fraud — the deliberate installation of defeat devices in diesel cars sold globally — triggered D&O claims in Germany, the US, and Australia. German shareholders brought direct claims against VW supervisory board members for breach of oversight duties. The case illustrated the growth of non-US (D2) D&O litigation as European and Australian shareholder rights regimes matured. German courts began accepting that supervisory board members had personal liability for inadequate risk oversight — a significant departure from prior German corporate law interpretation.

2017#MeToo — Board Oversight LiabilityNew Risk Category

The #MeToo movement exposed systematic sexual harassment and assault by senior executives at major companies: Harvey Weinstein (Weinstein Company), Les Moonves (CBS), Steve Wynn (Wynn Resorts), and dozens of others. D&O claims followed against board members who had allegedly known of misconduct and failed to act. CBS shareholders sued the CBS board over Les Moonves; the derivative settlement included $120m in payments and governance reforms. #MeToo established "tone at the top" and board oversight of culture as D&O underwriting risk factors.

CBS derivative settlement: $120m
2020COVID-19 — 200+ Securities Class Actions in 12 monthsPandemic Wave

COVID-19 triggered over 200 securities class actions in 2020 alone — the highest single-year filing total in the post-PSLRA era. Companies were sued for misrepresenting pandemic preparedness, supply chain resilience, PPE capabilities, vaccine development timelines (Inovio, Moderna competitors), remote working capacity, and the financial impact of lockdowns. The COVID wave hardened D&O pricing: 2020–2021 saw the largest rate increases since 2002, with large US public company primary programmes increasing 50–150% over two years.

2021SPAC Litigation WaveNew Exposure

The SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company) boom of 2020–2021 created a new D&O exposure class. When SPAC targets underperformed post-merger — as most did — shareholders sued SPAC sponsors for misrepresenting target fundamentals, projected financials, and conflicts of interest (SPAC sponsors receive 20% of shares, creating alignment problems). SPAC D&O was initially priced cheaply; by 2021 insurers recognised the exposure and significantly increased premiums. Over 50 SPAC-related securities class actions were filed in 2022 alone.

2022FTX / Sam Bankman-Fried CollapseCrypto D&O

The collapse of FTX — at the time the world's third-largest crypto exchange — in November 2022 is the defining crypto D&O event. SBF, CFO Caroline Ellison, CTO Gary Wang, and other FTX/Alameda Research executives face criminal charges and SEC enforcement for misappropriation of customer funds, securities fraud, and wire fraud. FTX had raised $1.8bn from institutional investors including Sequoia, SoftBank, and Ontario Teachers. D&O claims against FTX's board and professional advisers are ongoing as of 2024.

2023Silicon Valley Bank / Signature Bank FailuresBanking D&O

SVB's failure in March 2023 — the largest US bank failure since Washington Mutual (2008) — triggered D&O class actions alleging that SVB executives misled investors about interest rate risk exposure and the sustainability of its deposit base. SVB CEO Greg Becker sold $3.6m in shares days before the bank's collapse, drawing insider trading scrutiny. Signature Bank's simultaneous failure generated similar claims. The SVB and Signature failures reignited FI D&O (D5) underwriting concerns about bank management disclosures around balance sheet risk.

2024SEC Climate Disclosure Rules & AI Governance Claims (Emerging)Frontier Risk

The SEC's climate disclosure rules (finalised March 2024, partially stayed pending litigation) require public companies to disclose material climate-related risks and Scope 1 and 2 emissions. Directors who approve disclosures containing material misstatements face personal D&O exposure. In parallel, AI governance claims are emerging: boards of AI companies and AI-adopting enterprises face potential claims for inadequate algorithmic risk oversight, AI-driven discrimination, and failure to disclose material AI-related risks in securities filings. The first wave of AI D&O class actions is expected 2025–2027.
