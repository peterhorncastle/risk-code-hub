Module 04 — Underwriting Methodology

D&O Underwriting Methodology

D&O underwriting combines financial analysis, governance assessment, litigation exposure modelling and sector risk evaluation. The premium is determined by the interaction of policy limit, market capitalisation (or revenue), industry sector, financial health indicators, governance quality, and claims history. Understanding these factors is essential for brokers structuring placements and underwriters pricing risk.

Key Rating Factors

📊

Market Capitalisation / Revenue

Primary proxy for D1/D2 premium base. Larger market cap = larger class action exposure (more shareholders, bigger share price drops, higher settlement quantum). Revenue used for D3/D4/D5 where no market cap exists.

High Impact
🏭

Industry Sector

Technology, healthcare/pharma, and financial services attract the highest D&O rates — sectors with the most securities class actions historically. Utilities and real estate attract lower rates. Crypto/digital assets commands heavy loading.

High Impact
🏛️

Jurisdiction of Incorporation

Delaware (US) = highest litigation exposure. US-listed non-US companies (D1) face higher rates than equivalent D2 risks. Australian risks (increasing class action frequency) command a premium vs comparable European risks.

High Impact
📉

Share Price Volatility

High share price volatility is a direct predictor of securities class action frequency — a large stock drop triggers plaintiff lawyers to investigate for potential securities fraud claims. Betas above 1.5 attract rate loading.

High Impact
💹

Financial Health Indicators

Debt-to-equity ratio, liquidity ratios, revenue trends, earnings quality. Companies with deteriorating financials, going-concern warnings, covenant breaches, or restated accounts face significantly higher rates.

High Impact
🤝

M&A / Transaction Activity

Companies announcing major M&A transactions face elevated claim risk: Delaware appraisal proceedings, fiduciary duty claims, buyer class actions if deal price seen as inadequate. Ongoing M&A negotiations disclosed in application command loading.

Medium Impact
📋

Board Composition & Governance

Independent director majority; separation of CEO/Chairman roles; audit committee expertise and independence; board diversity. SOX audit committee requirements. Companies with governance failures (non-independent audit committees, CEO-Chairs) load rates.

Medium Impact
⚖️

Claims History (5 years)

Prior securities class actions, regulatory investigations, derivative suits. A single prior claim requires explanation; recurring claims or ongoing regulatory investigations are major red flags. Nature of prior claims (settled vs won on motion) matters.

Medium Impact
👤

Auditor Quality

Big 4 auditor vs non-Big 4. Auditor change within 12 months (red flag — possible audit opinion disagreement). Audit qualifications or going-concern opinions. Non-Big 4 auditor on a large public company is unusual and warrants enquiry.

Medium Impact
🌱

ESG / Climate Exposure

Degree of ESG disclosure risk; sector-specific (energy, mining, agriculture). Companies making aggressive net-zero or ESG commitments face greenwashing litigation risk. SEC climate rule compliance status for US D1.

Medium Impact
🔄

IPO / Recent Listing

First 3 years post-IPO are highest-risk for D1/D2: projections frequently miss, management credibility untested, investor base unsophisticated. IPO-year D&O commands 25–50% loading vs established listed peers.

Medium Impact
🏦

SMCR / Personal Accountability (D5)

For FI D5 risks: SMCR certification, accountability maps, governance libraries, regulatory relationship quality. FCA/PRA enforcement history of named senior managers is primary D5 pricing driver.

High Impact — D5 Only

D&O Premium Calculation Methodology

D&O premiums are calculated using a rate per million (RPM) methodology — the annual premium expressed as a rate per million of policy limit — adjusted for the risk factors above. RPM varies enormously: a well-governed utility company may pay £500/£1m limit; a US-listed tech startup with high volatility may pay $5,000/$1m limit or more. The RPM is applied to the policy limit to determine the base premium, then adjusted by a composite risk modifier.

D&O Premium Calculation

Base Rate per Million (RPM) = f( Sector, Market Cap Band, Jurisdiction )
Composite Risk Modifier = Governance Score × Financials Score × Claims Score × Volatility Factor
Base Annual Premium = Policy Limit (£m) × RPM × Composite Risk Modifier
Side A DIC (if separate): additional 15–30% of primary programme premium
Example: £50m limit × £750 RPM × 1.10 modifier = £41,250 annual premium

Limit Sizing: How Much D&O Do Companies Buy?

There is no statutory minimum D&O limit — companies choose their own coverage levels. A common market benchmark for US public companies is approximately 10–15% of market capitalisation as total programme limit (across all layers). This is a soft benchmark only; companies in high-litigation sectors often buy more. Delaware courts and institutional investors increasingly scrutinise whether companies maintain adequate D&O limits.

⚠️ The Insured vs Insured Exclusion — A Critical Underwriting Condition

The "insured vs insured" (IvI) exclusion is one of the most litigated provisions in D&O policies. It excludes claims brought by one insured (typically the company or another director) against another insured. Its original purpose was to prevent collusive claims — e.g. a company suing its own directors to recover defence costs by claiming on the D&O policy.

However, in practice the IvI exclusion can cause problems in legitimate scenarios: a receiver or liquidator suing former directors on behalf of the company (are they an "insured" person?); a derivative action brought by shareholders on behalf of the company; competing executive factions within the company. Modern policy forms carve back IvI exclusions for derivative suits, bankruptcy trustee claims, and whistleblower claims. Underwriters review IvI carve-backs carefully, particularly on D5 (financial institutions) where regulatory receivers frequently sue former directors.

The Run-Off (Tail) Decision: DD Coverage and M&A

When a company is acquired, its D&O policy terminates at closing — and with it, coverage for any claims arising from pre-acquisition acts that are not yet reported. The solution is a "run-off" or "tail" policy (DD code), which provides claims-made coverage for the former insured entity and its directors for acts that occurred before the acquisition, for a specified period (typically 3–6 years).

Run-off premiums are calculated as a multiple of the expiring annual premium — typically 150–250% for a 3-year tail and 200–350% for a 6-year tail. The multiple depends on the risk profile: a company being acquired after a contested proxy fight or with regulatory investigations pending will pay a significantly higher multiple than a consensual acquisition of a clean risk.

From the Lloyd's underwriter's perspective, run-off is an attractive class: the risk profile is known (no new acts can occur), the limit is fixed, and the premium is collected upfront. The main concern is the latency of D&O claims — securities class action investigations can take 18–36 months to crystallise into filed claims, meaning an apparently clean company can produce claims well into the run-off period.
