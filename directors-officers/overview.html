Financial Lines — D&O

Directors & Officers Liability

D&O insurance protects the personal assets of company directors and officers against claims alleging wrongful acts in their management role. It is the most litigated class in the Lloyd's financial lines market — driven by US securities class actions, regulatory enforcement, M&A disputes, and an accelerating wave of ESG and AI governance claims.

The Side A / B / C Structure

D&O policies are structured in three funding buckets — Sides A, B and C — each responding to a different layer of corporate liability. Understanding which "side" responds to a given claim is fundamental to D&O placement and claims management. Not all policies include all three Sides: smaller private company forms (D3) often combine A and B only, while Side C is typically reserved for public companies facing securities claims against the entity itself.

A

Side A — Non-Indemnifiable Loss

Responds when the company cannot or will not indemnify its directors/officers. Classic triggers: company insolvency, legal prohibition on indemnification (e.g. criminal fine), or company refusal. Protects personal assets directly. Cannot be eroded by company bankruptcy. Side A DIC policies provide excess personal protection above the primary policy.

B

Side B — Company Reimbursement

Reimburses the company for costs it has already paid to indemnify its directors and officers. The company steps in to pay defence costs and settlements on behalf of its executives, then the insurer reimburses the company. Available only when the company has the legal ability and financial capacity to indemnify — i.e. not in bankruptcy.

C

Side C — Entity Securities Coverage

Covers the company entity itself (not just individuals) for securities claims — typically US securities class actions where both the company and its officers are named defendants. Side C is only available for D1 (US public) and some D2 policies. The entity and its directors share the same policy limit, creating a "burning" concern in large securities class actions.

What D&O Covers: Key Insuring Agreements

Defence Costs

Legal fees, expert witness costs, court costs and expenses to defend a covered claim. Often the largest component — major D&O litigation can cost $20–50m+ to defend, regardless of outcome.

Settlements & Judgements

Court-approved settlements or final judgements in covered civil claims. Excludes criminal fines/penalties (typically not insurable by law). FCPA/bribery penalties typically excluded.

Regulatory Investigation Costs

Costs to respond to formal regulatory investigations by SEC, FCA, ASIC, DOJ, SFO. Formal order of investigation required in most policies. Informal/preliminary enquiries often excluded.

Employment Practice Claims

Some D&O policies (especially private company D3) include basic EPLI cover for wrongful dismissal, discrimination, harassment claims against officers in their capacity as managers.

Derivative Action Defence

Shareholders suing directors on behalf of the company alleging mismanagement, waste or breach of fiduciary duty. Common in Delaware (Caremark duties; Revlon duties in M&A).

Extradition / Bail Bond

Costs associated with defending extradition proceedings and securing bail for directors facing criminal charges in foreign jurisdictions. Increasingly relevant in cross-border financial crime.

Emergency Costs Advance

Advancement of defence costs before formal adjudication of coverage — a critical feature. US courts generally allow advancement; UK/EU advancement is subject to company law restrictions.

Crisis Management / PR

Some broader policies include reputation management and crisis PR costs following a covered event. More common in private company and not-for-profit forms. Rarely included in large public company programmes.

⚠️ The "Burning Limit" Problem — Side C and the Entity

When a US securities class action names both the company (Side C) and its directors (Sides A/B) as defendants, defence costs and settlements for all defendants come from the same policy limit. A $50m policy shared between 10 individual directors and the company entity can be exhausted by the entity's settlement costs before the individuals have funded their own defence.

The solution — a dedicated Side A-only "difference-in-conditions" (DIC) policy for directors' personal protection — has become standard practice for large public company D&O programmes. Side A DIC sits above (or alongside) the primary programme and responds only for non-indemnifiable individual losses, providing ringfenced protection from entity claims burning through shared limits.

The "Wrongful Act" — What D&O Actually Covers

The D&O policy is triggered by a "wrongful act" — typically defined as any actual or alleged breach of duty, neglect, error, misstatement, misleading statement, omission, or breach of trust by an insured person acting in their capacity as a director or officer. The definition is broad by design: it does not require an actual wrongful act, only an allegation of one.

What D&O does not cover: deliberate fraud or dishonesty (once proven — not merely alleged), personal profit from fraudulent conduct, criminal fines, bodily injury or property damage (these go to other policies), and claims between insured persons (the "insured vs insured" exclusion). The fraud exclusion requires a final adjudication — a D&O insurer cannot unilaterally refuse to defend by alleging fraud before a court has found it.

The claims-made basis is critical: coverage depends on the claim being first made and reported during the policy period (or extended reporting period). A director who leaves a company should ensure tail coverage or an extended reporting period is in place — their acts as a director during their tenure are only covered if claims arise and are reported while they (or the company) have active coverage.
