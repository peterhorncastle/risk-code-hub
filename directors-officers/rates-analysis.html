D&O Rates & ROE Analysis

The D&O market has experienced two major hard market cycles since 2000 — post-Enron/WorldCom (2002–2004) and the COVID/securities litigation surge (2019–2022). The subsequent softening from 2022 has been among the most aggressive in modern financial lines history. This module tracks the pricing cycle from 2000 to 2026 and models return-on-equity under three loss scenarios.

⚠️ The Softening Risk: Has D&O Gone Too Far? (2024)

The D&O softening cycle of 2022–2024 has been among the most aggressive in modern financial lines history. US large-cap primary premiums fell 40–60% from 2021 peaks; excess layers fell even further as capacity flooded back from Bermuda and new Lloyd's syndicates entered. By mid-2024, some underwriters were questioning whether the market had overshot adequate pricing.

The concern centres on the latency of D&O claims. Securities class actions take 3–5 years to resolve from filing to settlement. The 2020–2021 COVID litigation wave — written at hard market rates — has largely been settled. But the 2022–2024 book, written at dramatically softer rates, faces an uncertain loss environment: AI governance claims, ongoing crypto/FTX tail, climate disclosure litigation, and SVB-related banking sector claims are all in early development. If any of these produce large-scale litigation waves, the 2022–2024 D&O book — underwritten at post-hard market pricing — may prove materially underpriced.

D&O vs Other Financial Lines: The Claims Latency Problem

Unlike property insurance where most claims are reported within 12 months of an event, D&O claims have an unusually long tail. A securities class action is typically filed 6–18 months after the alleged misstatement becomes public. Legal proceedings then take 3–5 years to reach settlement or dismissal. By the time a D&O claim is fully resolved, the policy that covers it may be 5–8 years old.

This latency creates a structural challenge for D&O underwriters: they must estimate the ultimate loss from a policy year before the majority of claims arising from that year are even filed, let alone settled. Reserve development — the adjustment of reserves as claims develop — can be dramatic in D&O: a book that appears profitable at year 2 can deteriorate significantly by year 5 as class actions develop and settle. Lloyd's requires D&O syndicates to maintain robust IBNR (incurred but not reported) reserves precisely because of this latency risk.
