2. Pricing Cycle Phases

2000–2003 — Hard Market Entry

Post-2000 Flood Events Drive Rate Increases

The autumn 2000 UK floods (estimated £1bn insured loss) VER and subsequent winter storms in 2001–2002 prompted material premium increases. Competition remained but loss ratios had deteriorated after several soft years. The ABI's Statement of Principles (2002) on flood insurance was a direct response to growing insurer reluctance to offer flood cover in high-risk areas.

2003–2007 — Softening & PCW Growth

Price Comparison Websites Compress Margins

The rapid growth of PCWs (Confused.com 2002, Compare the Market / GoCompare 2006) VER intensified new business price competition. Average household insurance premiums fell in real terms as insurers competed on price leadership. Loyalty pricing (charging renewal customers above new business rate) emerged as a structural feature as insurers cross-subsidised acquisition. Frequency claims (escape of water, theft) were managed down through policy wordings and excess increases.

2007–2009 — Summer 2007 Floods Reset

£3bn Event Triggers Structural Repricing

The summer 2007 UK floods — 55,000 homes flooded, approximately £3bn insured loss VER — produced the most significant UK household rate increase of the modern era. Flood Zone 2 and 3 properties saw disproportionate premium increases. Reinsurance costs increased. Several composite insurers re-examined their flood aggregation and began withdrawing new business from high-risk postcodes.

2009–2014 — Benign Loss Period, Soft Market

No Major Cat Events; Continued Margin Pressure

A relatively benign loss period for UK household allowed pricing to soften. PCW distribution continued to expand share. Loyalty pricing gap widened — long-standing customers cross-subsidised competitive new business pricing. The ABI estimated the average renewal premium was £75–£100 above equivalent new business pricing by 2014 for home insurance. EST FCA (successor to FSA, created 2013) began market surveillance of pricing practices.

2014–2016 — Winter Floods 2013–14 & Flood Re Launch

Flood Re Changes Market Structure

Storms St Jude, Xaver, and the Somerset Levels inundation (winter 2013–14) produced approximately £1.1bn in insured losses. VER The launch of Flood Re in April 2016 changed the market structure fundamentally for flood risk: the cost of ceding flood risk to Flood Re (fixed premium by council tax band) became a floor, removing incentive for competitive flood underpricing in Zone 2/3 properties.

2019–2022 — FCA GIPP Intervention

Loyalty Pricing Outlawed: Structural Market Reset

FCA PS21/5 (May 2021), effective 1 January 2022, banned renewal premiums exceeding equivalent new business pricing. VER The structural impact was significant: insurers could no longer subsidise new business acquisition costs with loyalty penalty income. New business prices increased; renewal prices for loyal customers decreased or held flat. Pricing algorithms were restructured across the market. Some insurers withdrew from PCW channels.

2022–2026 — Inflation Shock & Continued Hardening

BCIS Rebuild Cost Inflation Drives Premium Rises

Post-COVID building cost inflation saw the BCIS Rebuilding Cost Index rise approximately 28% between 2021 and 2023. VER Automatically, adequate sum-insured levels required significant increases. Insurers faced a double hit: rising rebuild costs increased claims costs on losses; and under-insured properties (estimated 80% of UK stock EST) meant average clause issues. Storm Eunice (February 2022, ~£400m) and a cluster of North Sea storms in 2023–2024 maintained elevated loss ratios. UK household insurance premiums were rising at double-digit rates annually in 2022–2024.

3. FCA GIPP Reform — Structural Impact Analysis

🏛️ FCA PS21/5 — The Loyalty Pricing Ban (January 2022)

The FCA's General Insurance Pricing Practices (GIPP) market study found that 6 million home and motor insurance customers were paying an aggregate loyalty penalty of over £1.2bn per year. VER (Source: FCA PS21/5, May 2021.) The key findings for household insurance specifically:

  • Home insurance: approximately 3 million customers paying an average £70/year loyalty penalty VER
  • Loyal customers of 5+ years were paying approximately 70% more than new customers at the same insurer for equivalent cover EST
  • The loyalty pricing structure cross-subsidised customer acquisition: PCW new business rates were loss-making, recovered from loyalty pricing on retained book
  • Pricing algorithms incorporated "price optimisation" — customers identified as less price-sensitive (older, rural, less digitally engaged) were charged more VER

Post-January 2022 structural changes:

  • New business rates increased (subsidy removed) — PCW shoppers saw headline prices rise
  • Renewal prices for loyal customers fell or held flat — genuine repricing to risk cost
  • Combined ratios increased in 2022–23 as market adjusted (underwriting cycle reset)
  • Some insurers exited PCW aggregator channels to protect margin
  • Auto-renewal cancellation made simpler — churn rates increased slightly
