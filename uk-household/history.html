Module 01 — History & Origins

History of UK Household Insurance

From the Great Fire of London in 1666 through mutual fire offices, nationalisation debates, the 1990s subsidence crisis, and the landmark Flood Re scheme — the history of protecting British homes.

1666 — The Founding Event

Great Fire of London & the Birth of Fire Insurance

The Great Fire of London (2–5 September 1666) destroyed approximately 13,200 houses across 373 acres of the City, leaving some 70,000 of the 80,000 inhabitants homeless. VER Total insured loss: nil — no insurance existed. The catastrophe directly prompted the creation of the world's first property insurance institution.

In 1667, Nicholas Barbon established the first fire insurance office, offering to insure timber houses for 2.5% of their annual rental value (brick houses at 1.5%) per year. By 1680 he had formed the Fire Office, and in 1684 the Friendly Society emerged as a competing mutual. Early fire policies were supplemented by fire brigades maintained by each insurer — a firemark (metal plaque) on the wall identified insured properties. Inter-company co-operation was limited and fires in uninsured or rivals' properties were simply watched. VER

1710–1840 — The Mutual Era

Sun Fire Office, Phoenix, and the Growth of Mutual Societies

The Sun Fire Office was established in 1710 — one of the earliest offices to survive to modern times, eventually becoming part of Royal Sun Alliance. By the early 18th century, several fire insurance offices were operating in London, with the Hand-in-Hand (1696), Sun (1710), Union (1714), and Westminster (1717) all competing for the growing market. VER

The Phoenix Assurance Company was founded in 1782, soon expanding beyond London to the provinces and overseas — the first British insurer with genuine national reach. By 1840, the UK had over 100 fire insurance offices and mutual societies, largely competing on industrial and commercial risks. Domestic household fire insurance was expensive relative to incomes, and much of the working-class housing stock was effectively uninsured.

The introduction of the income tax (1799, permanent from 1842) produced the first significant fire insurance tax — 1s 6d per £100 insured on fire policies. VER This tax remained until 1869, when Gladstone's government abolished it as part of free-trade reforms. Abolition significantly widened access to household insurance.

1840–1950 — Mass Market Emergence

The Prudential, Building Societies, and the Link to Mortgages

The mid-19th century saw the emergence of the great British composite insurers — General Accident (1885), Commercial Union (1861), Liverpool & London & Globe. Crucially, the growth of building societies from the 1840s onwards — and legislation (notably the Building Societies Act 1874) — created a structural link between mortgage lending and buildings insurance: borrowers were required to insure their mortgaged property. VER This tied-insurance model (bank/building society as agent of insurer) dominated household insurance distribution for over a century.

Household contents insurance (as opposed to purely fire insurance) was a relatively late development. The concept of "all-in" household policies covering contents against theft and accidental damage as well as fire emerged in the 1920s and 1930s, initially as a product for middle-class homeowners. Working-class renters — the majority of the population in early 20th-century Britain — had little or no contents cover.

Post-WWII, the Attlee government's nationalisation programme raised questions about the private insurance industry. However, unlike the banks and utilities, the insurance sector was not nationalised — partly because of Lloyd's unique and complex structure, and partly because the industry's investment in UK gilts and equities made it a useful private accumulator of national savings. EST

1950–1990 — Mass-Market Household Arrives

Post-War Growth, Composite Policies, and the Tied Agency Era

Rising home ownership rates (from ~30% in 1918 to ~67% by 1990) and the expansion of building society mortgages made household insurance a mass market. VER The standard household "package policy" combining buildings and contents became the dominant product. Composite insurers — Commercial Union, General Accident, Royal Insurance, Sun Alliance — distributed largely through tied agents (bank branches, building societies) and independent brokers.

The Financial Services Act 1986 introduced the first modern regulatory framework for investment-linked insurance. General insurance — including household — remained largely outside detailed regulation until the late 1990s. The absence of regulation enabled aggressive pricing cycles and practices (notably "loyalty pricing" — charging long-standing customers more than new customers) that persisted for decades.

The 1987 Great Storm (15–16 October 1987) was the defining catastrophe of modern UK household insurance. VER Winds of up to 122 mph struck southern England overnight; 18 million trees were felled; an estimated 300,000 homes were damaged. ABI total insured loss: approximately £2bn (1987 values), equivalent to approximately £6–7bn in 2024 values. The event exposed the extent of accumulated exposure in the UK household book and prompted the first serious discussions about storm PML (Probable Maximum Loss) modelling.

1989–1995 — The Subsidence Crisis

Drought, Clay, and the Subsidence Epidemic

The 1989–1991 drought — the driest period in southern England since records began in some areas — triggered the most costly subsidence event in UK insurance history. Extended dry periods caused shrinkage in clay-rich soils (particularly across London, Essex, Kent, and East Anglia), which in turn caused significant differential settlement in shallow-founded buildings. Claims volumes exploded from 1990 to 1993. VER

The ABI estimated cumulative subsidence claims costs of approximately £3bn between 1988 and 1995. VER (Source: ABI, British Geological Survey.) Several major insurers reported significant reserving difficulties as claims development proved longer than expected — subsidence claims frequently take 2–4 years to resolve (soil investigation, tree root surveys, structural monitoring, underpinning). The British Geological Survey's (BGS) National Shrink-Swell Susceptibility Dataset was developed partly in response to this event to give insurers better spatial data on clay-soil risk.

The crisis prompted major changes to household underwriting: subsidence excesses increased significantly (from a nominal sum to £1,000 standard), subsidence-only policies emerged for high-risk areas, tree proximity surveys became standard practice, and some areas of London and Essex became effectively uninsurable in the standard market.

1994–2010 — Aggregator Revolution & Price Wars

Price Comparison Websites and the Race to the Bottom

The emergence of price comparison websites (PCWs) transformed UK household insurance from the late 1990s. Confused.com launched in 2002, followed by MoneySuperMarket, Compare the Market (2006), and GoCompare (2006). VER By 2015, the majority of UK household policies were sold through PCWs, fundamentally shifting pricing power to consumers and compressing margins.

PCW distribution incentivised "price optimisation" — insurers identified that consumers rarely switched at renewal, allowing them to increase renewal premiums far above the risk cost while maintaining competitive new business pricing. The ABI estimated that loyal customers were paying on average £75 more per year on home insurance than new customers at the same insurer by 2019. EST This practice became the central target of FCA regulatory intervention.

The summer 2007 UK floods — the most costly UK flood event on record — resulted in 55,000 homes flooded and insured losses of approximately £3bn. VER (Source: ABI.) Hull, Gloucestershire, Tewkesbury, Sheffield, and Yorkshire bore the brunt. The event accelerated debate about the long-term insurability of flood risk and directly triggered the negotiations that eventually led to Flood Re.

2011–2016 — Flood Re Negotiations

Statement of Principles, the Flood Insurance Market and Flood Re

Before Flood Re, the ABI and Government operated a voluntary "Statement of Principles" (2002, revised 2008) under which ABI members committed to continue providing flood insurance to existing customers and not to withdraw from areas regardless of flood risk — in exchange for the Government investing in flood defences. VER

By 2012, the Statement of Principles was approaching breakdown. Insurers had threatened to withdraw from high-risk flood areas; Government investment in flood defences had not kept pace with rising risk. Negotiations between the ABI and Treasury produced the Water Act 2014, which created the legislative framework for Flood Re Ltd. Flood Re launched on 4 April 2016. VER

The 2013–14 winter floods (Storms St Jude, Xaver, and the Somerset Levels inundation) occurred mid-negotiation, producing approximately £1.1bn in insured losses and demonstrating the urgency. VER (Source: ABI, Swiss Re sigma.)

2019–2022 — The FCA Intervention

GIPP Reform: Ending the Loyalty Penalty

The FCA's General Insurance Pricing Practices (GIPP) market study (2019–2020) found that 6 million home and motor insurance policyholders were paying more than £1.2bn per year in loyalty penalties — excess premiums charged to long-standing customers above new business pricing. VER (Source: FCA PS21/5, May 2021.)

FCA Policy Statement PS21/5 (May 2021) introduced new rules effective from 1 January 2022: insurers cannot quote renewal premiums higher than an equivalent new business quote for the same customer. VER The rules also required firms to make it easy to cancel auto-renewal. The impact was significant: insurers restructured pricing algorithms, withdrew from PCWs in some cases, and took margin pressure across the market. New business rates rose as "loyalty tax" subsidy disappeared; some long-standing customers saw premium reductions for the first time.

2022–Present — Climate, Net Zero & Affordability

Emerging Challenges: Inflation, Retrofit, and Flood Re Phase-Out

Post-COVID building cost inflation (BCIS Rebuilding Cost Index rose approximately 28% between 2021 and 2023) VER produced widespread under-insurance — rebuild values set pre-2021 were significantly below actual reconstruction costs, with some estimates suggesting 80% of UK homes were under-insured. Insurers and surveyors responded by flagging average clauses and raising minimum sum-insured requirements at renewal.

The net zero retrofit agenda — government targets for heat pump installation (replacing gas boilers), solar panel installation, EV charging points, and improved insulation — is creating new household underwriting challenges. Heat pump fires (from compressors and refrigerant leaks) are an emerging claims category. Solar panel damage (storm, hail, fire from electrical faults) is a growing sub-peril. EV charging point liability (fire from overnight charging) is insufficiently rated in most standard policies. EST — based on emerging loss experience reports from specialist insurers.

Flood Re's planned transition to a risk-reflective pricing model from 2039 is driving the Property Flood Resilience (PFR) agenda — insurers are incentivising flood-resistant construction (raised floor levels, flood-resistant doors and air brick covers) as a precondition for continued insurability after Flood Re winds down. VER (Source: Flood Re Quinquennial Review 2022.)
