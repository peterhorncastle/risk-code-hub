Timeline

October 1987
The Great Storm — South & East England
Winds of up to 122 mph struck overnight 15–16 October, killing 18 people and felling 18 million trees. Approximately 300,000 homes damaged across Kent, Sussex, Essex, and Greater London. VER ABI estimated insured losses of ~£2bn (1987 values). The defining UK household catastrophe — first event to test the modern market's storm accumulation exposure.
~£2bn insured (1987) / ~£6.5bn in 2024 values VER
January–February 1990
Burns Day Storm & February Storms
25 January 1990: the Burns Day Storm struck the UK and northern Europe, with 47 killed in the UK and gusts of 107 mph recorded. Followed by further major storms in February 1990. Combined insured losses across the UK: approximately £3bn (1990 values), making it the costliest single-year storm sequence in UK history to that point. VER (Source: Swiss Re sigma, Munich Re NatCat.)
~£3bn insured (1990) / ~£7.5bn in 2024 values VER
1989–1995
Subsidence Crisis — Drought & Clay Shrinkage
Extended droughts in 1989–1991 caused severe shrinkage of clay-rich soils across London, Essex, Kent, East Anglia, and the East Midlands — the most subsidence-prone areas of the UK (BGS National Shrink-Swell Dataset). VER Cumulative ABI-estimated insured costs: approximately £3bn (1988–1995). Claims resolution took 2–5 years per property due to structural monitoring requirements, engineering investigation, and underpinning works. Several major composites faced material reserve development.
~£3bn cumulative insured (ABI estimate) VER
1994–1998
Direct Line Revolution & Telephone Distribution
Direct Line Insurance (founded 1985) reached mass-market scale in the mid-1990s, establishing direct telephone distribution and fundamentally disrupting the broker/tied-agent model. By 1998, telephone and direct channels accounted for a significant share of UK household policies. VER This era set the stage for PCW (price comparison website) disruption in the 2000s and drove a sustained compression in acquisition costs and premiums.
October 2000
Autumn 2000 UK Floods
Exceptionally wet October–November 2000 caused widespread flooding across England and Wales — particularly the River Ouse (York), Severn (Shrewsbury, Worcester), and Thames (Oxford). Over 10,000 homes flooded. VER ABI estimated insured losses of approximately £1bn. The event triggered the first formal discussion of a flood insurance "pool" to maintain affordability for high-risk properties.
~£1bn insured (ABI) VER
November 2002
ABI Statement of Principles on Flood Insurance
The ABI and UK Government agreed the Statement of Principles — ABI members committed to continue offering flood insurance to existing customers in areas with "significant flood risk" (greater than 1.3% annual probability), provided the Government continued to invest in flood defence. VER The Statement was a voluntary arrangement that held the market together for a decade before being replaced by Flood Re.
August 2004
Boscastle Flash Flood — Cornwall
A flash flood on 16 August 2004 swept through the village of Boscastle, Cornwall. Three rivers in flash flood simultaneously; the entire village centre was inundated. Over 100 vehicles washed into the sea; the majority of properties in the village flooded or damaged. VER Insured losses approximately £15m — small in market terms but significant for the village and for extreme rainfall/flash flood awareness.
~£15m insured VER
January 2005
Carlisle Floods
River Eden flooding inundated over 2,000 properties in Carlisle following extreme rainfall in the Lake District. Three people killed; significant commercial and residential damage. VER Insured losses approximately £200m. Carlisle's flood defences were subsequently upgraded — the 2005 flood remains the benchmark design event for the city's defence scheme.
~£200m insured VER
June–July 2007
Summer Floods — Hull, Tewkesbury, Sheffield, Yorkshire
The most costly UK flood event on record. Prolonged heavy rainfall in June and July 2007 caused widespread flooding across England. Hull was the worst-affected city — approximately 8,600 homes and 1,300 businesses flooded, with some areas inundated for weeks. Tewkesbury Abbey was an iconic image as the surrounding town flooded. Sheffield, Doncaster, and large areas of West and South Yorkshire also severely affected. 55,000 homes flooded nationally. VER (Source: ABI, Pitt Review 2008.)
~£3bn insured (ABI) VER
2008–2012
PCW Dominance & Loyalty Pricing Begins
By 2012, MoneySuperMarket, Compare the Market, GoCompare, and Confused.com collectively dominated UK household insurance distribution, accounting for over 60% of personal lines sales. EST PCW distribution drove a race to minimise new-business prices, creating structural incentives to cross-subsidise new business with high retention pricing on existing customers — the origin of the "loyalty penalty" that FCA subsequently challenged. Hiscox and specialist insurers largely stayed out of PCW channels, maintaining specialist positioning.
October 2013 — Winter 2013/14
Storm St Jude, Storm Xaver & Somerset Levels Flooding
Storm St Jude (27–28 October 2013) — the most powerful autumn windstorm since 1987, killing 4 people in the UK. Storm Xaver (5–6 December 2013) — significant North Sea coastal flooding. Winter 2013–14 also produced the Somerset Levels flood: approximately 600 homes flooded for up to 10 weeks, becoming a national media event and catalyst for flood defence investment debates. VER Combined winter 2013–14 insured losses: approximately £1.1bn (ABI).
~£1.1bn combined insured (ABI) VER
December 2015
Storms Desmond, Eva & Frank — Cumbria Floods
Three named Atlantic storms struck in quick succession: Storm Desmond (5–6 December) set UK rainfall records in Cumbria (341.4mm in 24 hours at Honister Pass), flooding over 16,000 properties across Cumbria, Lancashire, and Yorkshire. Carlisle was flooded again despite the post-2005 defence upgrades — Desmond exceeded the design event. Storms Eva and Frank followed, maintaining saturated ground conditions. VER (Source: Environment Agency, ABI.)
~£1.3bn insured (Desmond/Eva/Frank combined, ABI) VER
April 2016
Flood Re Launch
Flood Re Ltd commenced operations on 4 April 2016 — a landmark moment for the UK household insurance market. VER (Source: Flood Re, Water Act 2014.) The scheme enabled standard market insurers to cede flood risk on eligible high-risk properties to a central pool at fixed ceded premiums by council tax band (Band A: ~£210/yr; Band H: ~£1,214/yr). Cross-subsidy levy of approximately £180m per year spread across all household insurers. Approximately 350,000 policies now in Flood Re at peak years. EST
February 2020
Storms Ciara, Dennis & Jorge
Three named storms struck in rapid succession in February 2020, the most active February storm sequence in the UK for decades. Storm Ciara (8–9 February) produced widespread wind damage and localised flooding. Storm Dennis (15–16 February) brought extreme rainfall to Wales and northern England — extensive flooding in the Wye and Severn valleys, South Wales valleys, and parts of Yorkshire. VER ABI estimated combined insured losses of approximately £500m.
~£500m insured (ABI) VER
January 2022
FCA GIPP Reform — End of the Loyalty Penalty
FCA Policy Statement PS21/5 rules came into force on 1 January 2022, prohibiting insurers from charging renewal customers more than equivalent new business customers. VER (Source: FCA PS21/5.) Estimated annual benefit to consumers: over £1.2bn in combined home and motor insurance savings. Market impact: major repricing exercise by all UK household insurers; some PCW withdrawal; new business rates increased to offset removal of loyalty pricing subsidy. Hiscox and Lloyd's specialist market less directly affected due to non-PCW distribution.
February 2022
Storm Eunice — 122 mph Gusts, Red Warning
Storm Eunice (18 February 2022) produced the highest recorded wind gust in England (122 mph, The Needles, Isle of Wight) since the 1987 Great Storm. A rare Red weather warning was issued for south-east England and Wales. Widespread structural damage to homes, roofs, and outbuildings across southern England, Wales, and Northern Ireland. VER (Source: Met Office.) UK insured losses approximately £400m (ABI/Swiss Re estimate). EST
~£400m UK insured EST
Summer 2022
Extreme Heat & Subsidence Wave
The UK's hottest ever recorded temperature (40.3°C, Coningsby, Lincolnshire, 19 July 2022) VER was followed by a prolonged dry summer — the driest summer in England since 1976. Extensive clay shrinkage and a surge in subsidence claims across south-east England and East Anglia. ABI estimated a 30–40% uplift in subsidence notifications vs annual average. EST Claims expected to develop over 2022–2025 given typical subsidence resolution timeline. BGS updated shrink-swell risk maps following the event.
October 2023
Storm Babet — Scotland & Eastern England
Storm Babet (18–20 October 2023) brought exceptional rainfall to eastern Scotland and north-east England. The River South Esk flooded the town of Brechin (Angus) — the worst flooding there in living memory, with hundreds of homes inundated. Significant flooding also in Yorkshire and Lincolnshire. VER (Source: SEPA, Environment Agency.) Estimated UK insured losses £350–500m. EST
~£350–500m UK insured EST
November 2023
Storm Ciaran — South Coast & Channel Islands
Storm Ciaran (1–2 November 2023) brought the most extreme winds to southern England in autumn since 1987, with gusts of 100+ mph recorded across the Isle of Wight and south coast. Major structural damage to buildings in Hampshire, West Sussex, and Kent. VER (Source: Met Office.) UK insured losses estimated £300–450m. EST Note: Ciaran caused very large losses across France and Italy — total European insured impact ~€4bn.
~£300–450m UK insured EST
2023–2024
Building Cost Inflation & Under-Insurance Crisis
Post-COVID supply chain disruption and labour cost inflation pushed the BCIS Rebuilding Cost Index up approximately 28% between 2021 and 2023. VER (Source: BCIS.) Surveys suggested 8 in 10 UK homes may be under-insured for buildings — rebuild sums set before 2021 were typically 20–40% below current reconstruction costs. Insurers, loss adjusters (Crawfords, McLarens), and surveyors (RICS) all warned of material under-insurance at scale. The ABI called for better rebuild cost guidance. Some Lloyd's household policies moved to index-linked sums insured to address the problem automatically.
