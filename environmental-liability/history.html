Pre-1970

Pollution Covered Under General Liability — The Pre-Awareness Era

Prior to the environmental movement of the 1970s, pollution and contamination losses were largely covered under standard Comprehensive General Liability (CGL) policies. Industrial discharge into rivers, airborne emissions and chemical disposal were common practices with minimal regulatory oversight. Insurers did not price pollution exposure separately because the liability framework to enforce cleanup simply did not exist. VER

  • Standard CGL provided broad bodily injury and property damage coverage with no pollution carve-out
  • The 1960s saw the beginnings of environmental awareness: Rachel Carson's Silent Spring (1962) galvanised public opinion on chemical pollution VER
  • The 1969 Cuyahoga River fire in Ohio — the river catching fire due to industrial discharge — became a defining symbol of US environmental degradation VER
1970–1979

The Environmental Decade — NEPA, Clean Air, Clean Water & the Pollution Exclusion Debate

The 1970s were transformative for US environmental law, producing the legislative framework that would later drive EIL demand. The first Earth Day was held on 22 April 1970. VER

  • NEPA (1970): National Environmental Policy Act — environmental impact assessments for federal projects VER
  • Clean Air Act 1970: EPA established; air quality standards mandated VER
  • Clean Water Act 1972: Prohibits discharge of pollutants into navigable waters without permit VER
  • RCRA 1976: Resource Conservation and Recovery Act — regulation of hazardous waste from "cradle to grave" VER
  • Love Canal 1976–78: Hooker Chemical dumped 21,000 tons of chemicals in a residential area in Niagara Falls, NY. EPA declared a federal emergency in 1978. VER This event — more than any other — forced Congress to enact Superfund (CERCLA).
  • Insurance industry response: insurers began lobbying to exclude "expected or intended" pollution from CGL policies; some policies added qualified pollution exclusions from 1973–1982 VER
1980–1985

CERCLA — The Superfund Revolution & the Birth of EIL

The enactment of CERCLA on 11 December 1980 fundamentally changed the economics of environmental liability. VER By creating retroactive, strict, joint-and-several liability, CERCLA created a massive demand for insurance coverage that standard GL policies could not provide.

  • CERCLA 1980: Created the Superfund trust fund (initially $1.6bn, later $8.5bn) to fund EPA-led cleanups; PRPs responsible for full reimbursement VER
  • Retroactive liability: Companies liable for contamination that occurred decades before CERCLA — including predecessors, parent companies and even lenders VER
  • Joint and several: Any one PRP can be held for 100% of cleanup costs regardless of their proportionate contribution VER
  • First standalone Environmental Impairment Liability policies developed in the early 1980s, initially in the surplus lines market EST
  • Times Beach, MO (1982): Dioxin-contaminated waste oil sprayed on roads; EPA bought out the entire town of 2,200 residents for ~$36.7m — became an icon of the Superfund era VER
  • Bhopal disaster, India (December 1984): Union Carbide India methyl isocyanate release — approximately 3,787 official deaths (estimates range to 16,000+); $470m settlement in 1989 VER. Raised global awareness of chemical plant liability, though the coverage was in the context of third-party liability rather than EIL.
1986–1995

The Absolute Pollution Exclusion — Creating a Standalone Market

The ISO (Insurance Services Office) introduced the absolute pollution exclusion into the standard CGL policy form in 1986. VER This removed virtually all pollution coverage from general liability, creating a structural demand gap that could only be filled by standalone EIL policies.

  • 1986 ISO CGL form: the absolute pollution exclusion replaced the earlier "qualified" pollution exclusion; US courts have broadly upheld its scope VER
  • Sandoz warehouse fire, Basel (November 1986): chemical runoff into the Rhine killed fish for 400km; triggered major European environmental regulatory response VER
  • SARA 1986 (Superfund Amendments and Reauthorization Act): strengthened CERCLA; added EPCRA (Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act); increased cleanup standards VER
  • Exxon Valdez (March 1989): 11 million gallons of crude oil in Prince William Sound, Alaska VER; Exxon ultimately paid ~$3.4bn in cleanup and ~$507m punitive damages (2008 Supreme Court) VER
  • Oil Pollution Act 1990 enacted directly in response to Exxon Valdez VER
  • EIL market grows through the early 1990s: AIG and XL Environmental (later XL Catlin) emerge as market leaders EST
  • Woburn, MA W.R. Grace case: trichloroethylene contamination linked to childhood leukaemia cluster; $8m settlement 1986; featured in A Civil Action (1998) VER
1996–2009

Market Maturation, Brownfield Development & EU Legislation

The late 1990s saw EIL become an established specialty class. Brownfield redevelopment — the conversion of contaminated industrial land to new uses — drove demand for transaction-based environmental insurance products. The EU developed its own regulatory framework.

  • PG&E Hinkley, CA (1996 settlement): Chromium-6 groundwater contamination; $333m settlement — the largest of its kind at the time, made famous by Erin Brockovich VER
  • Brownfield incentives in US: EPA Brownfields Program established 1995 — tax incentives and liability releases for voluntary cleanup encouraged redevelopment; EIL policies became near-standard for brownfield transactions EST
  • ASTM Phase I ESA standard developed: E1527-97 (1997), providing the due diligence baseline for property transactions VER
  • GE Hudson River PCB case: EPA listed Hudson River as Superfund site 1984; GE forced to begin dredging 2009; NRD settlement $19m (2019) VER
  • EU Environmental Liability Directive 2004/35/CE: Adopted 21 April 2004; required transposition by EU member states by 30 April 2007 VER
  • UK Environmental Damage Regulations 2009 implemented ELD into UK law VER
  • EIL market grows in Europe post-ELD; Lloyd's market begins developing European environmental programmes EST
2010–2020

Deepwater Horizon — The Largest Environmental Loss in History

The Deepwater Horizon blowout and oil spill of 2010 was the largest accidental marine oil spill in history. VER While primarily an energy insurance loss, BP's total costs — including cleanup, legal settlements, NRD and fines — exceeded $65bn, making it a defining event for environmental liability. EST

  • Macondo well blowout: 20 April 2010; Deepwater Horizon rig explosion killed 11 workers VER
  • 4.9 million barrels of oil released over 87 days before well capped 15 July 2010 VER
  • BP Clean Water Act penalties: $5.5bn (2015) VER; NRD: $8.8bn (2016) VER; total costs ~$65bn+ EST
  • Flint, Michigan water crisis (2014–16): lead contamination through corroded pipes; state of Michigan reached $600m settlement with residents (2021) VER
  • Volkswagen emissions scandal (2015): NOx defeat device scandal — $33bn+ total costs; created new legal theory of "environmental fraud liability" VER
  • Aliso Canyon gas leak (2015–16): largest methane release in US history — 109,000 tonnes CH₄ VER; SoCal Gas total costs ~$1.6bn+ EST
  • PFAS litigation begins emerging as systemic class: Minnesota v. 3M (2010); Camp Lejeune contamination awareness growing EST
2020–Present

PFAS — The Systemic Liability Crisis & Climate Change Intersection

PFAS contamination has become the defining environmental liability story of the 2020s. The scale of potential liability — estimated at hundreds of billions of dollars — is reshaping the EIL market's approach to underwriting, policy wording and exclusions.

  • East Palestine, OH train derailment (3 February 2023): Norfolk Southern derailment; vinyl chloride burn-off; widespread contamination; $600m+ settlement (2024) VER
  • 3M PFAS settlement: ~$10.3bn to resolve drinking water claims from US public water suppliers (June 2023) VER
  • DuPont/Chemours/Corteva: ~$1.185bn PFAS settlement (June 2023) VER
  • EPA PFAS MCL: Maximum Contaminant Levels for PFOA/PFOS set at 4 parts per trillion (April 2024) VER — triggers billions in water treatment liability
  • Camp Lejeune Justice Act (2022): US veterans and family members allowed to sue for PFAS/TCE contamination at Marine Corps Base Camp Lejeune (1953–1987) VER; estimated 900,000+ claims EST
  • Climate change and environmental liability: wildfire smoke air quality claims, flooding-exacerbated contamination, transition risks creating new environmental exposures EST
  • EIL underwriters increasingly adding explicit PFAS exclusions; London market debate ongoing on scope EST
