Multinational Programme Architecture

Large multinationals typically structure EPLI as a master policy (D6 for the US parent) with local D7 admitted or non-admitted extensions for international subsidiaries. The architecture reflects the severity asymmetry between the US (high severity, outside-limits defence, punitive damages) and the rest of the world (lower severity, within-limits defence, no punitive damages).

Chapter 11 / Insolvency Mass redundancy claims; rejection of employment contracts; ERISA plan termination claims (overlap with DD) Run-off cover purchased pre-filing if financially viable; EPLI claims become unsecured creditor claims; bankruptcy trustee may pursue EPLI coverage on behalf of employee class

Cross-Border Case Study — Global Technology Manufacturer

TechScale Global Inc — Multinational EPLI Programme Restructure

Company ProfileUS-headquartered technology hardware manufacturer; 6,200 employees globally: 3,800 US (60% California), 850 UK, 600 Germany, 500 India, 250 Singapore, 200 other
Trigger Event15% global RIF following acquisition; simultaneous EEOC class action filed (age discrimination in RIF selection) and UK Employment Tribunal cluster claim (6 employees, unfair dismissal)
Pre-RIF StructureD6 master policy: $3m limit / $100k SIR. No D7 coverage. No RIF protocol. No adverse impact analysis performed before layoffs announced.
D6 Loss (US)EEOC charge escalates to federal class action (ADEA + Title VII). 47 claimants; statistical evidence showed 40+ cohort was 2.3× more likely to be selected for RIF. Settlement: $8.4m including consent decree requiring statistical RIF analysis for 3 years. Policy limits eroded by $3m; $5.4m uninsured.
D7 Loss (UK)6 Employment Tribunal unfair dismissal claims; employer had no D7 coverage. Total ET awards: £210,000. Paid uninsured. ACAS early conciliation was not used — wasted opportunity to resolve before ET filing.
Germany (D7 gap)Works council consultation not properly conducted; Federal Labour Court ordered reinstatement of 8 employees. Reinstatement costs and back pay: €340,000. No D7 coverage — entirely uninsured.
Post-Event RestructureNew global programme: D6 $10m primary + $5m excess (US); D7 £3m UK admitted; D7 EU non-admitted extension (Germany, France, Netherlands); D7 APAC extension (Singapore, India). Total premium increase: +185% vs prior year. RIF protocol mandated by insurer as condition of renewal.
Key LessonD7 coverage is not optional for multinationals — UK/EU employment law claims are real and uninsured losses accumulate quickly. Works council consultation obligations in Germany are a structural risk that requires D7 local admitted coverage, not just a master non-admitted policy.

⚠ UK Employment Rights Bill 2024 — Major D7 Exposure Change

The UK Employment Rights Bill (introduced 2024, expected to take effect 2026) proposes the most significant expansion of UK employment rights in a generation. Key changes with direct D7 EPLI implications:

  • Day-one unfair dismissal rights: Removes the current two-year qualifying period. All employees will have unfair dismissal protection from day one of employment. This dramatically increases the volume of potential ET claims — currently most short-tenure dismissals are non-litigable.
  • Flexible working as a day-one right: Employers must consider flexible working requests from day one; refusal grounds are narrower. Retaliation for requesting flexible work becomes an immediate EPLI risk.
  • Enhanced whistleblowing protections: New categories of protected disclosures; worker (not just employee) protection extended.
  • Zero-hours contract restrictions: Requirements to offer guaranteed hours to zero-hours workers meeting minimum usage thresholds — creates new claims exposure for non-compliance.

D7 underwriters should begin building in a 25–40% exposure increase for UK risks when the Bill takes effect. Existing D7 policies should be reviewed for limit adequacy against post-Bill claim volumes.

Coordinating D6 and D7: The "One Programme" Approach

The most efficient multinational EPLI structure is a single coordinated programme with the D6 US master policy acting as the lead insurer and D7 local policies aligned to the same terms, conditions, and retroactive date. Key coordination requirements:

Consistent retroactive date: All local D7 policies must share the same retroactive date as the D6 master. Gaps in retro date continuity leave pre-date acts uninsured — a particular risk on acquisition where the acquired entity has pre-existing EP acts.

DIC / DIL provisions: The master D6 policy should include a Difference-in-Conditions (DIC) or Difference-in-Limits (DIL) clause that fills gaps between local D7 admitted policy terms and master terms. This is critical in Australia, where the positive duty obligation may trigger claims not covered under a standard D7 form.

Reporting and claims management: A single global claims manager (typically the D6 primary insurer) should coordinate EPLI claims across all jurisdictions. Cross-border claims — where an employee based in one country files against a manager in another — require rapid jurisdictional analysis of which policy responds and which tribunal has jurisdiction.
