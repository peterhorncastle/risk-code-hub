Employment Practices Liability (EPLI)

Standalone employment practices liability insurance covering wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment, retaliation and failure to promote claims against employers by employees, former employees and applicants. D6 covers US risks; D7 covers non-US risks worldwide.

Coverage Structure

Core Cover

Wrongful Termination / Dismissal

Unlawful discharge, constructive dismissal, breach of implied contract of employment. Largest single source of EPLI claims by volume.

Core Cover

Discrimination

Race, sex, religion, national origin, age (ADEA 40+), disability (ADA), pregnancy, sexual orientation, gender identity. Both direct and disparate impact theories.

Core Cover

Sexual Harassment / Hostile Environment

Quid pro quo and hostile work environment harassment. Faragher/Ellerth affirmative defence for employers with robust anti-harassment programmes.

Core Cover

Retaliation

Adverse action against employees who report discrimination, file EEOC charges, take protected leave (FMLA), or engage in protected activity. Fastest-growing claim category.

Optional Extension

Third Party EPL

Claims by customers, clients or vendors alleging discrimination or harassment by insured's employees. Common in retail, hospitality and financial services.

Sub-Limited / Excluded

Wage & Hour Defence Costs

Defence costs for FLSA / California Labor Code wage and hour class actions. Usually sub-limited ($100k–$500k defence only) or excluded. Never covers back-pay awards.

US Legislative Framework (D6)

1963

Equal Pay Act

Prohibits wage differentials based on sex for equal work in the same establishment.

1964

Title VII Civil Rights Act

Prohibits employment discrimination based on race, colour, religion, sex, national origin. Created the EEOC. Foundation of all US EPL claims.

1967

ADEA

Age Discrimination in Employment Act — protects employees aged 40 and over. Surge in claims during mass layoffs and restructurings.

1978

Pregnancy Discrimination Act

Amends Title VII to prohibit discrimination on grounds of pregnancy, childbirth or related medical conditions.

1990

ADA

Americans with Disabilities Act — prohibits discrimination against qualified individuals with disabilities; duty to provide reasonable accommodation.

1991

Civil Rights Act 1991

Introduced compensatory and punitive damages; right to jury trial for intentional discrimination. Transformed EPLI from low-stakes to high-severity.

1993

FMLA

Family and Medical Leave Act — 12 weeks unpaid protected leave. Retaliation for taking FMLA leave is a major claim driver post-COVID.

2009

Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act

Resets 180-day EEOC filing window with each discriminatory paycheck — dramatically extended window for equal pay claims.

⚠ Claims-Made Policy: Retroactive Date & Reporting Obligations

EPLI is written on a claims-made basis. Three critical structural points:

  • Retroactive date: EEOC charges filed before the retro date are excluded even if the insured learned of the charge during the policy period.
  • EEOC charge = a "claim": Most policies define a claim to include an EEOC charge, state agency charge, or written demand. Failure to report promptly can void coverage.
  • Run-off on RIF / M&A: Workforce reductions and M&A events dramatically increase EPLI exposure. Run-off (tail) cover for 2–3 years is advisable post-RIF.

Why EPLI Exists as a Standalone Class

Prior to the Civil Rights Act 1991, most employment claims were low-severity: the EEOC could only seek reinstatement and back pay, with no jury trials and no punitive damages. Post-1991, compensatory and punitive damages became available, and jury awards escalated sharply. GL policies began explicitly excluding employment-related claims, creating a coverage gap that the EPLI market emerged to fill from the mid-1990s onwards.

Today, over 95% of US businesses with more than 15 employees (the Title VII threshold) face potential EPLI exposure. The #MeToo movement (2017–2019) caused the sharpest single-period rate increase in the class's history — some insurers saw 30–50% rate increases in financial services and entertainment. COVID-19 (2020) added retaliation and FMLA dimensions as mass furloughs triggered a new wave of wrongful termination claims.
