Rating Factors

Impact: High

Employee Count & Headcount Growth

Primary exposure driver. More employees = more potential claimants. Rapid headcount growth (acquisitions, expansions) increases risk of inconsistent HR practices.

Impact: High

US State Mix — California Exposure

California's FEHA offers broader protections than federal law; CFRA extends FMLA rights; PAGA enables representative actions by any employee. California employees may add 30–60% to premium versus national average.

Impact: High

Claims History (5 Years)

Prior EEOC charges, employment tribunal claims, internal grievances, and settlement history. Frequency matters more than severity — repeat claim patterns indicate systemic culture issues.

Impact: High

Industry / Sector

Financial services, technology, media/entertainment, and hospitality attract higher rates. Healthcare, manufacturing, and retail are mid-range. Professional services and education are lower risk.

Impact: High

HR Infrastructure Quality

Dedicated HR function, written employment policies, employee handbook, documented onboarding/offboarding procedures. Absence of basic HR infrastructure is a significant red flag.

Impact: High

Anti-Harassment Training Programme

Frequency, format (online vs in-person), manager-specific training, and documentation. Post-Faragher/Ellerth, absence of training removes the employer's affirmative defence. California mandates biennial training for employers of 5+.

Impact: Medium

Management Diversity

Proportion of women and minorities in management and on the board. Homogeneous senior teams are a glass-ceiling risk indicator. Pay equity analysis results requested at £/$100m+ limit.

Impact: Medium

Termination Procedures

Documented termination process, progressive discipline, performance improvement plans (PIPs), exit interview consistency. Undocumented terminations are the single biggest source of wrongful dismissal exposure.

Impact: Medium

Workforce Reduction History

Prior RIFs (reductions in force) — whether statistical analysis of selection criteria was performed to test for ADEA / disparate impact exposure. Post-RIF EEOC charge rates are significantly elevated for 12–24 months.

Impact: Medium

Arbitration Agreements

Mandatory arbitration clauses for employment claims reduce class action risk and lower severity. Post-#MeToo, some states (California, New York) restrict arbitration for harassment claims — check current enforceability.

Impact: Medium

Wage & Hour Exposure

California and New York employers with hourly or non-exempt workers face elevated FLSA/state wage & hour class action risk. Meal break, overtime and misclassification exposure drives sub-limit or exclusion decisions.

Impact: Lower

AI Hiring Tools

Emerging factor: use of automated resume screening or AI performance management tools without bias auditing is a growing EEOC risk. EEOC 2024 guidance makes employers liable for algorithmic disparate impact.

Premium Formula & Methodology

D6 US EPLI — Indicative Base Rate Formula

Base Premium = (Employee Count × Rate Per Head) × Limit Factor × State Loading × Sector Multiplier × Claims Modifier

Rate Per Head: Typically $80–$400 per employee per year depending on sector and state mix. Financial services and media at upper end; professional services at lower end.

Limit Factor: Indexed to 1.00 at $1m limit. Additional limit factors: $2m = 1.70×; $5m = 2.90×; $10m = 4.50×; $25m = 8.00×. Excess layers priced separately.

State Loading: California +30–60%; New York +20–35%; Illinois/New Jersey +10–20%; all other states base.

Claims Modifier: No claims in 5 years = 0.85×; 1 claim = 1.20×; 2 claims = 1.60×; 3+ claims = 1.90–2.50× or decline.

⚠ California — The Most Complex US State for EPLI

California's employment law framework materially exceeds federal protections in every dimension relevant to EPLI:

  • FEHA: California Fair Employment and Housing Act — broader protected classes, lower employer threshold (5 employees vs Title VII's 15), unlimited compensatory damages
  • CFRA / FMLA: California Family Rights Act extends leave to same-sex partners, in-laws and grandparents not covered by federal FMLA
  • PAGA: Private Attorneys General Act — any employee can bring a representative action on behalf of all other aggrieved employees; PAGA penalties are not insurable
  • Arbitration restrictions: AB 51 (partially enjoined) and SB 1480 restrict arbitration for FEHA claims — class action exposure is elevated
  • Pay transparency: SB 1162 (2023) requires pay scale disclosure in job postings; pay data reporting to DFEH annually
  • Biennial harassment training: SB 1343 mandates harassment training for all employees of 5+ every two years; supervisors require 2 hours, non-supervisors 1 hour
