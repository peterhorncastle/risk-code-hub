Core HR Controls — Rate Credit Indicators

Rate Credit: High (up to −15%)

Anti-Harassment Policy & Training

Written policy + biennial training (annual for managers). Documented completion records. Training must be interactive, not passive. California SB 1343 mandates for 5+ employees.

Rate Credit: High (up to −12%)

Documented Termination Process

Progressive discipline policy, performance improvement plans (PIPs), documented performance reviews, signed acknowledgements. Undocumented terminations are the single biggest wrongful dismissal risk driver.

Rate Credit: High (up to −12%)

Complaint & Investigation Procedure

Multi-channel reporting (HR, hotline, ombudsman). Written investigation procedure. External investigator for senior-level complaints. Faragher/Ellerth requires prompt, corrective action to maintain affirmative defence.

Rate Credit: Medium (up to −8%)

Pay Equity Analysis

Annual or biennial pay equity audit across gender and race/ethnicity. Results reviewed by board or compensation committee. Pay transparency policy (required in CA/NY/CO/IL). Remediates unexplained pay gaps proactively.

Rate Credit: Medium (up to −8%)

Dedicated HR Function

CHRO or senior HR professional with direct board access. HR staff ratio proportionate to headcount. HR generalists embedded in business units for large employers.

Rate Credit: Medium (up to −8%)

RIF / Workforce Reduction Protocol

Statistical adverse impact analysis before any RIF. ADEA "reasonable factors other than age" (RFOA) documentation. Outplacement services. WARN Act compliance review for mass layoffs.

Foundational

Employee Handbook

Current (reviewed annually), signed acknowledgement at hire, covers all protected classes, at-will statement (where applicable), complaint procedures. Outdated handbook is an underwriting concern.

Foundational

Onboarding & Offboarding Consistency

Standardised hiring practices. Background check policy. Exit interview programme. Consistent offboarding reduces constructive dismissal and unequal treatment claims.

Foundational

Workplace Accommodation Process

Documented ADA / FEHA interactive process for disability accommodation requests. FMLA eligibility determinations in writing. Accommodation denials documented with undue hardship analysis.

Faragher / Ellerth Affirmative Defence — Checklist

Employers who can prove both prongs of this Supreme Court affirmative defence can avoid or limit vicarious liability for supervisory sexual harassment where no tangible employment action occurred.

  • 1

    Prong 1A — Written Anti-Harassment Policy

    Policy must be clear, distributed to all employees (including via handbook), state zero-tolerance, define harassment broadly, and identify multiple reporting channels including bypass routes past the immediate supervisor.

  • 2

    Prong 1B — Training Programme

    Interactive training (not video-only) for all employees; enhanced manager training covering investigation obligations. Annual or biennial cadence with documented completion records per employee.

  • 3

    Prong 1C — Prompt Investigation

    All complaints investigated within a defined timeframe (typically 5–10 business days to begin). Written investigation report. Corrective action proportionate to findings. Written notice of outcome to complainant.

  • 4

    Prong 2 — Employee Unreasonably Failed to Use Procedures

    This prong is the employee's burden but the employer must have made the complaint procedure genuinely accessible. Confidentiality protections and non-retaliation commitment must be credible — not just stated in the handbook.

  • 5

    Critical Limitation — Tangible Employment Action

    If the supervisor's harassment resulted in a tangible employment action (termination, demotion, pay cut, transfer), the affirmative defence is unavailable. Employer is strictly liable. This makes supervisor conduct monitoring and prompt response to early harassment signals critically important.

Termination Best Practices — The Underwriter's Checklist

Wrongful termination is the largest single EPLI claim type by volume. The following checklist represents the underwriting minimum standard for documenting terminations defensibly:

StepBest PracticeEPLI Risk if Omitted
1Written performance improvement plan (PIP) prior to termination for performance issuesNo documented basis → "pretext" inference → discrimination or retaliation claim
2Consistent application of discipline policy across similarly situated employeesInconsistency → comparator evidence for discrimination claim
3HR review and sign-off on all terminations above entry levelManager-only decisions → no institutional quality control on protected class exposure
4Legal review for any employee who filed an EEOC charge, protected leave, or made a complaint in past 12 monthsTemporal proximity to protected activity → presumption of retaliation
5ADEA statistical analysis for any RIF involving 10+ employeesAdverse impact on 40+ cohort → class ADEA exposure without RFOA documentation

7Final paycheck and benefits continuation notices per state lawCalifornia: immediate final pay required; failure = PAGA penalties

⚠ Retaliation — The Fastest-Growing EPLI Claim Type

Retaliation claims now represent the largest single category of EEOC charges filed annually — exceeding race, sex, and disability discrimination individually. Protected activities that trigger anti-retaliation protection include:

  • Filing or supporting an EEOC charge or employment tribunal claim
  • Complaining internally about discrimination, harassment or pay inequity
  • Taking protected FMLA / CFRA / ADA accommodation leave
  • Reporting SOX / Dodd-Frank financial misconduct (financial services employers)
  • Raising OSHA / safety complaints (COVID-era retaliation surge)
  • Exercising pay transparency rights (salary inquiry discussions)
  • Participating as a witness in an investigation (even internal HR investigation)

Temporal proximity (adverse action within 90 days of protected activity) creates a rebuttable presumption of retaliation. Underwriters require evidence that managers receive specific training on retaliation risk and that employment decisions involving recently protected employees are routed through HR and legal.

The #MeToo Underwriting Transformation: What Changed After 2017

Prior to 2017, most EPLI policies were renewed based on claims history and headcount with minimal qualitative assessment of workplace culture. After #MeToo, the Lloyd's market and US carriers introduced structural underwriting changes that remain in place today:

Executive Conduct Warranty: Many policies now include a warranty by the insured that it is not aware of any material harassment complaints involving C-suite executives. A material breach of this warranty can void coverage for the specific claim — the Fox News pattern of senior executive misconduct knowledge making this particularly acute.

Clawback Provisions: Following the McDonald's Easterbrook case, some markets offer D&O/EPLI combined policies with explicit clawback coverage for recoupment costs when companies seek to recover compensation from misbehaving executives.

Culture Attestation: Large risks (500+ employees) are now commonly required to complete an annual HR culture attestation covering harassment training completion rates, complaint volumes and resolution times, turnover in management, and board-level oversight of HR practices.
