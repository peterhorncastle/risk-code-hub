Module 01 — History & Origins

History of Employment Practices Liability

From the Civil Rights Act 1964 to #MeToo and COVID-era retaliation claims — six decades of expanding legal exposure that created and continues to reshape a $4.5bn+ US insurance market.

The Long Road to a Market

Unlike most insurance classes, EPLI did not emerge in response to a single catastrophic event. It developed slowly over three decades as US employment law evolved from a system offering modest remedies into one capable of producing nine-figure jury awards. The transformation was driven by statute, case law, and cultural change in roughly equal measure.

1963–1971 — The Legislative Foundation

Congress Creates the Exposure

The Equal Pay Act (1963), Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964), and the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (1967) established the basic framework of US employment discrimination law. However, in their original form these statutes were largely toothless from an insurance perspective: the EEOC could seek only back pay and reinstatement, with no jury trials and no punitive damages. The maximum award in a Title VII case was two years of back pay — a small, predictable exposure that general liability policies could quietly absorb.

The EEOC itself was underfunded and slow: a charge filed in 1970 might not receive right-to-sue letter for several years. Employers treated employment claims as an HR administrative cost rather than an insurance risk. No standalone EPLI market existed because the exposure did not yet warrant one.

1972–1990 — Building Blocks

Case Law Expands Liability; GL Begins to Retreat

A series of Supreme Court decisions in the 1970s and 1980s gradually expanded employer liability. Griggs v. Duke Power Co. (1971) established disparate impact theory — employers could be liable for facially neutral practices with discriminatory effects, without proof of intent. Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson (1986) recognised sexual harassment as a form of sex discrimination under Title VII, with the employer potentially liable for a supervisor's conduct even without actual knowledge.

As employment claims grew in frequency, general liability insurers began adding employment-related exclusions to CGL policies. By the mid-1980s, most CGL forms excluded employment discrimination and sexual harassment claims entirely. This created a coverage gap that would eventually need filling — but the market for a standalone product had not yet appeared because damages remained capped at back pay.

The Americans with Disabilities Act (1990) added a new category of protected status and a duty to provide "reasonable accommodation" — a concept requiring good-faith interactive process between employer and employee, the failure of which became a frequent claim trigger.

1991–1995 — The Market Is Born

Civil Rights Act 1991 Changes Everything

The Civil Rights Act of 1991 was the single most important event in the history of EPLI. Prior to 1991, Title VII remedies were equitable only — back pay and reinstatement. The 1991 Act introduced compensatory and punitive damages and, critically, the right to a jury trial for intentional discrimination claims. Punitive damages were capped ($50k for employers of 15–100 employees, up to $300k for employers of 500+ employees) but jury awards began to exceed these caps through creative legal theories.

Within two years of the 1991 Act, the first standalone EPLI policies appeared in the US market. AIG, Chubb, and several Lloyd's syndicates offered products primarily to Fortune 500 companies. Early policies were expensive, had low limits (typically $1m–$5m), and carried broad exclusions. Pricing was largely intuitive — there was no actuarial data for a class that barely existed.

The Clarence Thomas / Anita Hill hearings (1991) elevated sexual harassment into the national consciousness in a way no court case had managed. EEOC sexual harassment charges nearly doubled between 1991 and 1993. The Mitsubishi Motors sexual harassment class action (1998, $34m settlement) demonstrated that large, systematised harassment programmes could produce catastrophic losses.

1996–2008 — Market Growth and Broadening

GFC Triggers Wrongful Termination Wave

Through the late 1990s and 2000s, EPLI penetration expanded rapidly down-market from Fortune 500 to mid-market and ultimately to SME employers. Key drivers included: wider broker distribution, lower premiums as actuarial data accumulated, growing awareness of exposure, and the increasingly litigious nature of employment disputes.

The Coca-Cola racial discrimination settlement ($192.5m, 2000) and the Morgan Stanley sex discrimination settlement ($54m, 2004) established that class action EPLI losses could be catastrophic. Rating agencies and investors began scrutinising corporate culture and HR practices as governance metrics.

The Global Financial Crisis of 2008–2009 produced the largest single-year surge in US wrongful termination claims in the market's history. Mass layoffs at financial institutions, manufacturers, and retailers generated an immediate spike in EEOC charges — particularly age discrimination claims (ADEA) as older, more expensive employees were disproportionately selected in redundancy exercises. The market hardened modestly but absorbed the losses without the systemic disruption that property catastrophe events can cause.

2009–2016 — Wage & Hour and Stability

A New Peril: California Wage & Hour Class Actions

The post-GFC period saw relatively stable EPLI rates alongside a new and difficult-to-insure exposure: wage and hour class actions under the Fair Labor Standards Act and, particularly, the California Labor Code. Class actions alleging unpaid overtime, missed meal breaks, and misclassification of workers as independent contractors proliferated — driven by a plaintiffs' bar that had developed sophisticated class certification strategies and by California's Private Attorneys General Act (PAGA), which allowed employees to sue on behalf of the state.

Insurers responded by excluding wage and hour awards entirely and sub-limiting defence costs for wage and hour claims. The Lilly Ledbetter Fair Pay Act (2009) — resetting the 180-day filing window with each discriminatory paycheck — extended the tail of equal pay claims significantly, and a growing pay equity movement began demanding workforce pay analysis as an underwriting prerequisite.

The Supreme Court's decision in Obergefell v. Hodges (2015) extended marriage equality nationwide, creating a new protected class dimension as transgender rights litigation entered the employment context. The EEOC issued guidance in 2015 confirming that Title VII's "sex" prohibition included sexual orientation and gender identity — a position ultimately confirmed by the Supreme Court in Bostock v. Clayton County (2020).

2017–2019 — #MeToo

The Sharpest Rate Increase in the Market's History

The #MeToo movement, ignited by the Harvey Weinstein exposés in October 2017, produced the most rapid single-period rate increase the EPLI market had experienced. Within 12 months of the initial reporting, EPLI rates in entertainment, media, financial services, and technology increased 30–50% in some segments. Underwriters imposed new requirements: standalone harassment management policies, mandatory harassment training, executive conduct warranties, and board-level culture attestations.

The Fox News sexual harassment settlements ($90m+ between 2016–2017 for claims against Roger Ailes and Bill O'Reilly) demonstrated that individual senior executive misconduct could produce severe corporate losses. McDonald's CEO Steve Easterbrook's dismissal in 2019 for consensual relationships with employees, followed by his 2020 disgorgement ($105m clawback after further violations emerged), illustrated the intersection of EPLI and D&O governance risks. Pay equity class actions proliferated: Google ($118m, 2022), Sterling Jewelers/Signet ($175m, 2017).

2020–Present — COVID, Retaliation & AI

Pandemic Furloughs, FMLA Retaliation and Algorithmic Bias

COVID-19 generated a new wave of EPLI claims with distinct characteristics. Mass furloughs and redundancies in hospitality, retail, and aviation produced wrongful termination and ADEA age-selection claims. Workers who refused to return to unsafe workplaces, or who required accommodation for COVID-related disabilities or caregiving responsibilities, generated FMLA and ADA retaliation claims. Healthcare workers who raised safety complaints faced retaliation claims under OSHA whistleblower provisions.

Two emerging risk dimensions are reshaping the market as of 2024–2025. First, algorithmic hiring and performance management tools are generating EEOC challenges: the EEOC has issued guidance on AI hiring tool liability and class actions under Title VII are being tested on disparate impact theories applied to algorithmic screening. Second, pay transparency laws in California, New York, Colorado, and Illinois are creating new wage discrimination exposure vectors and generating regulatory compliance costs that some EPLI policies now cover.

Non-US markets (D7) are evolving equally rapidly. The UK Employment Rights Bill 2024 proposes day-one unfair dismissal rights (removing the current two-year qualifying period), significantly expanding D7 exposure in the UK. Australia's "positive duty" obligations under the Sex Discrimination Act — requiring employers to proactively eliminate harassment rather than merely respond to it — are reshaping D7 underwriting for Australian risks.

Key Milestones

1964

Title VII Civil Rights Act

The foundation. Prohibits employment discrimination by sex, race, religion, national origin. Created the EEOC.

1986

Meritor v. Vinson

Supreme Court: sexual harassment is sex discrimination under Title VII. Employers may be liable for supervisory harassment.

1991

Civil Rights Act 1991

Jury trials; compensatory and punitive damages. EPLI as a standalone insurance class is born within 2 years.

1998

Faragher / Ellerth

Supreme Court twin decisions establish employer affirmative defence for harassment — if they had a policy and the employee failed to use it.

2000

Coca-Cola $192.5m

Racial discrimination class action. Largest EPLI settlement to that date. Class action EPLI risk becomes undeniable.

2004

Morgan Stanley $54m

2017

#MeToo

Harvey Weinstein exposé triggers the sharpest EPLI rate increase in the market's history. Harassment underwriting transforms overnight.

2020

Bostock v. Clayton County

Supreme Court: Title VII's "sex" prohibition covers sexual orientation and gender identity. LGBTQ+ discrimination becomes a fully established EPLI peril.

Non-US Development (D7): A Different Legal Architecture

Outside the US, EPLI developed later and within a fundamentally different legal framework. UK employment tribunal awards have historically been modest (average unfair dismissal award ~£12,000) with no punitive damages — making the severity exposure far lower than D6. However, UK exposure is growing: discrimination awards are uncapped, whistleblowing claims carry significant awards, and the proposed Employment Rights Bill 2024 would remove the two-year qualifying period for unfair dismissal protection entirely.

Australian EPL exposure hardened significantly post-#MeToo, with the Sex Discrimination Act's positive duty obligation (effective from 2023) requiring employers to proactively prevent harassment rather than merely respond to it. EU risks benefit from GDPR's whistleblower and data subject rights overlay — creating new retaliation claim vectors for employees who exercise GDPR rights and are subsequently dismissed. D7 limits are generally lower than D6 and defence costs within limits is the market norm outside the US.
