Module 07 — Global Programme Structuring

D&O Global Programme Structuring

A multinational corporation with directors serving on boards in multiple jurisdictions faces layered D&O exposure: US securities class actions (the dominant peril), UK/EU regulatory enforcement, local director disqualification proceedings, and country-specific employment law. Global D&O programmes must be carefully structured to avoid gaps and ensure directors have meaningful personal protection wherever they serve.

Multinational D&O Programme Architecture

A large multinational typically structures its D&O programme as a master policy (placed at Lloyd's or in the London market) with local/admitted policies in jurisdictions that require them. The programme must address three coverage priorities: (1) personal protection of individual directors through adequate Side A limits; (2) company reimbursement coverage for corporate indemnification obligations; and (3) entity-level securities coverage where the company is named as co-defendant.

Side A DIC/XS Tower — Personal Executive Protection $50m xs programme
Third Excess Layer — Side A + B + C $75m xs $150m
Second Excess Layer — Side A + B + C $75m xs $75m
First Excess Layer — Side A + B + C $50m xs $25m
Primary Layer — Side A + B + C (Lloyd's lead) $25m xs nil SIR
Self-Insured Retention (Side B only — Side A always nil SIR) $250k–$2.5m SIR

Key structural points: Side A retentions are almost always nil — a director should never have to pay the first $X before coverage responds to protect personal assets. Side B retentions ($250k–$2.5m) are standard for large companies and reflect the corporate ability to absorb small losses. Total programme limit of $275m (including Side A DIC) is illustrative for a large-cap US-listed multinational; mega-caps (S&P 100) routinely purchase $500m–$1bn+ programmes.

M&A Transactions: Run-Off and Change of Control

D&O at corporate transactions — mergers, acquisitions, IPOs, spin-offs and insolvencies — requires specific programme management. The claims-made policy structure means that outgoing directors face claims for acts committed during their tenure, potentially years after the transaction closes and their policy has lapsed.

⚠️ The Run-Off Gap: Most Common D&O Coverage Failure

The single most common D&O coverage failure at Lloyd's is an acquisition where the seller failed to negotiate adequate run-off coverage. The sequence: company is acquired; policy lapses at acquisition close; two years later, a class action is filed against former directors for acts committed before the acquisition. The acquirer's D&O policy explicitly excludes pre-acquisition acts. The former directors have no coverage.

Standard market practice: the target company's D&O policy should include a "change of control" provision allowing purchase of a 6-year run-off endorsement at a premium pre-negotiated at policy inception (typically 200–300% of the annual premium for a full 6-year tail). This cost should be a negotiating point in M&A deal terms — the seller should ensure the acquirer contractually commits to purchasing run-off or that the premium is held in escrow at close.

Case Study: Rio Tinto — Cross-Jurisdiction D&O Complexity

Rio Tinto illustrates the complexity of a dual-listed mining company facing simultaneous D&O claims in multiple jurisdictions. The company is listed on both the LSE (primary) and ASX (secondary), with significant US ADR holders, operations across 6 continents, and directors serving on boards in multiple jurisdictions simultaneously.

In 2016, Rio Tinto faced SEC and DOJ investigations related to a Mozambique coal asset write-down, with allegations that executives had concealed the impairment from investors. The SEC brought civil charges; the DOJ investigated potential criminal liability. The case triggered D&O claims in the US (securities class action by US ADR holders), the UK (FCA investigation into market disclosure), and Australia (ASIC review of Australian listing rule compliance).

Rio's programme — a layered London market tower of approximately $350m (D3 primary with US ADR endorsement, effectively creating hybrid D1/D3 exposure) — was tested across three simultaneous jurisdictions with different legal systems, different limitation periods, and different standards of proof. The case underlines why large mining, energy and resources companies require bespoke multi-jurisdiction D&O programme design rather than off-the-shelf products, and why experienced Lloyd's financial lines underwriters who understand the interaction between US ADR exposure and non-US listings provide critical market value.
