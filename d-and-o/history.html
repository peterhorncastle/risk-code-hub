Module 01 — History & Origins

D&O Insurance History

From the first Lloyd's D&O policy in 1934 to the $1bn+ programme towers demanded by today's mega-cap corporations, the D&O market has been shaped by corporate scandal, securities legislation, and a uniquely American appetite for executive accountability litigation.

Era 1: Origins & the Securities Acts (1920s–1960s)

The Crash, Congress and the Birth of Corporate Liability

1920s–1960s

The 1929 Wall Street Crash and subsequent Great Depression exposed widespread corporate fraud and market manipulation. Congress responded with the Securities Act 1933 (governing new securities issuances) and the Securities Exchange Act 1934 (governing ongoing disclosure by listed companies). Section 10(b) of the 1934 Act and Rule 10b-5 — prohibiting fraud "in connection with the purchase or sale of any security" — would become the legal foundation for virtually every US securities class action for the next 90 years.

The first D&O insurance policy was written at Lloyd's of London in 1934, the same year the Exchange Act passed. The pioneering underwriter, Cuthbert Heath's team, recognised that the new securities legislation created substantial personal liability for corporate directors. Early D&O policies were bespoke, expensive and narrowly written — primarily purchased by financial institutions and large listed companies.

Through the 1940s–1960s, the market remained small. Corporate indemnification of directors was limited or prohibited in many US states; companies had little incentive to purchase Side B coverage when they could not legally reimburse directors. The explosion of D&O demand awaited both the liberalisation of indemnification laws and the growth of plaintiffs' securities litigation in the 1970s.

Era 2: Growth and the Delaware Revolution (1960s–1985)

Delaware Indemnification, SEC Expansion & Early Class Actions

1960s–1985

Delaware's General Corporation Law, repeatedly liberalised from the 1960s onwards, became the gold standard for US corporate governance — and indemnification. Section 145 of the Delaware GCL broadly permitted corporations to indemnify their directors and officers, and ultimately to require such indemnification in their charters. This created enormous demand for Side B coverage as companies formally committed to stand behind their executives.

The SEC expanded its enforcement agenda dramatically under the chairmanship of William Casey and later John Shad, bringing complex fraud and insider trading cases that named individual officers. The 1970s saw the rise of professional plaintiffs' class action law firms — Milberg Weiss being the most prominent — who developed the securities fraud class action as a systematic business. Stock price drops following adverse news became routine litigation triggers.

The D&O market grew steadily but remained concentrated in Lloyd's and a small number of US specialty insurers. AIG, under Hank Greenberg's leadership, expanded aggressively into management liability in the 1970s and 1980s, becoming the dominant US D&O carrier. Lloyd's syndicates provided capacity for large, complex risks that US admitted insurers could not or would not write.

The Business Judgment Rule — the judicial doctrine that courts would not second-guess business decisions made on an informed, good-faith basis — provided important protection for directors, but its limits were tested by the leveraged buyout wave of the 1980s, which generated significant creditor litigation against boards that approved debt-laden transactions.

Era 3: The Crisis & Savings and Loan Catastrophe (1985–1995)

S&L Collapse, Market Hardening and PSLRA Reform

1985–1995

The US Savings & Loan crisis of the late 1980s produced the largest wave of D&O losses the market had seen to that point. Over 1,000 S&L institutions failed; the Resolution Trust Corporation (RTC) and Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) pursued hundreds of D&O claims against bank directors, alleging negligence, breach of fiduciary duty and fraud. Total D&O losses from the S&L crisis exceeded $1bn — an enormous figure for the 1980s market.

Combined ratios deteriorated sharply. Many Lloyd's syndicates that had written D&O in the early 1980s without fully understanding the US litigation environment suffered catastrophic losses. The Names crisis at Lloyd's — in which individual underwriting members faced unlimited personal liability — was partly attributable to D&O and other US long-tail liability exposures. The period from 1987 to 1992 saw severe D&O market hardening: rates doubled or tripled, capacity contracted, and many underwriters exited the class entirely.

Congress responded to the perceived explosion of abusive securities litigation with the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act 1995 (PSLRA). The PSLRA imposed pleading standards requiring plaintiffs to allege specific facts showing "a strong inference of scienter" before discovery could proceed, and required identification of the lead plaintiff. It was intended to reduce frivolous claims. In practice, it raised the bar for class certification but did not eliminate securities litigation — it merely professionalised it.

Era 4: The Corporate Scandal Era (1998–2005)

Enron, WorldCom, Tyco — and Sarbanes-Oxley

1998–2005

The collapse of the dot-com bubble in 2000–2001 was followed by a wave of corporate accounting scandals of a scale not seen since the 1920s. Enron (bankruptcy December 2001, $63bn in assets) revealed systematic accounting fraud involving off-balance-sheet special purpose vehicles; its D&O insurers paid out over $300m in settlements. WorldCom (bankruptcy July 2002, $107bn — the largest to that point) restated $11bn in earnings; its D&O settlement reached approximately $50m from insurers.

Tyco International, HealthSouth, Global Crossing, Adelphia and Qwest Communications added to the claims wave. The Arthur Andersen accounting firm — implicated in Enron's audit failures — was destroyed by criminal conviction (later overturned, but too late). Audit quality and CEO/CFO personal accountability became the dominant corporate governance issues of the era.

Congress passed the Sarbanes-Oxley Act 2002 (SOX) in record time — 67 days from introduction to signature. SOX mandated CEO and CFO personal certification of financial statements (Section 302, 906), criminal penalties for knowing certification of false statements, enhanced audit committee independence, and creation of the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB). For D&O underwriters, SOX was a double-edged sword: it raised governance standards (reducing frequency) but increased individual director and officer liability (raising severity).

Era 5: The Financial Crisis (2006–2012)

Mortgage Fraud, Bear Stearns, Lehman and Dodd-Frank

2006–2012

The 2007–2009 Global Financial Crisis generated a second wave of catastrophic D&O claims centred on the financial sector. Countrywide Financial, Washington Mutual, IndyMac, Wachovia and dozens of smaller banks were the subject of FDIC D&O claims. The Bear Stearns hedge fund collapse (June 2007) and Bear's own failure (March 2008) triggered securities class actions. Lehman Brothers' bankruptcy — the largest in US history at $639bn in assets — spawned a decade of D&O litigation.

AIG's Financial Products division generated losses that required a $182bn federal bailout; AIG's D&O insurers faced extraordinary exposure. The US government's $85bn initial loan and subsequent conservatorship raised novel questions about state control and shareholder standing to sue. Ultimately, AIG's D&O settlement reached approximately $450m — one of the largest ever at the time.

The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act 2010 expanded the SEC's enforcement powers, created the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), introduced whistleblower financial rewards (10–30% of sanctions over $1m), and imposed new fiduciary-like standards on financial advisers. Dodd-Frank's whistleblower programme became a major source of SEC enforcement referrals, increasing investigation risk for financial institution executives.

Non-US D&O (D3/D4) markets also felt the crisis: UK Financial Services Authority (FSA) fines on HBOS, Royal Bank of Scotland and Barclays boards entered the regulatory record. RBS's £45bn bailout and the subsequent Parliamentary investigation into management failures set the template for post-crisis director accountability in the UK.

Era 6: SPAC Boom, ESG Litigation & COVID (2013–2022)

Cyber D&O, SPAC Explosion, ESG Claims and the Pandemic

2013–2022

The 2010s introduced entirely new categories of D&O risk. Major data breaches at Target (2013), Equifax (2017) and SolarWinds (2020) generated shareholder derivative suits alleging boards failed in their cybersecurity oversight duties — a new theory of D&O liability that blended cyber and financial lines exposure. The SEC's 2018 guidance (and later 2023 cyber disclosure rules) formalised cybersecurity as a material board responsibility.

The SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company) boom of 2020–2021 generated extraordinary D&O losses. SPACs — blank-cheque companies raising capital to merge with a target — were frequently promoted with optimistic revenue projections that proved wildly inaccurate post-merger. Over 100 SPAC-related securities class actions were filed between 2020 and 2023. The typical pattern: SPAC announcement, share price spike, merger completion, catastrophic revenue miss, stock collapse, securities class action. D&O underwriters who had aggressively priced SPAC business suffered severe losses in 2021–2022.

ESG litigation emerged as a sustained threat from 2019 onwards. Claims included: greenwashing shareholder suits (Exxon's climate disclosure, BP's "beyond petroleum" rebranding), social diversity disclosure claims (bank boards lacking gender/ethnic diversity), and governance failures (WeWork IPO collapse, allegations against Adam Neumann). The SEC's expanding ESG disclosure regime and the European Green Deal generated new board duties that will drive D&O claims for years.

COVID-19 generated a distinct wave: PPE fraud claims (executives selling shares before announcing supply chain failures), hospital network governance actions, and lockdown-related business interruption disputes where boards were alleged to have misled investors on pandemic preparedness.

Era 7: Stabilisation, AI Liability and New Frontiers (2022–present)

Market Correction, AI Governance Risk and the FTX Reckoning

2022–present

The 2022–2024 period saw D&O market stabilisation after the SPAC-driven losses. Premium rates softened from 2022 highs; capacity returned; new Lloyd's syndicates entered the financial lines market. However, structural loss drivers persisted: the FTX / Sam Bankman-Fried collapse (November 2022) generated the largest crypto D&O claims wave yet seen, with investors claiming SBF and FTX executives defrauded them of approximately $8bn. The case became a cultural flashpoint for crypto governance and exchange accountability.

The Silicon Valley Bank collapse (March 2023) — the second-largest US bank failure in history — triggered FDIC D&O claims against SVB's board for alleged failure to manage interest rate risk while selling shares in the weeks before collapse. The rapid regional banking crisis of Spring 2023 (Signature Bank, First Republic) raised fundamental D&O questions about board competency in asset-liability management.

Artificial intelligence governance is the emerging frontier. As companies make material representations to investors about AI capabilities and risks, the gap between promotional claims and technical reality creates classic securities fraud exposure. Early AI governance D&O claims were filed in 2023–2024 against technology companies alleged to have overstated AI readiness. The SEC's enhanced focus on AI disclosure accuracy signals that AI-related D&O claims will grow materially through the late 2020s.

Key Milestones in D&O History

1934

First Lloyd's D&O Policy

Written by Cuthbert Heath's syndicate in the same year as the US Securities Exchange Act. Established Lloyd's as the founding market for management liability.

1970

Delaware GCL Liberalised

Delaware's 1969 corporation law revisions broadly permitted director indemnification, creating demand for Side B coverage and establishing Delaware as the corporate law jurisdiction of choice.

1984

Smith v. Van Gorkom

Delaware Supreme Court ruled Trans Union's board breached its duty of care in a merger. Shock ruling; D&O demand surged. Delaware later amended its code to permit liability limitations for duty-of-care (not duty-of-loyalty) breaches.

1995

PSLRA Enacted

Private Securities Litigation Reform Act imposed heightened pleading standards for securities class actions, reducing frivolous suits but professionalising the plaintiffs' bar.

2002

Sarbanes-Oxley Act

Post-Enron/WorldCom legislative response. CEO/CFO personal certification of financials; criminal penalties; PCAOB. Transformed the personal liability landscape for US executives.

2005

Milberg Weiss Indictment

Leading securities class action law firm indicted for paying kickbacks to lead plaintiffs. Firm destroyed; marked the end of one era of plaintiffs' litigation and the rise of institutional investor plaintiffs.

2010

Dodd-Frank / Whistleblowers

Whistleblower financial rewards (10–30% of SEC sanctions) created a powerful enforcement referral pipeline. Shareholder say-on-pay votes added governance accountability.

2021

SPAC Wave Peak

Over 600 SPACs launched in 2021 alone. By 2022–2023 the post-merger securities litigation wave hit D&O underwriters who had aggressively priced SPAC risk.

2022

FTX Collapse

$8bn+ investor losses in FTX crypto exchange collapse. Sam Bankman-Fried convicted of wire fraud and conspiracy. Largest crypto governance failure to date; D&O claims still developing.

2023

SEC Cyber Disclosure Rules

SEC mandated 4-business-day disclosure of material cyber incidents and annual cybersecurity governance disclosures. Created formal cybersecurity D&O exposure for all SEC registrants.

The EPL and Fiduciary Liability Story: Parallel Development

Employment Practices Liability (EPL) insurance developed largely independently of D&O, emerging in the early 1990s in response to the Civil Rights Act 1991, which for the first time made compensatory and punitive damages available in employment discrimination cases. The 1991 Act transformed employment claims from relatively small back-pay disputes into bet-the-company litigation capable of generating $50m+ class action settlements.

The #MeToo movement (2017–2018) fundamentally changed EPL risk modelling: serial harassment claims that had been silently resolved by HR departments became public litigation and board-level reputational crises. For D&O underwriters writing combined management liability policies, the interaction between EPL (DA) and D&O (D1) coverage — where the same event triggers both securities fraud claims and EPL exposure — became a critical accumulation risk.

Fiduciary Liability (DD) under ERISA has been shaped above all by the explosion of excessive fee litigation against 401(k) plan sponsors. Since the Supreme Court's 2015 decision in Tibble v. Edison — holding that fiduciaries have a continuing duty to monitor and remove imprudent investments — plan sponsors have faced a sustained wave of claims alleging they offered high-cost mutual funds when lower-cost alternatives were available. Universities, hospitals and Fortune 500 companies have settled for tens of millions.
