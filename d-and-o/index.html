Directors & Officers Liability

D&O insurance protects corporate directors, officers and the companies that indemnify them against claims alleging wrongful acts in their management capacity. The US securities litigation environment drives global pricing; a single securities class action can exhaust $300m+ in limits. Covers D1–D5 (D&O), DA–DB (Employment Practices) and DD (Fiduciary Liability).

The D&O Coverage Structure: Sides A, B & C

D&O policies are uniquely structured around three coverage "sides" that reflect different indemnification scenarios. The distinction matters enormously: Side A protects individuals' personal assets when the company cannot or will not indemnify; Side B reimburses the company for costs already paid; Side C covers entity-level securities claims. Each side is priced and managed differently by underwriters.

SIDE A

Non-Indemnified Individual Cover

Pays directly to the director or officer when the company is unable (insolvent) or legally prohibited from indemnifying them. Protects personal assets — house, savings, investments. The most critical coverage for individual executives. Side A DIC/XS structures are common for Fortune 500.

SIDE B

Company Reimbursement

Reimburses the company after it has paid defence costs and settlements on behalf of its directors and officers. The largest share of D&O premium is typically allocated here. Company's balance sheet is the primary protection — insurance backstops corporate indemnification obligations.

SIDE C

Entity Securities Coverage

Covers the company itself as co-defendant in securities class actions. Only available for listed companies (D1, D3). Securities claims name both individual officers and the entity; Side C funds the entity's share of any settlement. Absent for private companies (D2, D4).

EPL

Employment Practices Liability (DA/DB)

Covers wrongful termination, discrimination (race, sex, age, disability), sexual harassment, retaliation, failure to promote and constructive dismissal. Often bought alongside D&O as a combined management liability package. US EPL is a separate high-frequency class in its own right.

FIDUCIARY

Fiduciary Liability (DD)

Covers pension trustees and benefit plan administrators for ERISA-based breach of fiduciary duty claims. Key exposures: excessive fee litigation (401k plans), imprudent investment selection, plan administration errors, and failure to monitor third-party service providers.

SIDE A DIC

Side A DIC / Excess

Difference-in-conditions Side A: drops down to pay when the primary D&O policy is exhausted, rescinded, or fails (e.g. rescission for misrepresentation). Dedicated Side A towers protect executives from catastrophic personal exposure in major enforcement actions.

⚠️ Claims-Made Basis: The Retroactive Date & Run-Off Problem

All D&O policies are written on a claims-made basis — coverage is triggered by when the claim is first made, not when the wrongful act occurred. This creates two critical structural issues that every underwriter and broker must understand:

Retroactive date: Claims arising from acts before the retroactive date are excluded. A new insurer taking over from a prior insurer must confirm the retro date is at least coterminous with the prior policy's inception, or prior acts are left uncovered.

Run-off (tail) coverage: When a company is acquired, sold, or enters insolvency, its D&O policy terminates. Claims arising from the pre-acquisition period may be filed years later — after the policy has lapsed. Run-off coverage (typically 6 years) must be purchased at the transaction to protect outgoing directors. This is a major M&A transaction cost.

Why US D&O Drives Global Pricing

The United States accounts for the vast majority of global D&O losses, driven by a unique combination of factors found nowhere else: the Private Securities Litigation Reform Act (PSLRA) class action mechanism, professional plaintiff law firms operating on contingency, jury trials for fraud claims, and a culture of corporate litigation far exceeding any other jurisdiction.

A company listed on a US exchange — whether headquartered in the US, UK, Canada or Australia — is exposed to US securities class actions under Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5 of the Securities Exchange Act 1934. This means non-US companies with NYSE or NASDAQ ADRs carry D1 exposure alongside their domestic D3 exposure. A UK miner listed in London and New York needs a programme that responds to both the FCA's Market Abuse Regulation and the SEC's securities disclosure rules.

For Lloyd's underwriters, understanding the D1/D3 distinction and the extent of US nexus is the starting point for any D&O submission.
