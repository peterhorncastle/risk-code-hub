Module 04 — Underwriting Methodology

D&O Underwriting Methodology

D&O underwriting assesses the probability that directors or officers will face covered claims — and the severity if they do. The key variables are jurisdiction (US-listed = highest), corporate governance quality, financial health, industry sector, and the specific structure of the coverage being purchased. Side A-only towers, public vs private, financial institutions and SPAC risks each require distinct underwriting approaches.

D&O Rating Factors

🌐

US Listing / Exchange Nexus

Companies listed on NYSE, NASDAQ or with significant US investor base face the full weight of US securities class action exposure under Section 10(b)/Rule 10b-5. D1 (US public) risks price at 3–10× comparable D3 (non-US public) risks for the same revenue.

Highest Impact
📊

Market Capitalisation / Revenue

Larger companies face larger securities claims (more shareholders = larger class, larger potential damages). Market-cap-based D&O limit adequacy benchmarks: 1–3% of market cap is a typical minimum for large-cap public companies.

Highest Impact
🏭

Industry Sector

Financial services (D5), healthcare, technology and emerging sectors (crypto, EV) attract highest base rates. Mining, energy and pharmaceutical companies face elevated disclosure liability due to complex reserve/trial data reporting obligations.

High Impact
📋

Corporate Governance Quality

Board composition (% independent directors), audit committee quality, separation of CEO/Chair, existence of a lead independent director, say-on-pay approval rates. ISS and Glass Lewis governance scores are proxies used by underwriters.

High Impact
💰

Financial Health & Leverage

Companies in financial distress face dramatically elevated D&O exposure — creditor claims (D2/D4), SEC accounting investigations, and "storm warnings" securities claims. Loss history, earnings restatements, going-concern qualifications are red flags.

High Impact
📈

Share Price Volatility / Beta

High beta stocks attract plaintiff law firms monitoring for price drops correlated with adverse news. A stock that falls 20%+ in a single day on disappointing earnings or regulatory news is typically reviewed for securities class action potential within 48 hours.

Medium Impact
🔍

Prior Claims & Litigation History

5-year D&O claims history including SEC inquiries, derivative demands, EEOC charges and prior securities class actions. A company with recent class action experience is statistically more likely to face another. Specific prior acts are critical to evaluate.

High Impact
📝

Accounting Quality / Auditor

Big 4 auditor vs. smaller firm; restatement history; material weaknesses in internal controls (SOX 404); complex accounting areas (revenue recognition, goodwill impairment, off-balance-sheet structures). Auditor changes without explanation are a red flag.

Medium Impact
🔄

M&A Activity / Transactions

Mergers generate go-shop litigation (shareholders claim inadequate sale price). LBOs generate creditor claims. SPAC mergers bring forward projections liability. Spin-offs and restructurings create fiduciary duty exposure. Run-off coverage is always required at closing.

Medium Impact
🌿

ESG & Disclosure Profile

Scope of voluntary ESG commitments, climate disclosure accuracy (TCFD/SEC climate rules), DEI programme disclosures. Companies making expansive ESG statements face "greenwashing" securities claims if performance falls short. Rising risk factor 2022–2026.

Medium Impact
🔐

Cyber Governance

Post-SEC 2023 cyber rules: board cyber oversight, CISO governance structure, material incident disclosure history. Companies with prior cyber breaches that were not disclosed in SEC filings face securities fraud exposure. SolarWinds CISO prosecution set a landmark.

Medium Impact
⚖️

Regulatory Investigation History

Active SEC, DOJ, FCA, FINRA investigations dramatically increase D&O likelihood and severity. Known regulatory investigations must be disclosed on D&O applications; non-disclosure is grounds for rescission. Regulatory D&O claims are often larger than securities class actions.

High Impact

Key D&O Policy Exclusions

Insured vs. Insured

Excludes claims by the company against its own directors/officers — prevents collusory insider claims. Critical exception: derivative suits brought by shareholders on behalf of the company are typically covered.

Personal Profit / Illegal Remuneration

Excludes claims arising from gaining a personal profit or advantage to which the insured was not legally entitled. Conduct exclusions: typically apply only after a final court adjudication — defence costs are paid pending determination.

Fraud / Dishonesty

Excludes claims arising from fraudulent, criminal or deliberately wrongful acts. Like personal profit, typically adjudicated — the insurer pays defence costs until conviction or final adverse finding.

Bodily Injury / Property Damage

D&O covers financial loss to claimants, not personal injury. BI/PD claims are excluded — covered under GL/products liability. Cyber BI to the company itself not covered under D&O (covered under CY).

Prior / Pending Litigation

Claims arising from litigation filed before the retroactive date, or matters known at inception. All pending claims and circumstances known at binding must be disclosed on the application.

ERISA (in D1/D2 — covered under DD)

ERISA breach of fiduciary duty claims are typically excluded from the main D&O policy and covered under a separate Fiduciary Liability (DD) policy. Avoid gap at inception by purchasing both simultaneously.

Professional Services (in D1 — covered under Tech PI/CZ)

Claims arising from performance of professional services for third parties excluded from D&O. Technology companies need CZ/Tech E&O alongside D1 to cover both D&O and professional liability exposures.

Pollution / Environmental

Environmental liability excluded from D&O. Note: shareholder securities claims alleging directors concealed environmental liabilities are D&O claims — the underlying environmental clean-up costs are excluded, but the securities fraud tail is covered.

D&O Premium Rating Methodology

D&O is rated on a rate-per-million-dollars-of-limit basis, adjusted for risk factors. Unlike property (rate per £100 TIV), D&O pricing reflects the probability of a claim reaching or exceeding the attachment point. A $25m primary layer is priced very differently from an $25m excess of $100m layer — the primary layer is the most expensive per million of limit.

D&O Annual Premium — Indicative Structure

Base Rate = f( Revenue / Market Cap, Jurisdiction, Sector )
Risk-Adjusted Rate = Base Rate × Governance Modifier × Claims History Factor × Market Cycle Factor
Layer Premium = Limit (£m) × Risk-Adjusted Rate × Layer Factor (primary 1.0×; excess layers 0.3–0.7×)
Example: $25m primary D1 US public co. × $150/m rate = $3,750 per $1m = $93,750 annual

⚠️ SPAC Underwriting: A Distinct Risk Class

SPACs (Special Purpose Acquisition Companies) require separate underwriting treatment from standard D1 IPO risks. A SPAC has two distinct risk phases: Phase 1 — the blank-cheque IPO period, during which investor presentations about a "target" company create forward-looking statement liability; Phase 2 — the de-SPAC merger period, during which the SPAC and target combine, generating merger-related securities litigation.

Post-2022, most Lloyd's D&O underwriters require: (i) formal SPAC questionnaires disclosing the target sector and projected financials; (ii) separate pricing for Phase 1 and Phase 2; (iii) specific run-off coverage for the SPAC shell post-merger; and (iv) underwriter consent for any de-SPAC transaction. SPAC D&O limits are typically lower (50–75% of equivalent IPO limits) and premiums are 25–50% higher per $m to reflect the known litigation pattern.

The Financial Institutions D&O (D5) Distinction

D5 risks — banks, insurers, asset managers, broker-dealers — are fundamentally different from commercial D&O for three reasons. First, they are subject to regulatory capital requirements that create a formal regulatory enforcement apparatus (PRA, FCA, OCC, FDIC, Fed, SEC, FINRA) with far more investigative resources than the SEC's corporate finance division. Second, their financial statements are complex and opaque — derivative valuations, reserve adequacy, capital ratio calculations — creating fertile ground for accounting fraud claims. Third, systemic risk: the failure of a major bank triggers cascading losses across the financial system, attracting Congressional investigations and enormous DOJ/regulatory settlements that dwarf typical corporate D&O claims.

D5 underwriters pay particular attention to: regulatory capital ratios (is the bank adequately capitalised?), credit quality (NPL ratios, loan loss provisions), the complexity and quality of the audit function, the bank's regulatory examination history (CAMELS ratings for US banks), and specific conduct risk areas (LIBOR, FX, PPI, money laundering compliance). The FDIC's aggressive use of D&O claims against failed bank directors — often pursuing directors personally for negligence in credit decisions — distinguishes D5 from all other financial lines classes.
