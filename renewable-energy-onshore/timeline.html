Module 02 — Renewable Energy Onshore (R2 / R4)

Major Events Timeline

Thirteen defining events across 44 years of onshore renewable energy insurance — from the first utility wind farms to the BESS fire era and US hail crisis that reshaped the market.

1981
Technology Milestone

Altamont Pass, California — World's First Utility-Scale Wind Farm

Pacific Gas & Electric commissions the first utility-scale wind farm at Altamont Pass, California. Initially ~600 small turbines (50–100kW); grows to 4,930 turbines by the mid-1980s. The project proved utility wind was commercially viable but suffered significant early reliability problems — gearbox failures, blade fatigue, and bird strike mortality made it a foundational learning experience for the industry and its insurers. US investment tax credits (ITC) drove speculative development with limited engineering rigour.

2000
Policy / Regulation

Germany EEG — Erneuerbare Energien Gesetz (Renewable Energy Sources Act)

Germany's landmark Renewable Energy Sources Act mandates priority grid access and guaranteed feed-in tariffs for wind, solar, and biomass. The EEG transforms Germany into the world's leading onshore wind market within five years — from 6GW in 2000 to 18GW by 2005. The subsidy framework also provides the revenue certainty that makes ALOR (Advanced Loss of Revenue) policies commercially viable, since guaranteed tariffs make revenue predictable. Lloyd's syndicates begin dedicating specialist capacity to the emerging German and European renewable energy class.

2006–2009
Serial / OEM Defect

Vestas V90 Gearbox Serial Failure — Wind Insurance's Defining Loss

A design deficiency in the main gearbox bearing of the Vestas V90 2.0MW turbine causes premature wear and catastrophic gearbox failure across a globally deployed fleet of 3,000+ units. Replacement cost per unit: £200,000–£350,000. Aggregate insured losses exceed £200m. Underwriters are caught without adequate serial defect language or gearbox sub-limits — many policies respond in full. The event reshapes the R2 market permanently: gearbox sub-limits (£5–10m/turbine), clearly defined serial defect clauses with minimum failure thresholds, and OEM warranty interaction clauses become standard.

2009
Market Development

US ARRA — American Recovery and Reinvestment Act Supercharges Solar & Wind

The Obama administration's Recovery Act extends the Production Tax Credit (PTC) for wind and creates an Investment Tax Credit (ITC) cash grant option for solar. US wind installations double from 2009 to 2012. More significantly, ITC parity for solar triggers a rapid build-out of utility-scale solar in California, Arizona, and Nevada. US becomes the primary growth market for Lloyd's renewable energy underwriters. The class scales rapidly — but US hail and wildfire exposure is not yet adequately priced.

2015
Technology Milestone

Solar Grid Parity — Unsubsidised Solar Cheaper Than New Fossil Fuel Generation

Solar PV achieves unsubsidised grid parity in Germany, Italy, Australia, and California. Module costs have fallen from $76/W in 1977 to $0.40/W — a 99.5% cost reduction in 38 years. The inflection point triggers an investment surge that removes the dependency on government subsidies and makes solar the default low-cost electricity source in sunny markets. For insurers, the transition from subsidy-backed to merchant revenue fundamentally changes ALOR risk: instead of a guaranteed tariff, sum insured calculations now depend on volatile power market prices.

2016–2018
Serial / OEM Defect

GE 1.5MW Main Bearing Failures — Largest Installed Onshore Wind Platform

The GE 1.5MW turbine is the most widely deployed onshore wind turbine globally — over 13,000 units installed across 30+ countries. A series of main bearing failures (particularly the non-drive-end main shaft bearing) across aged GE 1.5s becomes one of the most significant ongoing attritional loss drivers in the R2 class. Unlike the Vestas V90 event, GE bearing failures are age-related (affecting 10–15 year old units) rather than a single systemic design defect — but the scale of the installed base means aggregate losses are comparable. Underwriters begin requiring condition monitoring system (CMS) installation and annual inspection records for GE 1.5MW risks.

2019
Solar / Hail Loss

Texas & Oklahoma Hailstorm — US Solar Insurance Crisis Begins

A severe hailstorm tracking through Texas and Oklahoma causes near-total module damage at multiple utility solar farms — individual farm losses range from £20m to £80m. Many policies had been written with flat hail deductibles of £100,000–£250,000, wholly inadequate relative to the scale of loss. Loss ratios for US solar books reach 300–500% across multiple syndicates. The event triggers immediate repricing: hail deductibles move to 2–5% of TIV (or minimum £500/kW), hail sub-limits appear for the first time, and dual hail surveys (pre-construction and post-commissioning) become standard requirements. Capacity withdraws rapidly — available limits for US solar farm locations in hail zones fall by 50–60%.

2019
BESS / Fire Event

APS Surprise, Arizona — First Major BESS Thermal Runaway Insurance Loss

A 2MW/2MWh NMC (nickel-manganese-cobalt) lithium-ion BESS operated by Arizona Public Service explodes during a thermal runaway event, injuring 8 firefighters and destroying the installation. The explosion is caused by gas accumulation from thermal runaway that ignites when emergency responders open the container. The event establishes BESS thermal runaway as a novel, severe insured peril and triggers industry-wide review of BESS fire suppression adequacy. Key learning: inert gas suppression systems suppress fire but do not prevent gas build-up from thermal runaway — explosion risk remains. Vented systems and remote thermal management monitoring become best practice requirements. BESS rates immediately double for NMC chemistry assets.

2021
BESS / Fire Event

Victorian Big Battery, Australia — LFP Fire & LG Energy Solution Recall

Neoen's 300MW/450MWh Victorian Big Battery (Tesla Megapack units using LFP chemistry) suffers a fire during commissioning after an inverter fault triggers thermal runaway in a single Megapack unit. Although damage is contained to one unit (cost ~AUD 10m), the event demonstrates that even LFP (lithium iron phosphate — considered lower thermal runaway risk than NMC) is not immune to fire when combined with a grid fault. Simultaneously, LG Energy Solution announces a major recall of NMC modules used in residential and grid-scale BESS across Australia, US, and Korea after multiple thermal runaway events — a serial recall event with aggregate insurance implications exceeding £300m. Market capacity for BESS falls sharply; underwriters require detailed chemistry and BMS data before quoting.

2022
Policy / Regulation

US Inflation Reduction Act — Largest Renewables Investment in History

The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) commits $369bn to clean energy investment over 10 years, extending and expanding ITC and PTC for solar, wind, and BESS. The IRA is expected to drive 550GW of new US renewable capacity by 2030. For insurers, the IRA represents both the largest expansion of the addressable premium pool in the class's history and a concentration of new construction risk in the US hail corridor, wildfire-prone West, and hurricane-exposed Southeast. R4 (construction) premium volumes surge; R2 (operational) premiums follow with a 2–3 year lag. IRA also provides a 30% ITC for BESS — triggering explosive standalone BESS growth that rapidly outpaces underwriting capacity.

2023
Solar / Hail Loss

Record US Hail Season — £1bn+ Class Losses in a Single Year

A series of severe hailstorms across Texas, Oklahoma, Colorado, Kansas, and Nebraska produce the worst annual hail loss total in the history of utility solar insurance. Individual storms generate losses of £30–120m per farm; aggregate industry losses for the year are estimated at £1.2–1.8bn across all insurers. Even farms with 2–5% TIV hail deductibles face large net losses. The scale of loss forces further rate increases (30–50% in high-risk zones), mandatory hail vulnerability assessments (HVA) for all new US solar risks, and bifurcated limit towers with separate hail sub-limits becoming standard. Some syndicates withdraw entirely from US solar in hail-exposed states.

2025
BESS / Fire Event

Moss Landing, California — Largest BESS Insurance Loss in History

Vistra Corp's 300MW/1.2GWh Elkhorn Battery facility at Moss Landing, California — the world's largest BESS at the time — suffers a catastrophic thermal runaway fire event in January 2025. The fire burns for multiple days, forces evacuation of approximately 1,200 residents, and destroys the facility. Estimated insured loss: $700m+, making it the largest single BESS insurance loss in history. The event prompts immediate market response: 5–10 syndicates exit BESS entirely; remaining capacity adds 50–100% rate increases; mandatory fire separation distances between battery containers become standard; and Lloyd's considers aggregate BESS exposure limits across syndicate portfolios. BESS as a class enters a period of fundamental repricing and capacity restriction comparable to asbestosis in the 1980s.

2025–2026
Market Development

Hard Market Consolidation — Rate Adequacy Returns for First Time Since 2010

By early 2026, the combination of US hail losses (solar) and BESS thermal runaway events has driven rates for both sub-classes to technical adequacy for the first time since the market's competitive trough of 2012–2018. R2 onshore wind rates remain relatively stable (moderate rate increases; underlying loss experience manageable). Solar R2 rates in hail-exposed US zones are up 80–120% from 2018 lows. BESS R2 rates are up 200–400% from 2018 levels for NMC chemistry. The market is now bifurcated: European onshore wind and solar remain competitive and reasonably priced; US solar and BESS are hard market conditions with reduced capacity and mandatory technical requirements.
